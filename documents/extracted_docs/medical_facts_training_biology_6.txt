Enzymes do not just lower activation energy; they stabilize the transition state of a reaction.
Acid-base catalysis involves a molecule other than water acting as a proton donor or acceptor.
Covalent catalysis involves the formation of a transient covalent bond between the enzyme and substrate.
Metal ion catalysis uses tightly bound metals to orient substrates or facilitate redox reactions.
The catalytic triad in serine proteases consists of Aspartate, Histidine, and Serine.
Cooperativity in enzymes like hemoglobin is often described by the MWC (concerted) or KNF (sequential) models.
The Hill coefficient (n) measures the degree of cooperativity; n > 1 indicates positive cooperativity.
Homotropic effectors are substrates that also act as regulatory molecules.
Heterotropic effectors are molecules different from the substrate that regulate enzyme activity.
Zymogens (proenzymes) are inactive precursors that require proteolytic cleavage to become active.
The Gibbs-Donnan effect describes the behavior of charged particles near a semi-permeable membrane.
Living systems are 'islands of low entropy' maintained by a constant flux of energy from the environment.
Metabolic flux analysis (MFA) is used to quantify the rates of turnover of molecules in a pathway.
Metabolic channeling occurs when enzymes of a pathway are physically grouped to increase efficiency.
The 'Enzyme-Substrate-Product' (ESP) complex lifetime can be measured using femtosecond spectroscopy.
Ribozymes, like the ribosome and spliceosome, prove that RNA was likely the first biological catalyst.
The RNA World hypothesis suggests life began with self-replicating RNA molecules.
The 'Iron-Sulfur World' theory proposes life started at hydrothermal vents using mineral catalysts.
Membraneless organelles form through liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS).
Intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) in proteins often drive the formation of biomolecular condensates.
The proteasome uses ATP to unfold proteins before threading them into the catalytic core for degradation.
Ubiquitin chains linked through Lysine-48 target proteins for degradation; Lysine-63 links are for signaling.
SUMOylation is the covalent attachment of Small Ubiquitin-like Modifiers to regulate protein function.
The N-end rule relates the half-life of a protein to its N-terminal amino acid residue.
P-bodies are cytoplasmic granules where mRNA is stored or degraded.
Stress granules form in response to cellular stress to protect translation-ready mRNAs.
Molecular crowding can increase the effective concentration of molecules by orders of magnitude.
The 'excluded volume effect' favors the assembly of large macromolecular complexes.
Chaperonins like GroEL/GroES provide a 'safe cage' for proteins to fold away from the crowded cytosol.
The Twin-Arginine Translocation (TAT) system moves fully folded proteins across bacterial membranes.
Type III secretion systems (T3SS) act like 'molecular syringes' used by bacteria to inject toxins into host cells.
Quorum quenching is the process by which organisms interfere with bacterial quorum sensing.
Biofilms are communities of bacteria embedded in a self-produced matrix of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS).
The 'persistence' of bacteria in biofilms is a major cause of chronic infection and antibiotic resistance.
Bacteriophages can carry antibiotic resistance genes between bacteria via specialized transduction.
CRISPR-Cas9 was originally a bacterial defense mechanism against bacteriophage DNA.
PAM (Protospacer Adjacent Motif) sequences are required for Cas9 to distinguish 'self' from 'non-self' DNA.
Base editing allows for the conversion of one DNA base pair to another without double-strand breaks.
Prime editing uses a 'search-and-replace' mechanism to perform precise genome insertions and deletions.
Gene drives can bypass traditional Mendelian inheritance to spread a trait through 100% of offspring.
The 'Gene Drive' controversy centers on the potential for permanent, irreversible ecological changes.
Synthetic genomes, like Syn3.0, represent the minimum set of genes required for life.
Xenobiology explores life based on non-canonical genetic codes or alternative chemistries.
The Expanded Genetic Alphabet (e.g., d5SICS and dNaM) allows for the storage of more information in DNA.
Cell-free protein synthesis systems allow for the production of proteins without living cells.
Metabolic engineering aims to optimize genetic and regulatory processes within cells to produce chemicals.
Directed evolution uses iterative rounds of mutation and selection to create enzymes with new functions.
The Nobel Prize-winning Phage Display technique allows for the rapid screening of protein-ligand interactions.
Bio-orthogonal chemistry occurs inside living systems without interfering with native biochemical processes.
Click chemistry (like the azide-alkyne cycloaddition) is used to label biomolecules in vivo.
Molecular motors like kinesin and dynein use ATP to walk along microtubules.
The 'duty ratio' of a molecular motor is the fraction of time it spends attached to its track.
Myosin V is a processive motor, meaning it takes many steps before dissociating from actin.
The 'power stroke' in muscle contraction is triggered by the release of inorganic phosphate (Pi) from myosin.
Rigor mortis occurs because the absence of ATP prevents myosin from releasing the actin filament.
The sarcomere Z-discs anchor the plus ends of actin filaments.
Titin is the largest known protein and acts as a molecular spring in muscle cells.
The length-tension relationship in muscle is determined by the overlap between actin and myosin.
Calcium-induced calcium release (CICR) is the primary mechanism for muscle contraction in the heart.
Phospholamban regulates the activity of the SERCA pump in cardiac muscle.
Troponin I inhibits the interaction between actin and myosin; Troponin T binds to tropomyosin.
The Frank-Starling law states that the stroke volume of the heart increases in response to an increase in end-diastolic volume.
The baroreceptor reflex provides rapid, short-term regulation of blood pressure.
Chemoreceptors in the carotid bodies respond to changes in arterial pO2, pCO2, and pH.
The ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) ratio describes the efficiency of gas exchange in the lungs.
Surfactant reduces surface tension in the alveoli, preventing collapse during exhalation.
Compliance is the ease with which the lungs and chest wall can be expanded.
Shunt refers to blood that bypasses the gas exchange surfaces of the lung.
Dead space is the volume of air that is inhaled but does not take part in gas exchange.
The chloride shift (Hamberguer shift) maintains electrical neutrality as bicarbonate leaves red blood cells.
Carbonic anhydrase is one of the fastest enzymes known, essential for CO2 transport.
Methemoglobin is a form of hemoglobin where the iron is in the ferric (Fe3+) state and cannot bind O2.
2,3-Bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG) stabilizes the 'T' (low affinity) state of hemoglobin.
Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) has a higher affinity for O2 than adult hemoglobin (HbA) because it binds 2,3-BPG less strongly.
The 'Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve' is sigmoidal due to cooperative binding.
A 'Right Shift' in the curve (high CO2, low pH, high temp) facilitates oxygen unloading at the tissues.
Hyperbaric oxygen therapy increases the amount of dissolved oxygen in the plasma.
Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) redirects blood flow away from poorly ventilated alveoli.
The respiratory center in the medulla oblongata is primarily sensitive to pCO2 and H+ levels.
Juxtaglomerular (JG) cells are modified smooth muscle cells that act as baroreceptors in the kidney.
The macula densa senses sodium chloride concentration in the distal tubule.
Tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF) allows the kidney to regulate its own filtration rate.
The ' myogenic mechanism' causes afferent arterioles to constrict when blood pressure rises.
Parathyroid hormone (PTH) stimulates the activation of Vitamin D in the kidney.
The kidney is the primary site of gluconeogenesis during prolonged fasting.
Clearance is the volume of plasma from which a substance is completely removed per unit time.
Inulin clearance is the gold standard for measuring glomerular filtration rate (GFR).
Creatinine clearance is a clinical estimate of GFR.
The renal threshold for glucose is approximately 180 mg/dL.
Secondary active transport in the kidney often uses the sodium gradient established by the Na/K-ATPase.
The SGLT2 transporter reabsorbs the majority of glucose in the proximal tubule.
Loop diuretics (like furosemide) inhibit the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the thick ascending limb.
Thiazide diuretics inhibit the Na-Cl symporter in the distal convoluted tubule.
Spironolactone is a potassium-sparing diuretic that antagonizes aldosterone receptors.
Countercurrent exchange in the vasa recta prevents the 'washout' of the medullary osmotic gradient.
Vasopressin (ADH) inserts AQP2 water channels into the apical membrane of collecting duct cells.
Diabetes insipidus is characterized by the inability to concentrate urine due to ADH deficiency or resistance.
The ANP/BNP system acts as a natural antagonist to the RAAS system.
Respiratory acidosis is caused by hypoventilation and CO2 retention.
Metabolic acidosis is often compensated for by respiratory hyperventilation (Kussmaul breathing).
The anion gap helps differentiate causes of metabolic acidosis.
The 'Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation' is used to calculate the pH of a buffer system.
The major extracellular buffer is the bicarbonate system; the major intracellular buffer is phosphate.
Proteins can act as buffers due to the ionizable side chains of amino acids (especially Histidine).
Sensory transduction converts a physical stimulus into a graded receptor potential.
Phasic receptors adapt rapidly to a constant stimulus (e.g., touch); tonic receptors adapt slowly (e.g., pain).
The 'Two-Point Discrimination' threshold depends on the density of mechanoreceptors and receptive field size.
Lateral inhibition enhances the contrast between a stimulus and its surroundings.
The 'Gate Control Theory' of pain suggests that non-painful input can close the 'gates' to painful input in the spinal cord.
Endogenous opioids (endorphins, enkephalins) inhibit the transmission of pain signals.
The olfactory system is unique because it bypasses the thalamus on its way to the cortex.
Odorant receptors are GPCRs that activate adenylyl cyclase to open cAMP-gated ion channels.
The 'Labeled Line' principle states that each nerve fiber to the brain transmits only one modality of sensation.
Gustatory receptors for sweet, bitter, and umami are GPCRs; salty and sour use ion channels.
The organ of Corti is the site of auditory transduction in the cochlea.
Hair cells are depolarized by the entry of K+ ions through mechanically gated channels in the stereocilia.
The basilar membrane acts as a frequency analyzer (high frequencies at the base, low at the apex).
The semicircular canals detect angular acceleration; the otolith organs detect linear acceleration.
The lens of the eye becomes more spherical for near vision (accommodation) through ciliary muscle contraction.
Phototransduction in the dark: high levels of cGMP keep ion channels open, depolarizing the photoreceptor.
Phototransduction in the light: light-activated rhodopsin activates transducin, which activates PDE, lowering cGMP and hyperpolarizing the cell.
This 'dark current' means photoreceptors release more neurotransmitter (glutamate) in the dark.
Horizontal cells in the retina provide lateral inhibition, creating center-surround receptive fields.
Color blindness is most commonly due to the absence of one or more types of cone photopigments.
The optic chiasm is where fibers from the nasal retinas cross to the opposite side of the brain.
The primary visual cortex (V1) is organized into ocular dominance columns and orientation columns.
The 'Dorsal Stream' of visual processing is for 'Where/How'; the 'Ventral Stream' is for 'What'.
Synaptic pruning is the elimination of extra synapses to increase the efficiency of neural circuits.
The 'Sensitive Period' is a window in development where the brain is particularly plastic.
Hebbian learning: 'Cells that fire together, wire together.' Episodic memory stores specific events; semantic memory stores general knowledge.
The Papez circuit is a fundamental component of the limbic system involved in emotion and memory.
Working memory is primarily mediated by the prefrontal cortex.
Mirror neurons fire both when an individual performs an action and when they observe another doing it.
The 'Default Mode Network' is active when the brain is at rest and not focused on the external world.
Brainwaves: Delta (deep sleep), Theta (drowsy), Alpha (relaxed), Beta (alert), Gamma (high-level processing).
REM sleep is characterized by high brain activity, rapid eye movements, and muscle atonia.
The 'Monoamine Hypothesis' suggests that depression is caused by a deficiency of serotonin and/or norepinephrine.
The 'Dopamine Hypothesis' suggests that schizophrenia is linked to overactivity of dopamine pathways.
Parkinson's disease is caused by the death of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra.
Alzheimer's disease is characterized by extracellular amyloid-beta plaques and intracellular tau tangles.
Prion diseases (like CJD) involve the template-directed misfolding of the PrP protein.
Excitotoxicity involves the overactivation of NMDA receptors, leading to calcium overload and cell death.
The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is permeable to lipid-soluble substances and uses transporters for glucose and amino acids.
Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is produced by the choroid plexus and absorbed by the arachnoid villi.
The 'Hydrostatic Pressure' of blood vs.
the 'Oncotic Pressure' of plasma proteins determines fluid movement in capillaries.
Edema occurs when fluid filtration exceeds lymphatic drainage.
Hypoproteinemia (low blood protein) leads to edema due to decreased oncotic pressure.
Cytokines can act as autocrine, paracrine, or endocrine signals.
The 'Cytokine Storm' is a life-threatening systemic inflammatory response.
Complement system pathways: Classical (antibody-bound), Lectin (sugar-bound), and Alternative (direct pathogen binding).
C3b acts as an opsonin; C5a acts as a potent chemoattractant for leukocytes.
Diapedesis (extravasation) is the process of leukocytes crawling through blood vessel walls to reach an infection.
Selectins mediate the initial 'rolling' of leukocytes; integrins mediate firm adhesion.
Granulocytes include neutrophils (phagocytosis), eosinophils (parasites/allergy), and basophils (histamine).
Agranulocytes include monocytes (become macrophages) and lymphocytes (B and T cells).
Dendritic cells are the most potent professional antigen-presenting cells (APCs).
The 'Double Signal' for T cell activation: 1) TCR binds MHC/antigen, 2) CD28 binds B7 on the APC.
Without the second signal (co-stimulation), the T cell becomes anergic (unresponsive).
Clonal deletion is the process of removing self-reactive lymphocytes during development (Central Tolerance).
Peripheral tolerance is maintained by Regulatory T cells (Tregs).
Immune privilege occurs in sites like the brain and eyes where immune responses are suppressed to prevent damage.
Vaccination induces 'Herd Immunity' when a large enough proportion of the population is immune.
Antigenic drift is the gradual accumulation of mutations in viral surface proteins.
Antigenic shift is the sudden exchange of genetic material between different viral strains (causes pandemics).
The 'Original Antigenic Sin' describes the tendency of the immune system to prefer its first memory of a pathogen.
Antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE) occurs when non-neutralizing antibodies actually help a virus enter cells.
Somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) is the technique used for reproductive and therapeutic cloning.
Parthenogenesis is the development of an embryo from an unfertilized egg.
The 'Redox Potential' (Eh) of a cell reflects its overall oxidative or reductive state.
Glutathione is the major antioxidant buffer in the cytosol.
NADPH is the primary source of reducing power for anabolic reactions and antioxidant defense.
The pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) produces NADPH and ribose-5-phosphate.
Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency can lead to hemolytic anemia under oxidative stress.
The 'Crabtree Effect' is the inhibition of respiration by high concentrations of glucose (similar to Warburg).
Substrate cycles (futile cycles) occur when two opposing metabolic pathways are active simultaneously, generating heat.
Brown adipose tissue (BAT) uses UCP1 (thermogenin) to uncouple respiration from ATP synthesis to generate heat.
Leptin resistance is a key factor in the development of obesity.
Adiponectin is a hormone from fat tissue that increases insulin sensitivity.
The 'Thrifty Gene' hypothesis suggests that genes favoring fat storage were adaptive in our evolutionary past.
Epigenetic 'memory' of famine can be passed down through generations.
Metabolic syndrome is a cluster of conditions (obesity, high BP, high sugar) that increase heart disease risk.
Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is strongly linked to insulin resistance.
Glycation is the non-enzymatic attachment of sugars to proteins (e.g., HbA1c in diabetes).
Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) contribute to tissue aging and complications of diabetes.
The 'Polyol Pathway' converts glucose to sorbitol, contributing to osmotic damage in diabetic tissues.
Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) requires 'Intrinsic Factor' from the stomach for absorption in the ileum.
Pernicious anemia is an autoimmune disease that destroys gastric parietal cells, leading to B12 deficiency.
Folic acid is essential for DNA synthesis; deficiency during pregnancy causes neural tube defects.
Vitamin D acts more like a hormone than a vitamin, regulating hundreds of genes.
The 'Hygiene Hypothesis' has been expanded into the 'Old Friends' hypothesis.
Bacteriocins are antimicrobial peptides produced by bacteria to kill competing strains.
Phage therapy uses viruses to treat bacterial infections as an alternative to antibiotics.
The 'Viremia' is the presence of viruses in the blood.
The 'Window Period' is the time between infection and when a test can detect the pathogen or antibodies.
R0 (Basic Reproduction Number) is the average number of people one infected person will infect.
Zoonosis is a disease that can be transmitted from animals to humans.
Reverse genetics starts with a gene and looks for the resulting phenotype (e.g., gene knockout).
Forward genetics starts with a phenotype and looks for the underlying gene.
Synthetically engineered 'Logic Gates' can be built inside living cells using genetic circuits.
Biological 'Flash Memory' can be created by recording cellular events into DNA sequences.
DNA data storage uses the four bases to store digital information with incredible density.
The 'Human Protein Atlas' aims to map all human proteins in all major tissues and organs.
The 'Human Cell Atlas' aims to create a comprehensive reference map of every cell type in the human body.
Biomimetics (biomimicry) is the design of materials and systems modeled on biological entities.
Bioluminescence is the production of light by a living organism (e.g., luciferase reaction).
Bio-fluorescence is the absorption of light and its re-emission at a different wavelength.
The 'Great Filter' hypothesis in astrobiology suggests there is a barrier to the development of advanced civilizations.
The 'Goldilocks Zone' is the habitable zone around a star where liquid water can exist.
Extremophiles like Deinococcus radiodurans can survive extreme doses of radiation.
Synthetic biology 'parts' are standardized DNA sequences called BioBricks.
The 'Double-Blind' study is the gold standard for clinical trials to eliminate bias.
The 'Placebo Effect' demonstrates the powerful connection between the mind and physical health.
The 'Nocebo Effect' is the experience of negative side effects due to negative expectations.
Bioethics must evolve alongside technology (e.g., CRISPR babies, AI in healthcare).
The 'Principles of Biomedical Ethics': Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, and Justice.
Precision Medicine is the goal of providing the right treatment to the right patient at the right time.
