Advanced Reproductive Health & Intimate Medicine (Facts 51-100)
Semen contains fructose, which serves as the primary energy source for sperm motility.
The blood-testis barrier protects developing sperm from being attacked by the body's own immune system.
A typical ejaculate contains between 40 million and 300 million sperm cells.
The vagina maintains an acidic pH (3.8–4.5) to inhibit the growth of pathogenic bacteria.
Lactobacillus is the dominant genus of bacteria in a healthy vaginal microbiome, producing lactic acid.
The clitoris contains thousands of nerve endings and is the only human organ dedicated solely to sensory pleasure.
The corpus cavernosum and corpus spongiosum are the erectile tissues of the penis that fill with blood during an erection.
Nitric oxide is a key neurotransmitter that triggers the relaxation of smooth muscles in the penis to allow blood flow.
HCG (Human Chorionic Gonadotropin) is the hormone detected by pregnancy tests, produced after implantation.
The SRY gene on the Y chromosome is responsible for triggering the development of male gonads in an embryo.
Cryptorchidism is the medical term for undescended testes, a condition often monitored in male infants.
Sperm can survive inside the female reproductive tract for up to 5 days under optimal conditions.
The 'fertile window' generally includes the day of ovulation and the five days preceding it.
Ejaculatory duct obstruction is a rare but treatable cause of male infertility.
Vaginismus is the involuntary contraction of vaginal muscles, making penetration painful or impossible.
Uterine fibroids are non-cancerous growths of the uterus that can cause heavy menstrual bleeding.
The Skene's glands and Bartholin's glands provide lubrication for the female genital area.
Mittelschmerz is the one-sided, lower abdominal pain associated with ovulation.
The hymen is a thin piece of mucosal tissue that partially surrounds the vaginal opening and can vary greatly in shape.
Testicular cancer is most common in young men aged 15 to 35 and is highly treatable if caught early.
Ovarian cancer is often called a 'silent killer' because symptoms are often vague until the disease is advanced.
Preeclampsia is a pregnancy complication characterized by high blood pressure and signs of organ damage.
The amniotic sac usually ruptures ('water breaking') at the onset of or during labor.
A Cesarean section (C-section) is the surgical delivery of a baby through incisions in the abdomen and uterus.
Postnatal involution is the process by which the uterus returns to its pre-pregnancy size after birth.
Phimosis is a condition where the foreskin cannot be retracted over the glans of the penis.
Hypospadias is a congenital condition where the opening of the urethra is on the underside of the penis.
Seminal vesicles contribute about 60-70% of the total volume of semen.
The bulbourethral glands (Cowper's glands) produce a clear, alkaline pre-ejaculate to neutralize acidity in the urethra.
The average menstrual cycle length is 28 days, but ranges from 21 to 35 days are considered normal.
Prostaglandins are chemicals that cause the uterine muscles to contract during menstruation and labor.
Anovulation is a cycle in which the ovaries do not release an egg.
Hirsutism is the growth of excessive body hair in women, often linked to high androgen levels (like in PCOS).
Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS) is a rare, life-threatening complication of certain bacterial infections, historically linked to
tampon use.
The G-spot (Gräfenberg spot) is described as an erogenous area on the anterior wall of the vagina.
Spermatocele is a painless, fluid-filled cyst in the long, tightly coiled tube that lies above and behind each testicle.
Varicocele is an enlargement of the veins within the scrotum, which can lower sperm quality and count.
The term 'primary amenorrhea' is used when a person has not started menstruating by age 15.
Hysterectomy is the surgical removal of the uterus.
Salpingitis is the inflammation of the fallopian tubes, often caused by an infection.
The corpus luteum is what remains of the ovarian follicle after ovulation; it secretes progesterone.
Maternal-fetal medicine (perinatology) is a subspecialty of OB/GYN focusing on high-risk pregnancies.
The 'morning-after pill' (emergency contraception) works primarily by delaying or preventing ovulation.
An Episiotomy is a surgical incision of the perineum to enlarge the vaginal opening during childbirth.
Urogenital atrophy is the thinning and drying of vaginal tissues due to low estrogen after menopause.
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is an infection of the female reproductive organs, often a complication of STIs.
The PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen) test can be used to screen for prostate cancer, though it is not definitive.
Amniocentesis is a procedure used to take a small sample of amniotic fluid for genetic testing of the fetus.
Quickening is the term for the first time a pregnant person feels the fetus move.
Sperm are stored in the tail of the epididymis until ejaculation.
