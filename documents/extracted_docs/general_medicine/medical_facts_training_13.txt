200 Advanced First Aid Facts for AI Training
Check the scene for hazards like fire, downed power lines, or unstable structures before approaching.
The primary survey should be completed in less than 60 seconds.
If a victim is unresponsive and not breathing, call emergency services immediately.
Hands-only CPR is recommended for untrained bystanders.
The 'CAB' sequence stands for Compressions, Airway, Breathing.
For chest compressions, use the heel of one hand with the other hand on top.
Keep arms straight and shoulders directly over the hands during CPR.
A full chest recoil allows the heart to refill with blood between compressions.
Minimize interruptions in chest compressions to less than 10 seconds.
If two rescuers are present, switch roles every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue.
Rescue breaths should last about 1 second and cause visible chest rise.
Avoid excessive ventilation during CPR as it can decrease cardiac output.
An AED can be used on a victim submerged in water only after moving them to a dry area and wiping the chest.
Remove medicinal patches from the chest before applying AED pads.
Shave excessive chest hair if it prevents AED pads from sticking properly.
AED pads for adults can be used on children, but pediatric pads should not be used on adults.
In a pregnant victim, perform chest compressions slightly higher on the sternum.
For an infant, the compression depth is about 1.5 inches (4 cm).
Pulse checks are no longer recommended for lay rescuers.
If a person is gasping but not breathing normally, treat it as cardiac arrest (agonal gasps).
Opioid overdose can be identified by pinpoint pupils and respiratory depression.
Naloxone can be administered intranasally or intramuscularly.
Position an unconscious, breathing overdose victim in the recovery position.
Choking is characterized by an inability to speak, cough, or breathe.
Encourage a person to keep coughing if they have a partial airway obstruction.
Perform back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
For a choking pregnant woman, use chest thrusts instead of abdominal thrusts.
If a choking victim is too large for you to reach around, use chest thrusts.
In an infant, deliver 5 back blows followed by 5 chest thrusts.
Check the mouth for the obstructing object before every set of rescue breaths during CPR for a choking victim.
Venous bleeding is dark red and flows steadily.
Arterial bleeding is bright red and spurts in time with the heartbeat.
Capillary bleeding is a slow ooze from small vessels.
Elevation of a bleeding limb is no longer a primary recommendation; use direct pressure.
If a bandage is soaked through, add a second layer; do not remove the first.
Pressure dressings should be tight but not cut off circulation completely.
A tourniquet is used when direct pressure fails to control life-threatening limb bleeding.
Place a tourniquet 'high and tight' in mass casualty or dark situations.
Hemostatic dressings contain agents that speed up blood clotting.
Pack deep wounds with gauze before applying direct pressure.
Internal bleeding may present with 'guarding' (tensing the abdominal muscles).
A hematoma is a localized collection of blood under the skin.
Signs of a skull fracture include 'raccoon eyes' (bruising around eyes).
Battle’s sign is bruising behind the ear indicating a possible skull base fracture.
Clear fluid leaking from the ears or nose after head trauma may be cerebrospinal fluid.
Maintain manual stabilization of the head and neck in any suspected spinal injury.
A jaw-thrust maneuver is used to open the airway without moving the neck.
Penetrating eye injuries should be covered with a rigid shield (e.g., a paper cup).
Do not apply pressure to the eyeball in case of a penetrating injury.
Flush an eye for at least 15 minutes if exposed to chemicals.
Keep an amputated part cool but do not place it directly on ice.
Wrap an amputated limb in sterile gauze and place it in a waterproof bag.
First-degree burns affect only the epidermis (e.g., sunburn).
Second-degree burns involve blisters and damage to the dermis.
Third-degree burns involve all layers of the skin and may look white, brown, or black.
Never pop blisters on a burn as it increases the risk of infection.
For large burns, avoid using cold water for long periods to prevent hypothermia.
Electrical burns often have an entry and an exit wound.
Internal damage from an electrical shock can be much worse than it appears on the skin.
Chemical burns from powders (like lime) should be brushed off before flushing with water.
Radiation burns require the victim to be moved away from the source immediately.
Inhalation burns may be indicated by singed nose hairs or soot in the mouth.
An open chest wound requires an airtight seal on three sides to allow air to escape.
A flail chest occurs when multiple ribs are broken in two or more places.
Support a flail chest segment with a bulky dressing or pillow.
Evisceration occurs when organs protrude through an abdominal wound.
Cover protruding organs with a moist, sterile dressing; do not push them back in.
In shock, the body prioritizes blood flow to the brain, heart, and lungs.
Compensated shock presents with a fast heart rate and normal blood pressure.
Decompensated shock involves a significant drop in blood pressure.
Keep a shock victim warm to prevent the 'lethal triad' of trauma: acidosis, coagulopathy, hypothermia.
Anaphylaxis can cause rapid swelling of the airway within minutes.
Epinephrine works by constricting blood vessels and relaxing airway muscles.
Check for a second dose of epinephrine if help is delayed and symptoms persist.
Hyperglycemia (high blood sugar) can cause a fruity or 'acetone' smell on the breath.
Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) often presents with cold, clammy skin and confusion.
If a diabetic patient is conscious but the cause is unknown, give sugar.
A transient ischemic attack (TIA) is a 'mini-stroke' with symptoms that resolve within 24 hours.
Do not give aspirin to a person having a suspected stroke unless directed by a doctor.
Status epilepticus is a seizure lasting more than 5 minutes and is a medical emergency.
Postictal phase is the period of confusion following a seizure.
Inverted 'T' waves on an ECG can be a sign of myocardial ischemia.
Angina is chest pain that typically resolves with rest or medication (Nitroglycerin).
Aspirin for a heart attack should be chewed to increase the speed of absorption.
Inverted breathing or 'gurgling' indicates a compromised airway.
Asthma inhalers should be used with a spacer if available for better delivery.
Heat cramps are painful muscle spasms due to salt and water loss.
Heat exhaustion features heavy sweating; heatstroke features a lack of sweating.
Immersive cooling in ice water is the most effective treatment for heatstroke.
Moderate hypothermia presents with violent shivering and loss of coordination.
Severe hypothermia may cause the person to stop shivering and lose consciousness.
Rewarm the core of a hypothermia victim before the extremities (to avoid afterdrop).
Paradoxical undressing is a sign of severe hypothermia where the victim feels hot.
Frostbite causes skin to become hard, cold, and white or yellowish.
Do not rewarm frostbite if there is a chance the area will freeze again.
Blisters from frostbite should not be ruptured.
Bee stings should be scraped off with a credit card, not squeezed with tweezers.
Tick removal should involve pulling the tick straight up with fine-tipped tweezers.
Spider bites (Black Widow) can cause severe muscle cramping and abdominal pain.
Brown Recluse bites often cause a 'bullseye' pattern followed by skin necrosis.
For snake bites, remove rings or tight clothing before swelling begins.
Do not apply ice or a tourniquet to a North American pit viper bite.
Jellyfish stings should be rinsed with vinegar to neutralize the venom.
Do not use fresh water on jellyfish stings as it can trigger more venom release.
Poison ivy rash is caused by an oil called urushiol.
Wash skin with soap and cool water immediately after contact with poison ivy.
Activated charcoal is used in some poisoning cases to bind toxins in the stomach.
Call Poison Control (1-800-222-1222 in the US) for specific ingestion advice.
Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by medical professionals.
Corrosive substances (like bleach) can cause more damage if vomited back up.
A sprain is a tearing or stretching of ligaments.
A strain is a tearing or stretching of muscles or tendons.
Fractures can be closed (skin intact) or open (skin broken).
Crepitus is the grating sound made by bone ends rubbing together.
Splint a limb in the position it was found; do not try to straighten it.
Check for circulation (pulse, color, temperature) before and after splinting.
A triangular bandage can be used as a sling to support the upper arm.
Pelvic fractures carry a high risk of massive internal bleeding.
Stabilize a fractured pelvis with a pelvic binder or a tightly wrapped bedsheet.
The 'Rule of Threes' in survival: 3 minutes without air, 3 days without water, 3 weeks without food.
In wilderness first aid, a 'cleared' spine requires the patient to be fully alert.
High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE) presents with ataxia and confusion.
High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE) presents with shortness of breath at rest.
The only definitive treatment for altitude sickness is descent.
Snow blindness is essentially a sunburn of the cornea.
Use duct tape as a temporary treatment for blisters in a wilderness setting.
Triage: Red tags are for immediate life-threats.
Triage: Yellow tags are for delayed but serious injuries.
Triage: Green tags are for 'walking wounded'.
Triage: Black tags are for deceased or those with unsalvageable injuries.
The START method of triage stands for Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment.
In a mass casualty, prioritize your own safety first.
CISM (Critical Incident Stress Management) helps rescuers deal with trauma.
Consent must be obtained before helping a conscious adult.
Implied consent applies to unconscious victims or minors without a guardian.
Good Samaritan laws protect rescuers from liability if they act reasonably.
Abandonment occurs when a rescuer stops care without handing over to an equal or higher authority.
Negligence occurs when a rescuer fails to act according to their level of training.
Maintain patient confidentiality (HIPAA) unless sharing with medical professionals.
A secondary survey includes a 'SAMPLE' history: Signs, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last oral intake, Events.
Assess skin temperature using the back of your hand.
Cyanosis (blue skin) indicates a lack of oxygen in the blood.
A 'thready' pulse is weak and difficult to feel.
Agonal breathing is not effective breathing; start CPR.
Stridor is a high-pitched noise during inhalation indicating an upper airway blockage.
Wheezing is a whistling sound during exhalation indicating narrowed lower airways.
Rales are clicking or rattling sounds in the lungs indicating fluid.
Orthostatic vitals check for a drop in BP when moving from sitting to standing.
Assess pupil reaction with a penlight: pupils should be PERRL (Equal, Round, Reactive to Light).
Fixed and dilated pupils can be a sign of severe brain injury.
A 'blown pupil' (one dilated, one constricted) suggests intracranial pressure.
Nystagmus is involuntary eye movement that can indicate inner ear or brain issues.
Tympany (a drum-like sound) during abdominal percussion suggests gas.
Rebound tenderness is pain when pressure is released, suggesting peritonitis.
A 'step-off' in the spine is a palpable misalignment of vertebrae.
Assess distal sensory function by asking the patient which toe or finger you are touching.
Assess distal motor function by asking the patient to wiggle their toes or fingers.
Burn percentage: The palm of the patient's hand is roughly 1% of their body surface area.
A tourniquet should be placed at least 2 inches above the wound.
Do not place a tourniquet directly over a joint.
Tighten a tourniquet until the distal pulse is no longer felt.
Mark a 'T' and the time on the patient's forehead after applying a tourniquet.
If one tourniquet fails to stop the bleeding, apply a second one above the first.
A chest seal should be checked for signs of a tension pneumothorax.
A tension pneumothorax causes a shift in the trachea away from the injured side.
Needle decompression is a professional medical intervention for tension pneumothorax.
Sucking chest wounds are also called open pneumothoraxes.
Gastric distension (air in stomach) can occur if rescue breaths are too forceful.
Gastric distension increases the risk of vomiting and aspiration.
The diaphragm is the primary muscle of respiration.
The intercostal muscles between the ribs assist with breathing.
Accessory muscle use (neck/shoulders) indicates significant respiratory distress.
A silent chest in an asthma patient is a sign of impending respiratory failure.
Hyperventilation can cause tingling in the hands and feet (carpopedal spasm).
Pulse oximetry measures the percentage of hemoglobin saturated with oxygen.
A pulse ox reading below 94% generally indicates the need for oxygen therapy.
Nail polish or cold fingers can cause inaccurate pulse oximetry readings.
Carbon monoxide poisoning can cause a false high reading on a standard pulse oximeter.
Signs of CO poisoning include 'cherry red' skin and a throbbing headache.
Move CO poisoning victims to fresh air immediately.
Decompression sickness (the bends) is caused by nitrogen bubbles in the blood.
Divers with the bends should be kept on 100% oxygen and transported to a hyperbaric chamber.
Nitrogen narcosis feels like alcohol intoxication during a deep dive.
Drowning is the process of experiencing respiratory impairment from submersion in liquid.
Secondary drowning (pulmonary edema) can occur hours after a person is rescued from water.
A near-drowning victim must always be evaluated by a hospital.
Hypothermia can have a protective effect on the brain during drowning in cold water.
Do not stop CPR on a cold-water drowning victim until they are 'warm and dead'.
Emergency moves include the blanket drag, clothes drag, and firefighter's carry.
Log rolling is used to move a spinal-injury patient onto a backboard.
Maintain C-spine stabilization during the entire log roll process.
A quick way to check responsiveness is the AVPU scale: Alert, Verbal, Pain, Unresponsive.
GCS (Glasgow Coma Scale) is used to quantify the level of consciousness.
The lowest possible GCS score is 3, and the highest is 15.
A GCS score of 8 or less typically indicates a severe brain injury.
An impaled object in the eye should have both eyes covered to prevent sympathetic movement.
A pneumothorax is the presence of air in the pleural space between the lung and chest wall.
A hemothorax is the presence of blood in the pleural space.
First aid is about stabilizing the patient and preventing further injury until definitive care arrives.
