A radiopaque substance on an X-ray appears white because it absorbs more radiation.
A radiolucent substance appears black on an X-ray because radiation passes through it easily.
In a chest X-ray, air-filled lungs appear black, while the heart and bones appear white.
Pneumonia typically presents as a localized white opacity (consolidation) on a chest X-ray.
Pleural effusion is identified on an X-ray by the blunting of the costophrenic angles.
A tension pneumothorax is a medical emergency visible on X-ray as a collapsed lung with a tracheal shift.
Computed Tomography (CT) uses X-rays to create cross-sectional 'slices' of the body.
CT scans are superior for detecting acute intracranial hemorrhage (bleeding in the brain).
On a head CT, acute blood appears bright white, while older blood (chronic) appears darker.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves, not ionizing radiation.
MRI is the gold standard for visualizing soft tissues, spinal cord injuries, and brain tumors.
T1-weighted MRI sequences show fat as bright and water (CSF) as dark.
T2-weighted MRI sequences show water (CSF) as bright, making them useful for detecting edema.
Contrast agents like Gadolinium are used in MRI to highlight areas of inflammation or vascularity.
Iodinated contrast used in CT scans can be nephrotoxic and requires checking kidney function (Creatinine) first.
Ultrasonography uses high-frequency sound waves and is safe for use during pregnancy.
Hyperechoic structures on ultrasound appear bright (e.g., gallstones or bone).
Anechoic structures on ultrasound appear black (e.g., fluid-filled cysts or the bladder).
Doppler ultrasound measures the direction and velocity of blood flow through vessels.
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) scans use radioactive tracers to detect metabolic activity, often for cancer staging.
Hyperplasia is an increase in the number of cells in a tissue or organ.
Hypertrophy is an increase in the size of individual cells, leading to organ enlargement.
Atrophy is the shrinkage of cell size due to loss of cell substance or disuse.
Metaplasia is a reversible change where one adult cell type is replaced by another (e.g., in smoker's lungs).
Apoptosis is programmed cell death, a clean process that does not trigger inflammation.
Necrosis is accidental cell death caused by external injury, leading to an inflammatory response.
Ischemia is a restriction in blood supply to tissues, causing a shortage of oxygen and glucose.
Infarction is tissue death (necrosis) caused by a local lack of oxygen due to an obstruction of blood supply.
Edema is the accumulation of excess fluid in the interstitial spaces of tissues.
Anasarca is severe, generalized edema involving the entire body.
The four cardinal signs of acute inflammation are rubor (redness), calor (heat), tumor (swelling), and dolor (pain).
A fifth sign of inflammation often cited is functio laesa (loss of function).
Angiogenesis is the physiological process through which new blood vessels form from pre-existing vessels.
A thrombus is a blood clot that forms inside a blood vessel and remains there.
An embolus is a thrombus or foreign body that breaks loose and travels through the bloodstream.
Sepsis is a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.
Septic shock is sepsis combined with persistent hypotension despite fluid resuscitation.
An endoscopy allows direct visualization of the upper gastrointestinal tract (esophagus, stomach, duodenum).
A colonoscopy is used to visualize the large intestine and distal part of the small intestine.
A lumbar puncture (spinal tap) collects cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) from the subarachnoid space.
Normal CSF is clear and colorless; cloudiness may indicate bacterial meningitis.
A biopsy involves the removal of a small piece of tissue for microscopic pathological examination.
Cytology is the study of individual cells, often obtained via fine-needle aspiration (FNA).
Histology is the study of the microscopic structure of tissues.
Pathognomonic is a term for a sign or symptom that is specifically characteristic of a particular disease.
The incubation period is the time between exposure to a pathogen and the onset of symptoms.
A latent infection is an asymptomatic infection that can reactivate later (e.g., Tuberculosis or Herpes).
Auscultation is the act of listening to internal body sounds, usually with a stethoscope.
Percussion is tapping on the body surface to determine the density of underlying tissues.
Palpation is feeling the body with hands to assess size, consistency, and tenderness of organs.
Kyphosis is an exaggerated outward curvature of the thoracic spine (hunchback).
Lordosis is an exaggerated inward curvature of the lumbar spine (swayback).
Auscultating 'crackles' (rales) in the lungs often suggests fluid in the alveoli (e.g., heart failure).
Auscultating 'wheezes' suggests airway narrowing (e.g., asthma or COPD).
A cardiac murmur is an extra or unusual sound heard during a heartbeat, caused by turbulent blood flow.
Stenosis is the abnormal narrowing of a body channel or valve.
Regurgitation in heart valves occurs when a valve does not close tightly, allowing blood to leak backward.
An aneurysm is a localized, balloon-like bulge in the wall of a blood vessel.
A dissecting aneurysm occurs when blood enters the wall of an artery, stripping the layers apart.
Atherosclerosis is the buildup of fats, cholesterol, and other substances in and on the artery walls.
Stable angina is predictable chest pain that occurs with exertion and is relieved by rest.
Unstable angina is chest pain that occurs at rest and is considered a precursor to a heart attack.
Orthopnea is shortness of breath that occurs when lying flat, a classic sign of heart failure.
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND) is sudden shortness of breath that wakes a person from sleep.
Hematemesis is the vomiting of blood.
Hemoptysis is the coughing up of blood from the respiratory tract.
Melena is dark, tarry stools containing digested blood, usually from an upper GI source.
Hematochezia is the passage of fresh, bright red blood from the rectum.
Ascites is the accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, often due to liver cirrhosis.
Splenomegaly is the abnormal enlargement of the spleen.
Hepatomegaly is the abnormal enlargement of the liver.
Jaundice (icterus) is the yellowing of skin and eyes due to high bilirubin levels.
Pruritus is the medical term for itching.
Urticaria (hives) are red, itchy welts resulting from a skin reaction.
A papule is a small, solid, raised bump on the skin.
A vesicle is a small, fluid-filled blister on the skin.
A pustule is a small blister or pimple on the skin containing pus.
Cyanosis is a bluish discoloration of the skin resulting from poor circulation or inadequate oxygenation.
Clubbing of the fingers is a sign of chronic hypoxia, often seen in lung or heart disease.
Peripheral neuropathy is damage to the nerves of the peripheral nervous system, often causing numbness or tingling.
Areflexia is the absence of neurological reflexes.
Hyperreflexia is an overactive or overresponsive neurological reflex.
Ataxia is the loss of full control of bodily movements, leading to an unsteady gait.
Dysphasia is deficiency in the generation or comprehension of speech.
Syncope is a temporary loss of consciousness caused by a fall in blood pressure.
Vertigo is a sensation of whirling and loss of balance, often associated with inner ear issues.
Tinnitus is a ringing or buzzing noise in the ears.
Nystagmus is rapid, involuntary movement of the eyes.
Anisocoria is a condition characterized by unequal pupil size.
Hemiparesis is weakness on one side of the body.
Hemiplegia is total paralysis on one side of the body.
The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) measures eye, verbal, and motor responses.
Osteomyelitis is an infection in a bone.
Septic arthritis is a painful infection in a joint.
Gout is a form of inflammatory arthritis characterized by recurrent attacks of a red, tender, hot, and swollen joint.
Pseudogout is a form of arthritis caused by the deposition of calcium pyrophosphate crystals.
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) is an autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks its own tissues.
Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic inflammatory disorder affecting many joints, including those in the hands and feet.
Osteoarthritis is the most common form of arthritis, caused by protective cartilage wearing down over time.
Ankylosing spondylitis is an inflammatory arthritis affecting the spine and large joints.
Psoriatic arthritis is a form of arthritis that affects some people who have psoriasis.
Scleroderma is a group of rare diseases that involve the hardening and tightening of the skin.
Sjögren's syndrome is an immune system disorder characterized by dry eyes and a dry mouth.
Vasculitis is an inflammation of the blood vessels that causes changes in the blood vessel walls.
Glomerulonephritis is inflammation of the tiny filters in the kidneys (glomeruli).
Pyelonephritis is a type of urinary tract infection that affects one or both kidneys.
Nephrotic syndrome is a kidney disorder that causes the body to excrete too much protein in the urine.
Nephritic syndrome is characterized by inflammation of the glomeruli, hematuria, and hypertension.
Benign tumors are non-cancerous and do not spread to other parts of the body.
Malignant tumors are cancerous and can invade nearby tissues and spread to other parts of the body.
Metastasis is the spread of cancer cells from the place where they first formed to another part of the body.
Carcinoma is a type of cancer that starts in cells that make up the skin or the tissue lining organs.
Sarcoma is a type of cancer that begins in bone or in the soft tissues of the body.
Leukemia is cancer of the body's blood-forming tissues, including the bone marrow and the lymphatic system.
Lymphoma is a cancer of the lymphatic system, which is part of the body's germ-fighting network.
Myeloma is a cancer that forms in a type of white blood cell called a plasma cell.
Adjuvant therapy is additional cancer treatment given after the primary treatment to lower the risk of recurrence.
Neoadjuvant therapy is treatment given as a first step to shrink a tumor before the main treatment.
Palliative care is specialized medical care for people living with a serious illness, focused on symptom relief.
Remission is a decrease in or disappearance of signs and symptoms of cancer.
Relapse is the return of a disease or the signs and symptoms of a disease after a period of improvement.
Comorbidity is the presence of one or more additional conditions co-occurring with a primary condition.
Prognosis is the likely course of a disease or the chance of recovery or recurrence.
Diagnosis is the identification of the nature of an illness or other problem by examination of the symptoms.
Etiology is the cause, set of causes, or manner of causation of a disease or condition.
Epidemiology is the study of how often diseases occur in different groups of people and why.
Prevalence is the proportion of a population found to have a condition.
Incidence is the number of new cases of a condition in a population during a specific period.
Morbidity is the condition of being diseased or the rate of disease in a population.
Mortality is the state of being subject to death or the death rate in a population.
Prophylaxis is action taken to prevent disease, especially by specified means or against a specified disease.
Therapeutics is the branch of medicine concerned with the treatment of disease and the action of remedial agents.
Pharmacokinetics is the branch of pharmacology concerned with the movement of drugs within the body.
Pharmacodynamics is the branch of pharmacology concerned with the effects of drugs and the mechanism of their action.
Absorption is the process by which a drug enters the bloodstream.
Distribution is the process by which a drug is carried to its site of action.
Metabolism is the process by which the body breaks down a drug.
Excretion is the process by which a drug is removed from the body.
The half-life of a drug is the time it takes for the concentration of the drug in the blood to be reduced by half.
A dose-response relationship describes the change in effect on an organism caused by differing levels of exposure to a stressor.
The therapeutic window is the range of drug dosages which can treat disease effectively without having toxic effects.
Bioavailability is the proportion of a drug or other substance which enters the circulation when introduced into the body.
First-pass effect is a phenomenon of drug metabolism whereby the concentration of a drug is greatly reduced before it reaches the systemic circulation.
Steady state is reached when the rate of drug elimination is equal to the rate of drug administration.
A loading dose is an initial higher dose of a drug that may be given at the beginning of a course of treatment.
A maintenance dose is the dose of a drug that is given to maintain a steady state of the drug in the body.
Drug interaction is a situation in which a substance affects the activity of a drug when both are administered together.
Adverse drug reaction is an unwanted or harmful reaction experienced after the administration of a drug.
Tolerance is a person's diminished response to a drug, which occurs when the drug is used repeatedly.
Dependence is an adaptive state that develops from repeated drug administration and results in withdrawal upon cessation.
Withdrawal is the group of symptoms that occur upon the abrupt discontinuation or decrease in intake of medications or recreational drugs.
