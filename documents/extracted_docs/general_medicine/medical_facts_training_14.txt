In cardiac arrest, high-quality CPR with a compression rate of 100-120 per minute and depth of at least 5
cm is the most critical intervention before defibrillation.
Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO is administered every 3-5 minutes during cardiac arrest for both shockable and
non-shockable rhythms.
Ventricular fibrillation is treated with immediate defibrillation at 200 J biphasic, followed by CPR resumption
for 2 minutes before rhythm reassessment.
Amiodarone 300 mg IV is the first-line antiarrhythmic in refractory ventricular fibrillation after the third
shock.
In pulseless electrical activity, reversible causes are identified using the 4 H's and 4 T's: hypoxia,
hypovolemia, hypothermia, hydrogen ion, tension pneumothorax, tamponade, toxins, and thrombosis.
Airway management in the emergency setting follows the sequence: jaw thrust, oropharyngeal airway,
bag-valve-mask ventilation, supraglottic airway, and endotracheal intubation.
Rapid sequence intubation uses a sedative agent followed immediately by a neuromuscular blocking
agent to facilitate endotracheal intubation with minimal risk of aspiration.
Ketamine 1-2 mg/kg IV is preferred for RSI induction in hypotensive patients due to its sympathomimetic
properties that help maintain blood pressure.
Succinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg IV is the preferred paralytic for RSI when a short duration of action is desired or
a difficult airway is anticipated.
Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg IV is used as an alternative to succinylcholine, especially when succinylcholine is
contraindicated such as in hyperkalemia or penetrating eye injuries.
Cricothyrotomy is the definitive rescue airway when intubation and ventilation fail in a cannot-intubate,
cannot-oxygenate scenario.
In anaphylaxis, epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM into the lateral thigh is the first and most critical treatment.
Antihistamines and corticosteroids are adjuncts in anaphylaxis but must never replace or delay
epinephrine administration.
Anaphylactic patients should be observed for at least 4-6 hours after epinephrine administration due to the
risk of biphasic anaphylaxis.
In status asthmaticus, salbutamol via nebulizer or MDI with spacer is the first-line bronchodilator, with
ipratropium bromide added in moderate-to-severe cases.
Intravenous magnesium sulfate 2 g over 20 minutes is given in severe acute asthma exacerbation not
responding to initial bronchodilator therapy.
Systemic corticosteroids should be administered early in acute asthma exacerbation to reduce airway
inflammation and decrease hospitalization rates.
Heliox, a helium-oxygen mixture, can reduce airway resistance and improve ventilation in severe asthma
not responding to standard therapy.
In COPD exacerbation, controlled oxygen therapy targeting SpO2 88-92% prevents hypercapnic
respiratory failure from suppression of hypoxic drive.
Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation is the preferred respiratory support in COPD exacerbation with
hypercapnic respiratory failure to avoid intubation.
Tension pneumothorax presents with respiratory distress, absent breath sounds, hypotension, tracheal
deviation, and distended neck veins, requiring immediate needle decompression.
Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax is performed at the second intercostal space,
midclavicular line, using a 14-16 gauge needle.
Definitive treatment of pneumothorax is chest tube insertion in the fourth or fifth intercostal space, anterior
axillary line.
Massive hemothorax is defined as more than 1500 mL of blood in the pleural cavity and requires chest
tube drainage and surgical consultation.
Cardiac tamponade presents with Beck's triad: hypotension, distended neck veins, and muffled heart
sounds.
Pericardiocentesis is performed under ultrasound guidance with needle insertion at the subxiphoid angle
directed toward the left shoulder.
In acute ST-elevation myocardial infarction, primary percutaneous coronary intervention is the preferred
reperfusion strategy with a door-to-balloon time target of less than 90 minutes.
Fibrinolytic therapy is administered in STEMI when PCI is not available within 120 minutes and there are
no contraindications.
Aspirin 300 mg chewed immediately followed by a P2Y12 inhibitor such as ticagrelor or clopidogrel is
standard antiplatelet therapy in ACS.
Unfractionated heparin or low molecular weight heparin is given as anticoagulation in ACS to prevent
further thrombus formation.
Nitroglycerin is contraindicated in ACS if systolic blood pressure is below 90 mmHg, the patient has taken
a PDE5 inhibitor within 24-48 hours, or right ventricular infarction is suspected.
Right ventricular infarction is identified by ST elevation in V4R and is treated with IV fluids to maintain
preload, avoiding nitroglycerin and diuretics.
In acute decompensated heart failure with pulmonary edema, furosemide 40-80 mg IV, nitroglycerin
infusion, and non-invasive ventilation are the mainstay treatments.
Morphine is no longer routinely recommended in acute pulmonary edema due to evidence suggesting
increased mortality.
Aortic dissection is classified by the Stanford system: Type A involves the ascending aorta and requires
emergency surgery, Type B involves only the descending aorta and is managed medically.
In suspected aortic dissection, blood pressure should be controlled to a systolic of 100-120 mmHg using IV
labetalol or esmolol to reduce aortic wall stress.
CT angiography of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is the gold standard imaging for aortic dissection.
The D-dimer test has high sensitivity for excluding pulmonary embolism in low-to-intermediate pretest
probability patients using the Wells score.
Systemic thrombolysis with alteplase is indicated in massive PE with hemodynamic compromise, using a
dose of 100 mg over 2 hours.
Anticoagulation with unfractionated heparin is initiated in confirmed PE, with LMWH or DOAC used for
subacute management.
In ischemic stroke, IV alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) is given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset in
eligible patients.
Mechanical thrombectomy is indicated in large vessel occlusion ischemic stroke within 6-24 hours of
symptom onset in appropriately selected patients.
Blood pressure is not treated aggressively in ischemic stroke unless it exceeds 220/120 mmHg or the
patient is a thrombolysis candidate.
Hemorrhagic stroke requires neurosurgical consultation, blood pressure control targeting less than 140
mmHg systolic, and reversal of any anticoagulation.
In subarachnoid hemorrhage, nimodipine 60 mg orally every 4 hours is started to prevent cerebral
vasospasm.
CT of the head without contrast is the first imaging study in suspected subarachnoid hemorrhage; lumbar
puncture is performed if CT is negative but clinical suspicion remains high.
Xanthochromia or elevated red blood cell count in CSF confirms subarachnoid hemorrhage when CT is
negative.
Status epilepticus is defined as continuous seizure activity for more than 5 minutes or two seizures without
recovery of consciousness.
Benzodiazepines are the first-line treatment for status epilepticus: lorazepam 4 mg IV, diazepam 10 mg IV,
or midazolam 10 mg IM.
If seizures persist after benzodiazepines, second-line agents include levetiracetam, valproate, or
phenytoin/fosphenytoin IV.
Refractory status epilepticus requires intubation and continuous infusion of midazolam, propofol, or
barbiturates.
Hypoglycemia is the most easily reversible cause of altered consciousness and should be checked
immediately in any patient with confusion or coma.
Dextrose 50% 50 mL IV is given for confirmed hypoglycemia; thiamine 100 mg IV should precede glucose
in malnourished or alcoholic patients to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy.
Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state is characterized by blood glucose above 600 mg/dL, osmolality above
320 mOsm/kg, and minimal ketosis.
Diabetic ketoacidosis is treated with IV fluid resuscitation, insulin infusion starting at 0.1 units/kg/hour, and
electrolyte replacement especially potassium.
Insulin should not be started in DKA until potassium is confirmed to be above 3.5 mEq/L to avoid
precipitating fatal hypokalemia.
Bicarbonate administration in DKA is controversial and generally not recommended unless pH is below
6.9.
In sepsis, the Surviving Sepsis Campaign recommends administering 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid within 3
hours and broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour.
Blood cultures should be drawn before antibiotic administration in sepsis, but antibiotics must not be
delayed by more than 45 minutes to obtain cultures.
Vasopressors are initiated in septic shock when MAP remains below 65 mmHg despite adequate fluid
resuscitation; norepinephrine is the first-choice vasopressor.
Vasopressin 0.03 units/min can be added to norepinephrine in refractory septic shock to reduce
norepinephrine requirements.
Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day continuous infusion or 50 mg every 6 hours is considered in septic shock not
responding to fluid and vasopressor therapy.
Lactate level above 2 mmol/L indicates tissue hypoperfusion in sepsis even without overt hypotension
(cryptic shock).
The Quick SOFA score identifies sepsis risk in non-ICU settings using respiratory rate above 22, altered
mentation, and systolic BP below 100 mmHg.
Meningitis is treated empirically with ceftriaxone 2 g IV every 12 hours plus dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV
every 6 hours to reduce inflammation.
In suspected bacterial meningitis, antibiotics and dexamethasone should be given before CT scan if LP is
anticipated to be delayed.
CSF findings in bacterial meningitis include elevated WBC (predominantly neutrophils), elevated protein,
and decreased glucose.
Lumbar puncture is contraindicated if there is evidence of raised intracranial pressure, papilledema, focal
neurological deficits, or coagulopathy.
Herpes simplex encephalitis is treated empirically with acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV every 8 hours while awaiting
PCR confirmation.
Necrotizing fasciitis requires immediate surgical debridement as the cornerstone of treatment; antibiotics
alone are insufficient.
Empiric antibiotics for necrotizing fasciitis include piperacillin-tazobactam plus clindamycin and
vancomycin for MRSA coverage.
In cellulitis without systemic features, oral antibiotics targeting streptococci are adequate; IV antibiotics are
reserved for systemic illness, immunocompromise, or failed oral therapy.
Fournier's gangrene is a necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum and genitalia requiring aggressive surgical
debridement and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
In acute appendicitis, the Alvarado score stratifies patients into low, intermediate, and high risk to guide
imaging and surgical referral.
CT abdomen with IV contrast is the preferred imaging for acute appendicitis in adults; ultrasound is
preferred in children and pregnant women.
Perforated appendicitis requires IV antibiotics and surgical consultation for appendectomy; uncomplicated
appendicitis may be managed with antibiotics alone in selected patients.
Acute cholangitis is characterized by Charcot's triad: fever, jaundice, and right upper quadrant pain;
Reynold's pentad adds confusion and hypotension in severe cases.
Urgent ERCP is the treatment of choice for acute cholangitis to relieve biliary obstruction combined with IV
antibiotics.
Acute pancreatitis is managed with IV fluid resuscitation, analgesia, and bowel rest; enteral nutrition is
preferred over parenteral when tolerated.
Ranson's criteria and the APACHE II score assess pancreatitis severity; CT with contrast (CECT) identifies
necrotizing pancreatitis.
Paralytic ileus and bowel obstruction are differentiated clinically and radiologically; small bowel obstruction
may require nasogastric decompression and surgical evaluation.
In upper GI bleeding, resuscitation with IV fluids and blood products is initiated simultaneously with
endoscopy preparation.
Proton pump inhibitor infusion (omeprazole or pantoprazole) is started empirically in suspected peptic
ulcer bleeding to reduce rebleeding risk before endoscopy.
Variceal bleeding is treated with terlipressin or octreotide to reduce portal pressure, combined with urgent
endoscopic band ligation.
Sengstaken-Blakemore tube is a rescue measure for uncontrolled variceal bleeding as a bridge to
definitive treatment.
Antibiotic prophylaxis with norfloxacin or ceftriaxone is given in variceal bleeding to prevent spontaneous
bacterial peritonitis.
Ectopic pregnancy presents with amenorrhea, vaginal bleeding, and unilateral pelvic pain; it is a
life-threatening emergency requiring urgent evaluation.
Serum beta-hCG and transvaginal ultrasound are the key diagnostic tools in suspected ectopic pregnancy.
Ruptured ectopic pregnancy causes hemoperitoneum and hemodynamic instability requiring emergency
surgical intervention.
Methotrexate is used for medical management of unruptured ectopic pregnancy with beta-hCG below
5000 IU/L and no cardiac activity on ultrasound.
In placenta previa, transvaginal ultrasound is contraindicated and digital examination must be avoided;
stabilize and arrange caesarean delivery.
Eclampsia is managed with IV magnesium sulfate 4-6 g loading dose followed by 1-2 g/hour infusion to
prevent recurrent seizures.
Severe hypertension in pregnancy (BP above 160/110 mmHg) is treated with labetalol, hydralazine, or
nifedipine to prevent stroke.
In placental abruption with fetal distress and maternal hemodynamic instability, emergency caesarean
delivery is indicated.
Ovarian torsion presents with sudden-onset severe unilateral pelvic pain and requires urgent surgical
detorsion to preserve ovarian function.
Testicular torsion is a urological emergency presenting with sudden scrotal pain; detorsion must occur
within 6 hours for best testicular salvage rates.
Manual detorsion of the testis can be attempted in the emergency department while arranging urgent
surgical exploration.
Urinary tract infection with systemic signs (pyelonephritis or urosepsis) requires IV antibiotics and hospital
admission; fluoroquinolones or aminoglycosides are commonly used.
Uncomplicated lower UTI in women is treated with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, nitrofurantoin, or
fosfomycin as first-line oral agents.
Renal colic is managed with NSAIDs as first-line analgesia; opioids are added if pain is inadequately
controlled.
Alpha-blockers such as tamsulosin facilitate spontaneous passage of ureteral stones smaller than 10 mm.
Urinary retention is relieved by urethral catheterization; suprapubic catheterization is used when urethral
catheterization fails.
In acute kidney injury, nephrotoxic agents are discontinued, fluid status is optimized, and obstructive
causes are excluded by renal ultrasound.
Indications for emergency hemodialysis include refractory hyperkalemia, severe acidosis, pulmonary
edema unresponsive to diuretics, and uremic pericarditis.
Hyperkalemia with ECG changes requires immediate calcium gluconate 10 mL of 10% IV to stabilize the
cardiac membrane.
Sodium bicarbonate, insulin with dextrose, salbutamol nebulization, and sodium polystyrene sulfonate are
used to lower potassium levels in hyperkalemia.
Hyponatremia with neurological symptoms requires hypertonic saline 3% administered cautiously to raise
sodium by no more than 1-2 mEq/L per hour.
Overly rapid correction of hyponatremia risks osmotic demyelination syndrome; correction rate should not
exceed 10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours.
Hypercalcemia above 3.5 mmol/L is treated with IV saline hydration followed by bisphosphonates;
calcitonin provides a rapid but transient effect.
Addisonian crisis presents with hypotension, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and hypoglycemia in a patient
with adrenal insufficiency; hydrocortisone 100 mg IV is life-saving.
Thyroid storm is treated with propylthiouracil or methimazole to block synthesis, then iodine to block
release, plus propranolol and corticosteroids.
Myxedema coma is treated with IV levothyroxine, corticosteroids, passive rewarming, and supportive care.
Hypothermia below 30°C is associated with cardiac arrhythmias; patients are warmed by active external
and internal methods.
In severe hypothermia with cardiac arrest, ECMO rewarming is the preferred method when available.
Frostbite is rewarmed in water at 37-40°C after ensuring no risk of refreezing; avoid rubbing the affected
area.
Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency characterized by core temperature above 40°C and
neurological dysfunction; immediate rapid cooling is essential.
Evaporative cooling and cold water immersion are the most effective methods for rapidly reducing core
temperature in heat stroke.
Drowning victims require airway management, ventilatory support, CPR if indicated, and evaluation for
hypothermia and aspiration pneumonia.
In near-drowning, chest X-ray may underestimate pulmonary injury; all patients require observation for at
least 6-8 hours.
High-altitude cerebral edema is treated with immediate descent, supplemental oxygen, dexamethasone 8
mg IM, and portable hyperbaric therapy.
Decompression sickness is treated with 100% oxygen and urgent transfer to a hyperbaric oxygen facility.
Carbon monoxide poisoning is treated with 100% high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask; hyperbaric
oxygen is indicated in severe cases.
COHb levels do not reliably correlate with clinical severity in carbon monoxide poisoning; symptoms guide
management.
Cyanide poisoning is treated with hydroxocobalamin 5 g IV as the first-line antidote; sodium thiosulfate is
an alternative.
Organophosphate poisoning causes a cholinergic toxidrome; atropine in large doses and pralidoxime are
the specific antidotes.
The mnemonic SLUDGE describes cholinergic toxidrome: salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, GI
upset, and emesis.
Benzodiazepine overdose is reversed with flumazenil, but it must be used cautiously as it can precipitate
seizures in dependent patients.
Opioid overdose presents with the toxidrome of miosis, respiratory depression, and altered consciousness;
naloxone 0.4-2 mg IV/IM is the antidote.
In paracetamol overdose, N-acetylcysteine is started within 8 hours of ingestion based on the
Rumack-Matthew nomogram for optimal hepatoprotection.
Activated charcoal 1 g/kg is given within 1-2 hours of toxic ingestion if the airway is protected and the
substance is charcoal-adsorbable.
Gastric lavage is rarely indicated and only considered within 1 hour of a life-threatening ingestion when
activated charcoal is not appropriate.
Tricyclic antidepressant overdose causes QRS widening and hemodynamic instability; sodium bicarbonate
1-2 mEq/kg IV is the treatment.
Salicylate poisoning causes a mixed metabolic acidosis and respiratory alkalosis; urinary alkalinization
with sodium bicarbonate enhances elimination.
In beta-blocker overdose, high-dose insulin euglycemia therapy (HIET) with insulin at 1 unit/kg/hour is an
effective treatment in severe cases.
Calcium channel blocker toxicity is managed with IV calcium, high-dose insulin, vasopressors, and lipid
emulsion therapy in severe cases.
Intravenous lipid emulsion (Intralipid 20%) is used as rescue therapy in local anesthetic systemic toxicity
causing cardiovascular collapse.
Digoxin toxicity is treated with digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind or DigiFab) in life-threatening
arrhythmias or severe hyperkalemia.
Methanol and ethylene glycol poisoning are treated with fomepizole (preferred) or ethanol to inhibit alcohol
dehydrogenase, plus hemodialysis.
Isoniazid overdose causes seizures refractory to benzodiazepines; pyridoxine (vitamin B6) gram-for-gram
equivalent to INH ingested is the antidote.
In acute angle-closure glaucoma presenting with eye pain, nausea, and blurred vision, topical
beta-blockers, pilocarpine, and acetazolamide are used emergently.
Corneal foreign bodies are removed under slit-lamp magnification after topical anesthesia; rust rings
require ophthalmology follow-up.
Chemical eye injuries require immediate and copious irrigation with normal saline for at least 30 minutes
and ophthalmology consultation.
Central retinal artery occlusion is an ocular emergency presenting with sudden painless monocular vision
loss; treatment includes immediate lowering of intraocular pressure.
Retinal detachment presents with floaters, flashing lights, and a curtain across the visual field; urgent
ophthalmology referral is required.
Epistaxis is controlled initially by applying direct pressure for 10-15 minutes with the patient leaning
forward; anterior epistaxis may require nasal packing.
Ludwig's angina is a bilateral submandibular space infection causing potential airway compromise; early
airway management and IV antibiotics are critical.
Peritonsillar abscess is drained by needle aspiration or incision and drainage; IV penicillin plus
metronidazole covers the polymicrobial flora.
Epiglottitis presents with drooling, dysphagia, and high fever; the patient should be kept calm, and airway
management prepared before any examination.
In suspected epiglottitis, a lateral neck X-ray showing the thumb sign confirms the diagnosis, but imaging
should not delay airway management.
Croup is treated with humidified air or oxygen, a single dose of oral or IM dexamethasone, and nebulized
adrenaline for moderate-to-severe cases.
Bronchiolitis in infants is managed supportively with oxygen supplementation, nasal suctioning, and
hydration; no role for bronchodilators or steroids.
Febrile seizures are generally benign and self-limiting; antipyretics are given for comfort but do not prevent
recurrence.
In pediatric sepsis, the Pediatric Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (pSOFA) score and SIRS criteria
guide diagnosis and management.
Fluid boluses in pediatric septic shock are 10-20 mL/kg of isotonic crystalloid; aggressive fluid
resuscitation is titrated to hemodynamic response.
Intussusception in children presents with colicky abdominal pain, red-currant jelly stool, and a palpable
sausage-shaped mass; pneumatic reduction is first-line.
Pyloric stenosis presents in neonates at 3-6 weeks with projectile non-bilious vomiting; ultrasound shows
an elongated thickened pyloric channel.
Mesenteric ischemia presents with severe abdominal pain out of proportion to physical examination
findings; CT angiography is the diagnostic study of choice.
Volvulus of the sigmoid colon presents with abdominal distension and constipation; X-ray shows a
coffee-bean sign; flexible endoscopy is first-line treatment.
Acute limb ischemia presents with the 6 Ps: pain, pallor, pulselessness, paresthesias, paralysis, and
poikilothermia.
In acute arterial occlusion, IV heparin is started immediately, and emergent embolectomy or thrombolysis
is arranged.
Compartment syndrome is diagnosed clinically by pain with passive stretch and firmness of the
compartment; fasciotomy is required when compartment pressure exceeds 30 mmHg.
Crush syndrome causes rhabdomyolysis and myoglobinuria; treatment includes aggressive IV fluid
resuscitation with normal saline to achieve urine output of 200-300 mL/hour.
Sodium bicarbonate may be added to IV fluids in rhabdomyolysis to alkalinize urine and prevent myoglobin
precipitation in renal tubules.
In traumatic hemorrhage, damage control resuscitation uses a 1:1:1 ratio of packed red blood cells to fresh
frozen plasma to platelets.
Massive transfusion protocol is activated when more than 10 units of packed red blood cells are expected
within 24 hours.
Tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 minutes is given as early as possible (within 3 hours) of traumatic
hemorrhage to reduce mortality.
In tension pneumothorax with hemodynamic compromise, needle thoracostomy is performed immediately
without waiting for imaging.
The Glasgow Coma Scale assesses eye opening, verbal response, and motor response, with a score of 8
or below indicating need for airway protection.
FAST ultrasound examination (Focused Assessment with Sonography in Trauma) evaluates for free fluid
in Morrison's pouch, splenorenal space, pelvis, and pericardium.
Extended FAST (eFAST) includes bilateral thoracic windows to detect pneumothorax and hemothorax.
In penetrating abdominal trauma with hemodynamic instability, immediate operative exploration is
indicated without delay for imaging.
Blunt splenic injury is graded I-V on CT; grades I-III in stable patients may be managed non-operatively
with serial imaging.
Liver lacerations causing ongoing hemorrhage are managed with angioembolization or operative packing
and repair.
Pelvic fractures with hemodynamic instability may benefit from pelvic binder placement, pre-peritoneal
packing, or angioembolization.
Traumatic brain injury management focuses on preventing secondary injury by maintaining MAP above 80
mmHg, SpO2 above 95%, and normocapnia.
Hyperosmolar therapy with mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg IV or hypertonic saline 3% is used for raised ICP causing
herniation.
In spinal cord injury, methylprednisolone is no longer routinely recommended due to insufficient evidence
of benefit and significant side effects.
Immobilization of the cervical spine is maintained until injury is excluded clinically using the NEXUS criteria
or Canadian C-Spine Rule.
Burns are classified by depth: superficial (first degree), partial thickness (second degree), and full
thickness (third degree).
The rule of nines estimates burn surface area: head and neck 9%, each arm 9%, each leg 18%, anterior
trunk 18%, posterior trunk 18%, and perineum 1%.
Fluid resuscitation in burns uses the Parkland formula: 4 mL/kg per percentage TBSA burned of lactated
Ringer's solution over 24 hours, with half given in the first 8 hours.
Early escharotomy is required for circumferential full-thickness burns of the chest or extremities causing
respiratory compromise or compartment syndrome.
Inhalation injury is suspected with burns in an enclosed space, singed nasal hair, carbonaceous sputum,
and stridor; early intubation is life-saving.
Electrical burns may cause cardiac arrhythmias, rhabdomyolysis, and internal injuries disproportionate to
surface burns; cardiac monitoring and aggressive fluid resuscitation are essential.
In drowning resuscitation, CPR is performed for at least 30 minutes and should not be abandoned early
due to the potential for neurological recovery in hypothermic victims.
Traumatic cardiac arrest has reversible causes that must be addressed simultaneously: hemorrhage
control, tension pneumothorax decompression, and pericardiocentesis for tamponade.
Resuscitative thoracotomy is indicated in penetrating trauma with witnessed cardiac arrest or loss of vital
signs within 15 minutes of arrival.
In snake envenomation, antivenom is the definitive treatment; wound incision, tourniquet, and suction
devices are harmful and contraindicated.
Black widow spider envenomation causes severe muscle cramps and spasms; treatment includes opioids,
benzodiazepines, and antivenom in severe cases.
Brown recluse spider bite causes progressive necrotic lesion; wound care and surgery may be needed for
severe necrosis.
Scorpion envenomation in children can cause sympathomimetic crisis requiring prazosin and IV antivenom
in severe cases.
Jellyfish stings are treated by removing tentacles without touching them bare-handed, rinsing with sea
water (not fresh water), and applying hot water immersion for pain relief.
Bee and wasp stings causing anaphylaxis require epinephrine; the stinger should be removed by scraping
rather than squeezing.
Rabies post-exposure prophylaxis includes wound cleaning, rabies immunoglobulin infiltrated at the wound
site, and active vaccination on days 0, 3, 7, and 14.
Tetanus prophylaxis is given based on wound type and immunization history; tetanus immunoglobulin is
added for high-risk wounds in inadequately vaccinated patients.
Mammalian bite wounds are copiously irrigated; prophylactic antibiotics with amoxicillin-clavulanate are
given for high-risk wounds such as hand bites and cat bites.
Human bite wounds are considered high-risk for infection with Eikenella corrodens and require antibiotic
prophylaxis and close wound surveillance.
In traumatic dental avulsion, the tooth is replanted as quickly as possible after rinsing with saline; storage
in milk or saliva preserves viability during transport.
Septic arthritis of a native joint requires urgent joint aspiration for Gram stain and culture, followed by IV
antibiotics and orthopedic consultation for joint washout.
Crystal arthropathies (gout and pseudogout) are treated with colchicine, NSAIDs, or corticosteroids in
acute flares; urate-lowering therapy is not started acutely.
Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis in cirrhosis is diagnosed by ascitic fluid PMN count above 250 cells/mm3
and treated with IV cefotaxime for 5 days.
Hepatic encephalopathy is treated with lactulose to reduce gut ammonia production and rifaximin for
prevention of recurrence.
Acute liver failure with encephalopathy and coagulopathy requires early contact with a liver transplant
center and N-acetylcysteine IV regardless of etiology.
In upper airway obstruction, back blows and abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver) are performed for a
conscious choking victim.
For an unconscious choking victim or failed Heimlich maneuver, direct laryngoscopy and Magill forceps
removal of the foreign body is attempted.
Wheezing in a child with sudden onset and no fever may indicate foreign body aspiration; rigid
bronchoscopy under general anesthesia is the treatment.
In severe head injury with decerebrate posturing, hyperventilation to PaCO2 35-40 mmHg can temporarily
reduce ICP as a bridge to neurosurgery.
Lumbar puncture in suspected LP in elderly patients must account for normal CSF pressure changes with
aging; cell counts and protein are more diagnostically relevant.
Cerebellar stroke presents with sudden ataxia, nausea, vomiting, and inability to walk; the absence of
hemiparesis can lead to diagnostic delay.
The HINTS exam (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) distinguishes central from peripheral vertigo
in the emergency setting with high sensitivity.
Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo is treated with canalith repositioning maneuvers such as the Epley
maneuver.
Ménière's disease presents with episodic vertigo, tinnitus, and low-frequency hearing loss; acute attacks
are treated with vestibular suppressants.
Bell's palsy is a peripheral facial nerve palsy treated with oral corticosteroids started within 72 hours of
onset; antivirals may be added for moderate-to-severe cases.
Temporal arteritis presents with headache, jaw claudication, and visual symptoms in patients over 50;
ESR and CRP are elevated; high-dose steroids are started immediately to prevent blindness.
In suspected temporal arteritis with visual symptoms, corticosteroids are started before biopsy results to
prevent irreversible vision loss.
Cauda equina syndrome presents with saddle anesthesia, urinary retention, and lower limb weakness;
emergency MRI and neurosurgical consultation are required.
Guillain-Barré syndrome presents with ascending weakness and areflexia; respiratory function must be
closely monitored for neuromuscular respiratory failure.
In Guillain-Barré syndrome, plasmapheresis or IV immunoglobulin hastens recovery; respiratory failure
requires mechanical ventilation.
Myasthenic crisis presents with respiratory failure requiring ventilatory support; plasmapheresis or IVIg is
used to manage the crisis.
In rhabdomyolysis, urine dipstick tests positive for blood in the absence of red blood cells on microscopy
due to myoglobinuria.
Sickle cell vaso-occlusive crisis is treated with IV opioids for pain, IV fluids, oxygen supplementation, and
assessment for precipitating infection.
Acute chest syndrome in sickle cell disease is treated with oxygen, IV fluids, bronchodilators, antibiotics,
and exchange transfusion in severe cases.
Priapism in sickle cell disease is managed with aspiration, intracavernosal phenylephrine, IV hydration,
and hematology consultation.
Hemolytic uremic syndrome in children presents with microangiopathic hemolytic anemia,
thrombocytopenia, and acute renal failure following E. coli O157:H7 infection.
Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura is a hematological emergency presenting with thrombocytopenia,
microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, fever, renal dysfunction, and neurological changes.
Plasma exchange is the treatment of choice for TTP and should be started emergently upon diagnosis.
Disseminated intravascular coagulation is treated by addressing the underlying cause; supportive care
includes FFP, cryoprecipitate, and platelet transfusion.
In hemophilia with major bleeding, factor replacement therapy is given immediately; factor VIII for
hemophilia A and factor IX for hemophilia B.
Warfarin-associated bleeding is reversed with 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) for
life-threatening hemorrhage, plus vitamin K IV.
Direct oral anticoagulant reversal in life-threatening bleeding uses andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors
and idarucizumab for dabigatran.
Heparin overdose is reversed with protamine sulfate: 1 mg per 100 units of UFH administered in the last 2
hours.
In acute hemolytic transfusion reaction, the transfusion is stopped immediately, IV saline is infused to
maintain urine output, and samples are sent for re-crossmatching.
Anaphylactic transfusion reaction is treated with epinephrine, antihistamines, and corticosteroids; the
transfusion is stopped.
Transfusion-related acute lung injury presents with non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema within 6 hours of
transfusion; treatment is supportive with mechanical ventilation if needed.
In patients with COVID-19 and hypoxemia, prone positioning in awake non-intubated patients (awake
proning) can improve oxygenation.
High-flow nasal oxygen therapy delivers up to 60 L/min of humidified oxygen and reduces the need for
intubation in moderate hypoxic respiratory failure.
Low tidal volume ventilation 6 mL/kg ideal body weight reduces ventilator-induced lung injury in ARDS.
Permissive hypercapnia is accepted in ARDS lung-protective ventilation strategies to minimize volutrauma.
In ARDS, prone positioning for at least 16 hours per day reduces mortality in severe cases (PaO2/FiO2
below 150).
Driving pressure (plateau pressure minus PEEP) below 15 cmH2O is associated with improved outcomes
in mechanically ventilated ARDS patients.
Ventilator-associated pneumonia prevention includes head-of-bed elevation to 30-45 degrees, oral care
with chlorhexidine, and daily sedation vacations.
In acute severe hypertension (hypertensive emergency), end-organ damage defines the emergency; blood
pressure is reduced by no more than 25% in the first hour.
Labetalol, nicardipine, and clevidipine are preferred IV agents for hypertensive emergency in most clinical
settings.
Hypertensive encephalopathy presents with headache, confusion, and visual disturbances; MRI may show
posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES).
In aortic dissection, both systolic BP and heart rate must be controlled; heart rate target is below 60 bpm
and BP below 120 mmHg systolic.
Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular rate is rate-controlled with IV metoprolol, diltiazem, or digoxin;
anticoagulation is started based on stroke risk.
Unstable atrial fibrillation causing hemodynamic compromise is managed with synchronized DC
cardioversion starting at 120-200 J biphasic.
SVT is terminated with vagal maneuvers first; if ineffective, adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push is given followed
by 12 mg if no response.
Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome with AF is a dangerous arrhythmia; AV nodal blocking agents
(adenosine, verapamil, digoxin) are contraindicated as they may accelerate conduction via the accessory
pathway.
Complete heart block with hemodynamic compromise is treated with transcutaneous pacing as a bridge to
transvenous pacing.
Atropine 1 mg IV is given for symptomatic bradycardia; it is ineffective in complete heart block and
infranodal bradycardias.
Long QT syndrome predisposes to torsades de pointes; magnesium sulfate 2 g IV is the treatment, and
QT-prolonging medications are discontinued.
Polymorphic VT (torsades de pointes) is treated with IV magnesium, overdrive pacing, and correction of
electrolyte abnormalities.
In acute pericarditis, NSAIDs plus colchicine is the standard first-line treatment; corticosteroids are
reserved for refractory cases.
Constrictive pericarditis presents with features of right heart failure and is distinguished from tamponade
by pulsus paradoxus and equalization of filling pressures.
Massive pulmonary embolism with obstructive shock may require extracorporeal membrane oxygenation
(ECMO) as a bridge to surgical embolectomy.
The Pulmonary Embolism Severity Index (PESI) stratifies PE severity and guides outpatient vs inpatient
management decisions.
Low-risk PE with low PESI score can be managed as an outpatient with rivaroxaban or apixaban without
hospitalization.
DVT of the lower extremity is treated with anticoagulation; below-knee isolated DVT may be managed with
repeat ultrasound surveillance in low-risk patients.
Phlegmasia cerulea dolens is a severe form of DVT with massive clot burden causing venous gangrene;
systemic thrombolysis or catheter-directed thrombolysis is considered.
In acute mesenteric ischemia due to arterial occlusion, systemic heparinization is started and urgent
angiography with potential thrombolysis or embolectomy is performed.
Femoral neck fractures in elderly patients require urgent orthopedic consultation; early surgical fixation or
arthroplasty reduces morbidity and mortality.
Fat embolism syndrome presents with respiratory failure, petechiae over the chest and axilla, and
neurological symptoms days after a long bone fracture.
Open fractures require antibiotics (usually cefazolin) within 3 hours and urgent orthopedic washout and
debridement in the operating room.
Scaphoid fractures with clinical tenderness require immobilization even if initial X-ray is normal; MRI or CT
is performed to confirm occult fractures.
Posterior shoulder dislocation is frequently missed; axillary or Y-view X-ray reveals the lightbulb sign;
reduction requires adequate analgesia and sedation.
Hip dislocation causes avascular necrosis risk that increases with time; reduction should occur within 6
hours of dislocation.
Procedural sedation in the emergency department uses propofol, ketamine, or fentanyl-midazolam
depending on the procedure and patient's hemodynamic status.
Ketamine-induced emergence reactions are reduced by administering low-dose midazolam 1-2 mg IV
before or with ketamine.
Laryngospasm during procedural sedation is managed with jaw thrust, positive pressure ventilation, and
succinylcholine 0.1-0.5 mg/kg IV if complete.
Point-of-care ultrasound in the emergency department guides procedures, confirms diagnoses, and
improves safety of thoracentesis, paracentesis, and vascular access.
Intraosseous access is established in any patient when IV access cannot be obtained; common sites
include the proximal tibia, distal femur, and humeral head.
Central venous access provides large bore access, allows pressure monitoring, and is required for
vasopressor infusion; internal jugular and subclavian routes are common.
Arterial line insertion provides continuous blood pressure monitoring and facilitates frequent blood
sampling in critically ill patients.
Lumbar puncture is performed at the L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace with the patient in the lateral decubitus or
sitting position.
Post-lumbar puncture headache is treated conservatively with analgesia and hydration; epidural blood
patch is used for refractory cases.
Paracentesis in tense ascites provides symptomatic relief and diagnostic information; large-volume
paracentesis requires albumin replacement at 8 g per liter removed.
Thoracentesis relieves symptomatic pleural effusion; post-procedure chest X-ray checks for
pneumothorax.
Pericardiocentesis is ideally performed under continuous echocardiographic guidance to confirm needle
position in the pericardial space.
In the emergency management of agitation, verbal de-escalation is attempted first; pharmacological
management uses haloperidol, benzodiazepines, or droperidol.
Droperidol 10 mg IM is highly effective for acute agitation and has a rapid onset; it requires ECG
monitoring for QT prolongation.
Excited delirium is a life-threatening emergency presenting with hyperthermia, extreme agitation, and
superhuman strength; rapid sedation and cooling are priorities.
In suspected drug-facilitated assault, urine and serum toxicology screens should be collected promptly as
many substances have short detection windows.
Non-accidental injury in children is suspected when the history is inconsistent with injuries, there are
bruises in unusual locations, or there are multiple injuries at various stages of healing.
Intimate partner violence requires a private, non-judgmental assessment; safety planning and referral to
social services are essential parts of emergency care.
Acute psychiatric emergencies require risk assessment for suicide and homicide; voluntary or involuntary
admission is arranged based on level of risk.
Serotonin syndrome presents with the triad of mental status changes, neuromuscular abnormalities, and
autonomic instability; cyproheptadine and benzodiazepines are used in treatment.
Neuroleptic malignant syndrome is characterized by fever, rigidity, altered consciousness, and autonomic
instability in patients on antipsychotics; dantrolene and bromocriptine are used.
In alcohol withdrawal, benzodiazepines are titrated to symptom severity using the CIWA-Ar scale;
phenobarbital and propofol are used in refractory cases.
Delirium tremens occurs 48-96 hours after alcohol cessation and carries a mortality rate of up to 5%;
intensive care with IV benzodiazepines is required.
Wernicke's encephalopathy presents with confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia; IV thiamine 500 mg
three times daily is the treatment.
In acute pain management, a stepwise approach based on the WHO analgesic ladder is used, starting
with non-opioids and escalating to strong opioids.
Regional nerve blocks such as femoral nerve block for hip fractures, fascia iliaca block, and intercostal
nerve blocks provide effective opioid-sparing analgesia.
Procedural pain in children is managed with intranasal fentanyl 1.5 mcg/kg, oral sucrose for infants, and
topical anesthetics for venipuncture.
In acute corneal abrasion, topical NSAIDs provide analgesia; topical anesthetics are used diagnostically
but not for ongoing pain management due to toxicity.
Wound closure in the emergency department uses primary closure for clean wounds within 6-12 hours;
contaminated or delayed wounds are managed with delayed primary closure.
Scalp lacerations bleed profusely due to high vascularity; firm pressure and rapid closure with staples or
sutures controls hemorrhage effectively.
Facial lacerations are closed with fine monofilament sutures for cosmetic reasons; sutures in the face are
removed at 3-5 days.
Hand lacerations involving tendons, nerves, or vessels require surgical consultation; flexor tendon injuries
require specialist repair.
Wound irrigation with normal saline under pressure is the single most important step in preventing infection
in traumatic lacerations.
Antibiotic prophylaxis is not routinely required for simple clean lacerations but is given for bite wounds,
contaminated wounds, and wounds in immunocompromised patients.
Retained foreign bodies in soft tissue are detected with plain X-ray (radiopaque), ultrasound (most soft
tissue material), or MRI (non-metallic objects).
Subungual hematoma occupying more than 50% of the nail bed is drained by nail trephination using a
heated paperclip or electrocautery device.
Fingertip amputations are managed with moist dressing and healing by secondary intention for minor
amputations; replantation is considered for larger injuries.
Dislocated fingers are reduced using digital nerve block or procedural sedation followed by longitudinal
traction and reduction maneuver.
Tibial plateau fractures with hemarthrosis may require aspiration for pain relief; CT defines the fracture
pattern and guides surgical planning.
In ankle fractures, the Ottawa Ankle Rules guide X-ray decision-making to reduce unnecessary imaging
while maintaining sensitivity for fractures.
Calcaneal fractures from fall from height are associated with lumbar spine fractures; spinal imaging is
performed in all patients with calcaneal fractures.
Achilles tendon rupture is diagnosed clinically with the Thompson test; ultrasound or MRI confirms the
diagnosis.
Plantar fasciitis is managed conservatively with stretching, NSAIDs, and orthotics; corticosteroid injection
is offered for refractory cases.
In septic shock, early goal-directed therapy principles guide resuscitation targeting MAP above 65 mmHg,
urine output above 0.5 mL/kg/hour, and normalization of lactate.
Procalcitonin is used as a biomarker to guide antibiotic initiation and de-escalation in sepsis and
respiratory infections.
In neutropenic fever, broad-spectrum antibiotics covering gram-negative organisms including
Pseudomonas are started within 1 hour of presentation.
Cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam is first-line antibiotic therapy in febrile neutropenia; vancomycin is
added for suspected gram-positive infection.
Malaria should be considered in any febrile patient returning from an endemic area; thick and thin blood
films plus rapid antigen testing confirm diagnosis.
Severe malaria with cerebral involvement or hemodynamic compromise is treated with IV artesunate as
the drug of choice.
Dengue fever with warning signs (abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, rapid
respiratory rate, progressive thrombocytopenia) requires hospitalization.
In Ebola virus disease, strict contact and droplet precautions with full PPE are essential; supportive care
including IV fluids and electrolyte management is the mainstay.
In suspected cholera with severe dehydration, oral rehydration solution is used for mild cases and IV
Ringer's lactate for severe cases; doxycycline reduces stool output.
Clostridium difficile colitis is treated with oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin; metronidazole is reserved for mild
cases where these are unavailable.
Toxic megacolon complicating Clostridium difficile requires IV fluids, IV vancomycin plus metronidazole,
surgical consultation, and consideration of fecal microbiota transplant.
In acute liver failure, management includes treatment of precipitating cause, N-acetylcysteine, lactulose for
hepatic encephalopathy, and early transplant evaluation.
Emergency ultrasound can identify pneumothorax by absence of lung sliding on B-mode and absence of
seashore sign on M-mode.
Free fluid in the abdomen on FAST ultrasound in a trauma patient indicates intraperitoneal hemorrhage
until proven otherwise.
Pericardial effusion on ultrasound is identified as an anechoic space around the heart; right ventricular
diastolic collapse indicates tamponade physiology.
Inferior vena cava diameter and collapsibility index on ultrasound estimate volume status and fluid
responsiveness in critically ill patients.
Bedside echocardiography in cardiac arrest identifies potentially reversible causes such as pericardial
tamponade, massive PE, and severe hypovolemia.
In smoke inhalation injury, carboxyhemoglobin above 25% or CNS symptoms are indications for
hyperbaric oxygen therapy.
Chemical burns from alkali substances cause liquefactive necrosis that penetrates deeply; copious
irrigation for at least 60 minutes is required.
Acid burns cause coagulative necrosis that creates a barrier limiting deep penetration; irrigation is still
required for 30-60 minutes.
Hydrofluoric acid burns can cause life-threatening hypocalcemia; calcium gluconate gel is applied topically
and IV calcium is given for systemic toxicity.
In lightning strike victims, multiple patients may be involved; uniquely, those in cardiac arrest are prioritized
first as they have the highest chance of survival with CPR.
Drowning in cold water offers a neuroprotective effect; resuscitation should be continued aggressively
even after prolonged submersion if the water was cold.
In altitude sickness, descent by 300-1000 m is the most effective treatment; acetazolamide 250 mg twice
daily aids acclimatization and treatment.
High-altitude pulmonary edema is treated with immediate descent, supplemental oxygen, and nifedipine
30 mg extended release for pulmonary vasodilation.
Barotrauma during mechanical ventilation manifests as pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum, or
subcutaneous emphysema and requires immediate recognition.
In procedural sedation, capnography monitoring of end-tidal CO2 provides early warning of apnea before
clinical desaturation occurs.
Pre-oxygenation before RSI uses high-flow oxygen for at least 3 minutes or 8 vital capacity breaths to
extend safe apnea time.
Apneic oxygenation with nasal cannula at 15 L/min during laryngoscopy maintains oxygenation and
extends safe apnea duration.
Cricoid pressure (Sellick's maneuver) during RSI is controversial; it may worsen laryngoscopic view and its
benefit in preventing aspiration is disputed.
Video laryngoscopy improves first-pass intubation success rates compared to direct laryngoscopy and is
recommended as first-line in difficult airway algorithms.
The bougie (tracheal introducer) improves intubation success in difficult airway scenarios and should be
readily available during all intubation attempts.
Confirmation of endotracheal tube placement requires waveform capnography; equal bilateral breath
sounds and symmetric chest rise are secondary confirmation methods.
In-line stabilization of the cervical spine is maintained during airway management in trauma patients;
manual in-line stabilization (MILS) reduces movement during intubation.
Nasotracheal intubation is an option in spontaneously breathing patients with difficult oral access; it is
contraindicated in apneic patients and suspected basilar skull fractures.
Needle cricothyrotomy provides emergency oxygenation for 30-45 minutes and is a temporary measure;
surgical cricothyrotomy provides definitive airway access.
The DOPE mnemonic helps identify causes of acute deterioration in intubated patients: displacement,
obstruction, pneumothorax, and equipment failure.
Auto-PEEP (intrinsic PEEP) in ventilated asthmatic patients is managed by reducing respiratory rate,
increasing expiratory time, and briefly disconnecting the ventilator.
In abdominal compartment syndrome, bladder pressure measurement above 20 mmHg with new organ
dysfunction confirms the diagnosis; decompressive laparotomy is required.
Rectal bleeding with hemodynamic instability requires urgent endoscopy, interventional radiology, or
surgical consultation based on the likely bleeding source.
In lower GI bleeding, CT angiography identifies the bleeding source and guides treatment by embolization
or surgery.
Angiodysplasia is the most common cause of lower GI bleeding in elderly patients and is managed
endoscopically or by angioembolization.
Diverticular bleeding is the most common cause of massive lower GI hemorrhage overall; most episodes
stop spontaneously but recurrence is common.
Mesenteric adenitis in children mimics acute appendicitis; ultrasound or CT differentiates the two
conditions.
Incarcerated inguinal hernia requires immediate surgical consultation; manual reduction is attempted if
there is no evidence of strangulation.
Strangulated hernia with bowel compromise requires emergency surgery; delay results in bowel necrosis
and increased mortality.
Acute urinary retention in older men is most commonly due to benign prostatic hyperplasia; urethral
catheterization provides immediate relief.
Paraphimosis (foreskin trapped behind the glans) is a urological emergency requiring manual reduction
after applying ice to reduce edema; dorsal slit or circumcision may be needed.
Balanitis with systemic features or failure to respond to topical antifungals requires systemic azole therapy
and evaluation for diabetes or immunosuppression.
Priapism lasting more than 4 hours is a urological emergency; intracavernosal phenylephrine injection is
first-line treatment for ischemic priapism.
In acute scrotum, color Doppler ultrasound differentiates testicular torsion (absent flow) from
epididymo-orchitis (increased flow); negative predictive value is very high.
Epididymo-orchitis is treated with antibiotics targeting the likely causative organism: ceftriaxone plus
doxycycline for STI, or fluoroquinolone for older patients.
Urethral injuries in pelvic fracture are suspected if blood is present at the urethral meatus, scrotal
hematoma, or perineal bruising; urethral catheterization is deferred until imaging.
Urinary tract infection in men should prompt investigation for an underlying abnormality such as
urolithiasis, obstruction, or prostatitis.
Phimosis causing urinary obstruction requires dorsal slit or circumcision; urethral catheterization may be
necessary as a temporizing measure.
In acute pancreatitis, early oral or enteral feeding within 24-48 hours is preferred over bowel rest as it
reduces infectious complications and length of stay.
Abdominal aortic aneurysm rupture presents with sudden severe abdominal or back pain, hypotension,
and a pulsatile abdominal mass; emergency vascular surgery is required.
Symptomatic abdominal aortic aneurysm with diameter above 5.5 cm in men and 5.0 cm in women
requires urgent surgical repair before rupture.
Inferior mesenteric artery occlusion causes ischemic colitis presenting with left lower quadrant pain and
bloody diarrhea; colonoscopy confirms diagnosis.
Shock index (heart rate divided by systolic BP) above 1.0 indicates significant hemodynamic compromise
and predicts the need for massive transfusion in trauma.
In pediatric trauma, the Pediatric Trauma Score and Revised Trauma Score stratify injury severity and
guide triage decisions.
Traumatic aortic injury is most common at the aortic isthmus just distal to the left subclavian artery; CT
angiography is diagnostic.
Cardiac contusion is suspected in blunt chest trauma with sternal fracture; troponin and ECG monitoring
are performed for 24 hours.
Rib fractures in elderly patients cause significant morbidity due to respiratory splinting; regional analgesia
such as thoracic epidural or paravertebral block reduces pneumonia risk.
Flail chest is caused by three or more consecutive rib fractures in two or more places, creating a
paradoxically moving segment that impairs ventilation.
Pulmonary contusion on CT appears as parenchymal consolidation and ground-glass opacification; it
peaks at 24-72 hours after injury.
Tracheobronchial injury presents with massive air leak, persistent pneumothorax despite chest tube, and
subcutaneous emphysema; bronchoscopy confirms and localizes the injury.
Diaphragmatic rupture from blunt trauma is more common on the left side; chest X-ray may show bowel
loops in the thorax or nasogastric tube above the diaphragm.
Esophageal perforation presents with chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema, and Hamman's sign
(crunching sound with heartbeat); early surgical repair is essential.
Renal trauma is graded I-V; grades I-III are managed conservatively, while grade IV-V injuries may require
angioembolization or surgical repair.
Bladder rupture is classified as intraperitoneal (requires surgery) or extraperitoneal (managed with
catheter drainage); CT cystography is diagnostic.
Penile fracture from blunt trauma during intercourse causes immediate pain, swelling, and deviation;
surgical repair is required within 24 hours for best outcomes.
Auricular hematoma (cauliflower ear) must be drained promptly and a compression dressing applied to
prevent fibrosis and cartilage necrosis.
Nasal fracture with septal hematoma requires urgent drainage to prevent avascular necrosis of the nasal
septum and saddle nose deformity.
Orbital blow-out fracture may trap the inferior rectus muscle causing diplopia on upward gaze; urgent
ophthalmology and maxillofacial surgery consultation is required.
Mandibular fractures are assessed clinically by step deformity, trismus, and malocclusion; panoramic
X-ray (OPG) or CT confirms the fracture.
Penetrating neck injuries are classified by zone; zone II injuries with hard signs of vascular or
aerodigestive injury require immediate surgical exploration.
Laryngeal fracture presents with hoarse voice, stridor, subcutaneous emphysema, and tenderness; urgent
airway management and ENT consultation are required.
In acute spinal cord injury, methylprednisolone is not routinely recommended but early surgical
decompression within 24 hours improves neurological outcomes.
Autonomic dysreflexia in spinal cord injury above T6 causes life-threatening hypertension triggered by a
noxious stimulus below the injury level; the stimulus must be identified and removed.
In maxillofacial trauma with midface fractures, look for CSF rhinorrhea indicating anterior skull base
fracture; prophylactic antibiotics are controversial.
Traumatic optic neuropathy presents with visual loss after periorbital trauma without obvious external
injury; high-dose steroids or optic nerve decompression may be considered.
Inhalation of toxic industrial gases such as ammonia, chlorine, and phosgene causes delayed pulmonary
edema; observation for 12-24 hours is required even if initially asymptomatic.
In acute poisoning with unknown substance, the toxidrome approach guides empirical antidote
administration while awaiting laboratory confirmation.
Activated charcoal is ineffective for alcohols, metals, caustics, hydrocarbons, and some pesticides; these
require alternative decontamination strategies.
Whole bowel irrigation with polyethylene glycol is used for body-packers (cocaine or heroin ingestion for
smuggling), iron overdose, and lithium poisoning.
Hemodialysis effectively eliminates lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates, and theophylline; it is
indicated for severe toxicity or renal failure preventing elimination.
In pediatric fever without a source, risk stratification using age, WBC, CRP, and procalcitonin guides
lumbar puncture and empirical antibiotic decisions.
Kawasaki disease in children presents with fever for more than 5 days plus four of five criteria: rash,
conjunctival injection, lip changes, cervical lymphadenopathy, and extremity changes; IVIG plus aspirin is
treatment.
Acute rheumatic fever following group A streptococcal pharyngitis is prevented by complete 10-day
penicillin therapy; diagnosis uses Jones criteria including carditis, polyarthritis, chorea, erythema
marginatum, and subcutaneous nodules.
