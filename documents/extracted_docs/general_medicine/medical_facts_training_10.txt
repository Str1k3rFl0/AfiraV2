The skin is the largest organ of the human body.
The integumentary system consists of the skin, hair, nails, and exocrine glands.
The skin has three primary layers: the epidermis, the dermis, and the hypodermis (subcutaneous
layer).
The epidermis is the outermost layer and provides a waterproof barrier.
Melanocytes are cells in the epidermis that produce melanin, the pigment responsible for skin
color.
Keratin is a tough, fibrous protein that provides structure to skin, hair, and nails.
The dermis contains connective tissue, hair follicles, and sweat glands.
Sebaceous glands produce sebum, an oily substance that lubricates the skin and hair.
Sweat glands (eccrine and apocrine) help regulate body temperature through evaporation.
The hypodermis primarily consists of adipose (fat) tissue for insulation and cushioning.
Vitamin D is synthesized in the skin when exposed to ultraviolet (UV) radiation.
Albinism is a genetic condition characterized by a partial or total lack of melanin.
Vitiligo is an autoimmune condition where the skin loses its pigment in patches.
Acne vulgaris is caused by the clogging of hair follicles with oil and dead skin cells.
Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is a chronic condition that makes the skin red and itchy.
Psoriasis is an autoimmune disease that causes rapid buildup of skin cells, creating scales.
Urticaria is the medical term for hives, often caused by an allergic reaction.
A macule is a flat, distinct, discolored area of skin less than 1 cm wide.
A papule is a small, raised solid pimple or swelling, often forming part of a rash.
A vesicle is a small, fluid-filled blister on the skin.
Pustules are small bumps on the skin that contain fluid or pus.
Pruritus is the medical term for itching.
Erythema is the medical term for redness of the skin.
Cyanosis is a bluish discoloration of the skin due to lack of oxygen.
Jaundice is a yellowing of the skin caused by high levels of bilirubin.
The ABCDE rule is used to identify potential melanoma: Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter,
Evolving.
Melanoma is the most dangerous form of skin cancer.
Basal cell carcinoma is the most common type of skin cancer.
Squamous cell carcinoma is the second most common form of skin cancer.
Alopecia is the medical term for hair loss.
Hirsutism is excessive hair growth in women in areas where men usually have hair.
Onychomycosis is a fungal infection of the fingernails or toenails.
A callus is a thickened area of skin caused by repeated friction or pressure.
A hematoma is a collection of blood outside of blood vessels, often seen as a deep bruise.
Cellulitis is a common, potentially serious bacterial skin infection.
Impetigo is a highly contagious bacterial skin infection, common in children.
Scabies is a skin infestation caused by tiny mites that burrow under the skin.
Tinea pedis is the medical term for athlete's foot, a fungal infection.
Warts are small, grainy skin growths caused by the Human Papillomavirus (HPV).
Contact dermatitis is a red, itchy rash caused by direct contact with a substance.
Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory skin condition that causes redness and visible blood vessels
on the face.
Decubitus ulcers, also known as bedsores, are caused by prolonged pressure on the skin.
Skin turgor is a sign of fluid loss (dehydration); slow return indicates poor hydration.
Collagen is the main structural protein in the dermis that provides strength and elasticity.
The skin's acid mantle is a thin, slightly acidic film on the surface that acts as a barrier to
bacteria.
Lentigines, often called age spots, are flat, brown spots caused by sun exposure.
Angiomas are benign growths made up of small blood vessels.
Keloids are raised scars that grow larger than the original injury.
Dermatology is the branch of medicine dealing with the skin, nails, and hair.
The skin acts as a sensory organ, containing receptors for touch, pressure, pain, and
temperature.
