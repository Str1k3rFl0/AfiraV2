Cardiovascular and Hematology Comprehensive Dataset
The heart is a muscular organ located in the mediastinum of the thoracic cavity.
The pericardium is a double-walled sac that encloses and protects the heart.
The heart consists of four chambers: two atria and two ventricles.
The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the superior and inferior vena cava.
The tricuspid valve separates the right atrium from the right ventricle.
The right ventricle pumps deoxygenated blood into the pulmonary artery toward the lungs.
The pulmonary valve prevents blood from flowing back into the right ventricle.
The left atrium receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via the four pulmonary veins.
The mitral valve, also known as the bicuspid valve, separates the left atrium from the left ventricle.
The left ventricle is the thickest chamber because it must pump blood to the entire body.
The aortic valve prevents blood from flowing back into the left ventricle from the aorta.
The sinoatrial node is the natural pacemaker of the heart located in the right atrium.
The atrioventricular node delays the electrical impulse to allow the ventricles to fill with blood.
The Bundle of His and Purkinje fibers distribute electrical signals throughout the ventricles.
Systole represents the phase of heart contraction and blood ejection.
Diastole represents the phase of heart relaxation and ventricular filling.
Cardiac output is the volume of blood pumped by the heart per minute.
Stroke volume is the amount of blood ejected by the left ventricle in one contraction.
Blood pressure is measured as the force of blood against the walls of the arteries.
Normal adult resting heart rate typically ranges from 60 to 100 beats per minute.
Arteries carry blood away from the heart and have thick elastic walls.
Arterioles are small branches of arteries that lead to capillaries.
Capillaries are the microscopic vessels where the exchange of gases and nutrients occurs.
Venules collect blood from capillaries and lead into veins.
Veins carry blood toward the heart and contain valves to prevent backflow.
Plasma makes up about 55 percent of total blood volume.
Red blood cells, or erythrocytes, are biconcave discs responsible for oxygen transport.
Hemoglobin is the iron-containing protein in red blood cells that binds to oxygen.
White blood cells, or leukocytes, are the primary cells of the immune system.
Platelets, or thrombocytes, are cell fragments essential for blood clotting.
Hematopoiesis is the process of blood cell formation occurring in the red bone marrow.
Erythropoietin is a hormone produced by the kidneys that stimulates red blood cell production.
The universal blood donor type is O negative.
The universal blood recipient type is AB positive.
Antigens on the surface of red blood cells determine a person's blood type.
The Rh factor is an inherited protein found on the surface of red blood cells.
Agglutination occurs when antibodies react with incompatible blood antigens.
Coronary arteries supply oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle itself.
Angina pectoris is chest pain caused by reduced blood flow to the heart muscle.
A myocardial infarction occurs when a coronary artery is completely blocked, causing tissue death.
Atherosclerosis is the hardening of arteries due to the buildup of plaque.
Hypertension is defined as chronically elevated blood pressure above normal ranges.
Heart failure is a condition where the heart cannot pump enough blood to meet the body's needs.
Atrial fibrillation is an irregular and often rapid heart rate that increases stroke risk.
Ventricular fibrillation is a life-threatening heart rhythm that requires immediate defibrillation.
An aneurysm is a localized bulge or ballooning in the wall of a blood vessel.
Deep vein thrombosis is the formation of a blood clot in a deep vein, usually in the legs.
A pulmonary embolism occurs when a blood clot travels to the lungs and blocks an artery.
Anemia is a condition characterized by a deficiency of red blood cells or hemoglobin.
Iron deficiency anemia is the most common form of anemia worldwide.
Sickle cell anemia is a genetic disorder where red blood cells take on a crescent shape.
Hemophilia is a genetic disorder where the blood does not clot properly.
Leukemia is a type of cancer that starts in blood-forming tissue like the bone marrow.
Lymphoma is a cancer of the lymphatic system, which is part of the immune system.
The lymphatic system returns excess interstitial fluid to the bloodstream.
Lymph nodes filter lymph and house white blood cells to fight infection.
The spleen acts as a blood filter and stores a reserve of blood cells.
The thymus gland is where T-lymphocytes mature and develop.
Systemic circulation carries blood between the heart and the rest of the body.
Pulmonary circulation carries blood between the heart and the lungs.
The carotid arteries supply oxygenated blood to the brain.
The jugular veins drain deoxygenated blood from the brain.
The femoral artery is a major blood vessel supplying the lower limbs.
Vasoconstriction is the narrowing of blood vessels to increase blood pressure or conserve heat.
Vasodilation is the widening of blood vessels to decrease blood pressure or release heat.
Baroreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid sinus monitor blood pressure changes.
Chemoreceptors detect changes in blood oxygen, carbon dioxide, and pH levels.
Serum is blood plasma without the clotting factors.
Fibrinogen is a plasma protein that is converted to fibrin during blood coagulation.
Vitamin K is a necessary cofactor for the synthesis of several clotting factors.
Prothrombin time measures how long it takes for blood to clot along the extrinsic pathway.
Hematocrit is the percentage of total blood volume made up of red blood cells.
Reticulocytes are immature red blood cells that indicate the rate of red cell production.
Bilirubin is a byproduct of the breakdown of old red blood cells by the spleen.
A murmur is an abnormal heart sound often caused by turbulent blood flow or valve issues.
Stenosis is the abnormal narrowing of a heart valve that restricts blood flow.
Regurgitation occurs when a heart valve does not close properly, allowing backflow.
Pericarditis is the inflammation of the sac surrounding the heart.
Endocarditis is an infection of the inner lining of the heart chambers and valves.
The P wave on an EKG represents atrial depolarization.
The QRS complex on an EKG represents ventricular depolarization.
The T wave on an EKG represents ventricular repolarization.
Bradycardia is a resting heart rate slower than 60 beats per minute.
Tachycardia is a resting heart rate faster than 100 beats per minute.
An Electrocardiogram is a test that records the electrical activity of the heart.
An Echocardiogram uses ultrasound waves to produce images of the heart's structure.
Cardiac catheterization is a procedure used to diagnose and treat certain cardiovascular conditions.
A bypass surgery creates a new path for blood to flow around a blocked coronary artery.
An angioplasty is a procedure to open narrowed or blocked blood vessels.
A stent is a small mesh tube used to keep an artery open after angioplasty.
The hepatic portal system carries nutrient-rich blood from the gut to the liver.
Albumin is the most abundant plasma protein and maintains osmotic pressure.
Globulins are plasma proteins that include antibodies and transport proteins.
The ductus arteriosus is a blood vessel in the fetus connecting the pulmonary artery to the aorta.
The foramen ovale is an opening in the fetal heart between the right and left atria.
Blood velocity is slowest in the capillaries to allow for the exchange of substances.
Shock is a life-threatening condition where organs do not receive enough blood flow.
Anaphylactic shock is a severe allergic reaction causing massive vasodilation.
Hypovolemic shock is caused by severe blood or fluid loss.
Septic shock is caused by a systemic infection leading to dangerously low blood pressure.
