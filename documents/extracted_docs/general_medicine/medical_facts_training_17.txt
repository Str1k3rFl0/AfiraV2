X-rays were discovered by Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen in 1895.
The basic principle of X-ray imaging is differential absorption of radiation by different tissues.
Air is the least dense and appears black on X-ray (radiolucent).
Metal and bone are the most dense and appear white on X-ray (radiopaque).
Standard chest X-rays are typically taken in PA (Postero-Anterior) and Lateral views.
In an AP (Antero-Posterior) chest X-ray, the heart often appears falsely enlarged.
Magnification occurs when the object being imaged is further from the detector.
Mammography uses low-energy X-rays to detect early signs of breast cancer.
Fluoroscopy provides real-time moving X-ray images of internal structures.
Barium sulfate is commonly used as a contrast agent for gastrointestinal fluoroscopy studies.
Computed Tomography (CT) uses a rotating X-ray tube to create cross-sectional images.
Hounsfield Units (HU) are used to measure radiodensity in CT scans.
Water is defined as 0 HU on the Hounsfield scale.
Air is defined as -1000 HU on the Hounsfield scale.
Cortical bone typically measures above +400 HU.
Acute blood in the brain typically measures between +60 to +80 HU.
Windowing in CT allows the radiologist to focus on specific tissue types (e.g., Lung window, Bone window).
Dual-energy CT can help differentiate between different types of kidney stones.
CT angiography (CTA) is used to visualize blood vessels after injecting iodine-based contrast.
Artifacts in CT can be caused by patient movement or metallic implants (beam hardening).
Iterative reconstruction algorithms in CT help reduce radiation dose while maintaining image quality.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) relies on the behavior of hydrogen protons in a strong magnetic field.
The primary magnetic field in MRI is measured in Tesla (T).
Common clinical MRI strengths are 1.5T and 3.0T.
Radiofrequency (RF) pulses are used to displace protons from their alignment in the magnetic field.
T1 relaxation time refers to the recovery of longitudinal magnetization.
T2 relaxation time refers to the decay of transverse magnetization.
Fluid (like CSF) appears dark on T1-weighted images and bright on T2-weighted images.
Fat typically appears bright on both T1 and T2 weighted standard images.
FLAIR (Fluid Attenuated Inversion Recovery) sequences suppress the signal from free fluid to highlight brain lesions.
Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI) is highly sensitive for detecting acute ischemic stroke within minutes.
Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC) maps help differentiate between true restriction and T2 shine-through.
Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) is a non-invasive way to image the bile ducts.
MRI is contraindicated in patients with non-compatible pacemakers or metallic fragments in the eye.
Gadolinium-based contrast agents shorten T1 relaxation times, making enhanced areas appear bright.
Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis (NSF) is a rare but serious risk of gadolinium in patients with severe renal failure.
Functional MRI (fMRI) measures brain activity by detecting changes in blood flow (BOLD signal).
Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves (2-18 MHz) and has no ionizing radiation.
The piezoelectric effect is the basis for how ultrasound transducers generate and receive sound waves.
Acoustic impedance is the resistance of a tissue to the passage of sound waves.
Anechoic structures (like simple cysts) do not reflect sound and appear black.
Hyperechoic structures (like gallstones) reflect most sound and appear bright.
Acoustic shadowing occurs behind highly reflective or absorptive objects like stones or bone.
Posterior acoustic enhancement occurs behind fluid-filled structures like the gallbladder.
Time-Gain Compensation (TGC) allows the operator to adjust brightness at different depths.
Color Doppler shows the direction of blood flow; usually red towards and blue away from the probe.
Power Doppler is more sensitive to slow flow but does not provide directional information.
Endovaginal ultrasound provides higher resolution images of the pelvic organs compared to transabdominal.
FAST exam (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma) checks for free fluid in the abdomen/pericardium.
Nuclear Medicine involves the administration of radiopharmaceuticals to evaluate organ function.
Technetium-99m is the most commonly used radioisotope in nuclear medicine due to its 6-hour half-life.
Gamma cameras detect photons emitted from the patient's body.
Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT) provides 3D nuclear medicine images.
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) uses tracers like FDG (fluorodeoxyglucose) to map glucose metabolism.
PET scans are frequently combined with CT (PET/CT) for precise anatomical localization of metabolic 'hot spots'.
Bone scans (scintigraphy) are highly sensitive for detecting bone metastases and stress fractures.
Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) scans are used to diagnose pulmonary embolism when CT is contraindicated.
Thyroid uptake scans use Iodine-123 to evaluate thyroid function and nodules.
The 'ALARA' principle stands for 'As Low As Reasonably Achievable' regarding radiation dose.
Lead aprons and thyroid shields are essential for protecting staff during fluoroscopy.
Stochastic effects of radiation (like cancer) have no threshold; the risk increases with dose.
Deterministic effects (like skin burns or cataracts) occur only after reaching a specific dose threshold.
Barium swallow studies evaluate the esophagus for structural or functional abnormalities.
A 'Double Contrast' study uses both barium and air to better visualize the mucosal surface.
The 'Steeple Sign' on a neck X-ray is characteristic of Croup (subglottic narrowing).
The 'Thumbprint Sign' on a lateral neck X-ray suggests Epiglottitis.
Sail Sign on a pediatric chest X-ray represents the normal thymus gland.
Kerley B lines on a chest X-ray are short horizontal lines indicating pulmonary edema.
Pneumoperitoneum (free air under the diaphragm) is best seen on an upright chest or abdominal X-ray.
The 'String Sign' in the terminal ileum can be seen in Crohn's disease during a small bowel series.
Intussusception often shows a 'Target Sign' or 'Crescent Sign' on ultrasound or CT.
Cholecystitis is often diagnosed via ultrasound by seeing gallbladder wall thickening and a positive sonographic Murphy sign.
Urolithiasis (kidney stones) are best detected using a non-contrast CT of the abdomen and pelvis (KUB protocol).
Pancreatitis on CT is characterized by fat stranding and diffuse enlargement of the pancreas.
Aortic dissection is classically diagnosed using IV contrast-enhanced CT showing an intimal flap.
Pulmonary Embolism (PE) is diagnosed on CTPA by identifying filling defects in the pulmonary arteries.
Subarachnoid hemorrhage appears as hyperdense (white) fluid in the cisterns and sulci on a non-contrast CT.
Epidural hematoma is typically biconvex (lens-shaped) and does not cross cranial sutures.
Subdural hematoma is typically crescent-shaped and can cross cranial sutures.
Hydrocephalus is identified by the enlargement of the ventricular system out of proportion to sulcal widening.
Cerebral edema is seen as a loss of grey-white matter differentiation on CT.
Multiple Sclerosis (MS) lesions are best visualized on MRI T2/FLAIR as periventricular white matter 'Dawson's fingers'.
Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA) can be done with or without contrast (using Time-of-Flight techniques).
Elastography is an imaging modality that maps the elastic properties and stiffness of soft tissue.
Virtual Colonoscopy (CT Colonography) is a less invasive alternative to traditional colonoscopy for screening.
Bone Age is typically determined by an X-ray of the left hand and wrist.
Hysterosalpingography (HSG) is an X-ray study used to evaluate the uterus and fallopian tubes for infertility.
Cystography involves filling the bladder with contrast to evaluate for reflux or rupture.
Voiding Cystourethrography (VCUG) is commonly used in children to diagnose vesicoureteral reflux.
The 'Golden Hour' in trauma often relies heavily on 'Pan-scan' CT imaging for rapid diagnosis.
Interventional Radiology (IR) uses imaging guidance to perform minimally invasive procedures like biopsies or stenting.
Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) removes background bone/tissue to isolate blood vessels.
Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA) uses heat generated by medium-frequency alternating current to treat tumors.
Uterine Artery Embolization (UAE) is an IR procedure used to treat symptomatic fibroids.
Percutaneous Nephrostomy is used to drain an obstructed kidney using ultrasound or fluoroscopic guidance.
Image-guided biopsies increase accuracy and decrease complications compared to 'blind' biopsies.
Infarction on MRI DWI shows 'restricted diffusion' as bright signal.
The 'Apple Core' lesion on a barium enema is a classic sign of colon cancer.
Spondylolisthesis is the displacement of one vertebra over another, seen on lateral spinal X-rays.
Pneumothorax is identified by the absence of lung markings at the periphery and a visible visceral pleural line.
Air bronchograms occur when air-filled bronchi are visible against fluid-filled (consolidated) lung tissue.
Atelectasis is the collapse of lung tissue, seen as increased density and volume loss on X-ray.
Golden S-sign on chest X-ray suggests right upper lobe collapse due to a central mass.
Mediastinal widening on X-ray can be a sign of aortic rupture or lymphadenopathy.
Calcification of the gallbladder wall is known as 'Porcelain Gallbladder' and is a risk for cancer.
The 'Coffee Bean' sign on an abdominal X-ray is characteristic of sigmoid volvulus.
Rigler's sign is seen when air is present on both sides of the bowel wall in pneumoperitoneum.
A 'Lead Pipe' appearance of the colon on barium studies suggests chronic ulcerative colitis.
Osteoblastic metastases (white) are common in prostate cancer; osteolytic (dark) are common in multiple myeloma.
DEXA scans (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry) are the gold standard for measuring bone mineral density.
T-scores in DEXA compare a patient's bone density to a healthy 30-year-old.
Z-scores in DEXA compare a patient's bone density to others of the same age and gender.
Myelography involves injecting contrast into the subarachnoid space to image the spinal cord.
Arthrography is imaging of a joint after the injection of a contrast medium.
Contrast-Induced Nephropathy (CIN) is a decline in renal function following contrast administration.
Pre-hydration with saline is one of the most effective ways to prevent contrast-induced nephropathy.
Metformin should be held for 48 hours after IV contrast in patients with impaired renal function.
A 'Cold Nodule' on a thyroid scan has a higher risk of malignancy than a 'Hot Nodule'.
Sentinel lymph node mapping uses radioactive dye to identify the first node where cancer may spread.
Scintigraphy for Meckel's diverticulum uses Pertechnetate to find ectopic gastric mucosa.
The 'Panda Sign' in Gallium-67 imaging can be seen in Sarcoidosis.
HIDA scan evaluates the function of the gallbladder and patency of the cystic duct.
Bone scans are more sensitive than X-rays for early detection of osteomyelitis.
DMSA scan is used in pediatrics to evaluate for renal scarring.
MRI sequences like SWI (Susceptibility Weighted Imaging) are excellent for detecting microhemorrhages.
MR spectroscopy (MRS) measures metabolite levels in tissues, helping differentiate tumors from abscesses.
A 'Bucket Handle' tear is a specific type of meniscus tear visible on knee MRI.
The 'Dreaded Black Line' on a tibial X-ray indicates a high-risk stress fracture.
Terry Thomas sign is a gap between the scaphoid and lunate bones, indicating ligament injury.
The 'Scotty Dog' with a collar sign on oblique lumbar X-rays indicates spondylolysis.
A 'Sailor's Cap' or 'Doughnut' sign in the brain can refer to specific abscess or tumor appearances.
Imaging of the 'Internal Auditory Canals' (IAC) is best done with high-resolution MRI.
Breast MRI is used for screening high-risk patients and evaluating implant rupture.
The 'Water-Lily' sign on X-ray or CT is classic for a hydatid cyst in the liver or lung.
CT Urography is a specialized CT used to evaluate the entire urinary tract for hematuria.
The 'Sentinel Loop' on an abdominal X-ray is a localized segment of dilated bowel near an inflamed organ.
Osteoid osteoma shows a classic 'nidus' (small radiolucent center) on CT or X-ray.
CT Perfusion is used in acute stroke to map salvageable brain tissue (the penumbra).
Maximum Intensity Projection (MIP) is a 3D rendering technique used in CTA and MRA.
The 'Sunburst' periosteal reaction on X-ray is highly suggestive of Osteosarcoma.
The 'Onion Skin' periosteal reaction is characteristic of Ewing Sarcoma.
Codman's Triangle is a sign of aggressive bone lesions where the periosteum is lifted.
Phleboliths are small, rounded calcifications in pelvic veins, often confused with ureteral stones.
The 'Fat Pad Sign' (Sail sign) on an elbow X-ray indicates an occult fracture and joint effusion.
Shoulder MR Arthrography is the preferred method for evaluating labral tears.
Cardiac MRI is the gold standard for quantifying ventricular volumes and ejection fraction.
Late Gadolinium Enhancement (LGE) on cardiac MRI identifies myocardial scarring or fibrosis.
Radiology reports should ideally use standardized lexicons like BI-RADS or LI-RADS.
The 'Vacuum Phenomenon' in a joint or spinal disc indicates the presence of gas, usually nitrogen.
Imaging is just one piece of the clinical puzzle and must always be correlated with the physical exam.
Artificial Intelligence (AI) in radiology is increasingly used for rapid screening of triage cases like ICH or PE.
Teleradiology allows for the remote interpretation of medical images across the globe.
The final diagnosis often requires a multidisciplinary approach involving radiologists, pathologists, and clinicians.
