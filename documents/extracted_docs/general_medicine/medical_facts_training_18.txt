Chest X-ray is performed in posteroanterior (PA) projection as the standard view, with the patient standing and the film cassette against the anterior chest.
An anteroposterior (AP) chest X-ray magnifies the cardiac silhouette due to beam divergence and is used for portable or bedside examinations.
On a well-positioned PA chest X-ray, the medial ends of the clavicles should be equidistant from the spinous process of the vertebra at that level.
A correctly exposed chest X-ray allows visualization of the vertebral bodies through the cardiac shadow without overexposing the lung fields.
The cardiothoracic ratio is measured as the widest cardiac diameter divided by the widest thoracic diameter; a ratio above 0.5 on a PA film suggests cardiomegaly.
On the lateral chest X-ray, the retrosternal and retrocardiac spaces should be clear; opacification of these spaces indicates pathology in the anterior or posterior mediastinum.
The right hemidiaphragm is normally higher than the left by up to 2.5 cm due to the underlying liver.
Loss of the right hemidiaphragm contour on chest X-ray indicates right lower lobe pathology due to the silhouette sign.
Loss of the left hemidiaphragm contour indicates left lower lobe or lingular pathology.
The silhouette sign states that two structures of the same radiodensity in contact with each other will obliterate the border between them.
Right middle lobe collapse or consolidation obliterates the right heart border due to the silhouette sign.
Lingular consolidation obliterates the left heart border.
Right lower lobe consolidation obliterates the right hemidiaphragm but preserves the right heart border.
Left lower lobe collapse on chest X-ray appears as a triangular opacity behind the heart with loss of the medial left hemidiaphragm.
Right upper lobe collapse causes elevation of the minor fissure and a triangular opacity in the right upper zone.
Left upper lobe collapse produces a veil-like opacity over the left hemithorax with no sharp borders; the Luftsichel sign (crescent of air) may be seen.
The Luftsichel sign represents hyperlucency between the aortic knuckle and the collapsed left upper lobe due to overinflated superior segment of the left lower lobe.
Right middle lobe collapse appears as a wedge-shaped opacity in the right lower zone on the PA film and loss of definition of the right heart border.
The Golden S sign is seen in right upper lobe collapse caused by a central mass; the reversed S shape is formed by the concave elevated minor fissure and the convex mass.
Complete lobar collapse causes ipsilateral mediastinal shift, elevation of the hemidiaphragm, and compensatory hyperinflation of adjacent lobes.
Atelectasis refers to collapse of alveoli; discoid or plate atelectasis appears as a thin linear horizontal opacity, commonly at the lung bases.
Air bronchograms within an opacity indicate that the bronchi remain patent and the surrounding alveoli are filled with fluid or cells, suggesting consolidation rather than collapse.
Pulmonary edema on chest X-ray shows bilateral perihilar ('bat-wing') opacities, Kerley B lines at the periphery, cardiomegaly, and upper lobe vascular diversion.
Kerley B lines are short horizontal lines at the lung periphery representing interlobular septal thickening due to fluid or lymphatic obstruction.
Upper lobe vascular diversion on chest X-ray indicates elevated pulmonary venous pressure and is an early sign of pulmonary edema.
Unilateral pulmonary edema occurs in patients who sleep on one side, in re-expansion pulmonary edema, or following aspiration.
Pleural effusion on erect chest X-ray produces a homogeneous opacity that blunts the costophrenic angle when more than 200-300 mL of fluid is present.
Subpulmonic pleural effusion accumulates between the lung base and the diaphragm; the apparent diaphragm peak is displaced laterally.
On supine chest X-ray, pleural effusion produces a homogeneous increased density over the lower lung zone without an air-fluid level.
A meniscus sign on erect chest X-ray confirms the presence of pleural fluid; the fluid curves up at the chest wall due to surface tension.
Pleural effusion exceeding half the hemithorax with contralateral mediastinal shift suggests a large effusion or malignant cause.
Ipsilateral mediastinal shift toward an opaque hemithorax suggests lung collapse rather than effusion.
Pneumothorax on erect chest X-ray is identified by a visible pleural line with absent lung markings peripheral to it.
A small pneumothorax may be visible only on expiratory chest X-ray; the lung becomes relatively smaller and the pneumothorax more apparent.
Tension pneumothorax is characterized by complete ipsilateral lung collapse, contralateral mediastinal shift, ipsilateral hemidiaphragm depression, and neck vein distension.
Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis and should not be delayed for imaging if the patient is hemodynamically compromised.
Deep sulcus sign on supine chest X-ray is a deep lucency in the lateral costophrenic angle and represents a pneumothorax in a supine patient.
Pneumomediastinum appears as air outlining the mediastinal structures, most commonly the aortic arch and the left cardiac border.
Subcutaneous emphysema on chest X-ray shows air tracking in the soft tissues of the chest wall and neck as streaky lucencies.
Pneumopericardium is rare and presents as air surrounding the cardiac shadow, limited by the pericardial reflections at the great vessels.
A widened mediastinum on chest X-ray (greater than 8 cm at the level of the aortic knuckle) raises concern for aortic dissection, mediastinal hematoma, or mass.
In aortic dissection, chest X-ray may show a widened mediastinum, irregular aortic contour, left pleural effusion, and tracheal deviation to the right.
The aortic knuckle should have a smooth, concave contour; irregularity or convexity may indicate aortic aneurysm or dissection.
Tracheal deviation to the right on chest X-ray in a patient with mediastinal widening suggests a left-sided aortic arch aneurysm or dissection.
Rib notching on chest X-ray is caused by enlarged intercostal arteries in aortic coarctation, appearing as irregular erosions on the inferior margins of ribs 3-8.
Pulmonary arterial hypertension on chest X-ray shows enlarged central pulmonary arteries with peripheral vascular pruning.
The ratio of the right descending pulmonary artery diameter to the tracheal diameter above 1 suggests pulmonary arterial hypertension.
Pulmonary embolism is an unreliable diagnosis on chest X-ray; findings include the Hampton's hump, Westermark sign, and Palla's sign.
Hampton's hump is a peripheral wedge-shaped pleural-based opacity in pulmonary embolism, representing pulmonary infarction.
Westermark sign is focal oligemia (decreased vascular markings) distal to a pulmonary embolism on chest X-ray.
Palla's sign is enlargement of the right descending pulmonary artery on chest X-ray, indicating massive pulmonary embolism.
CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) is the gold standard imaging for diagnosis of pulmonary embolism, with sensitivity and specificity above 95%.
On CTPA, pulmonary embolism appears as a filling defect within the contrast-opacified pulmonary arteries.
A saddle embolus straddles the bifurcation of the main pulmonary artery and is associated with high mortality and right heart strain.
Right heart strain in massive PE on CT is identified by right ventricular dilatation, interventricular septal bowing to the left (D-sign), and reflux of contrast into the hepatic veins.
Crazy paving pattern on CT refers to ground-glass opacity with superimposed interlobular septal thickening, resembling irregular stone paving; it has many causes including pulmonary alveolar proteinosis, COVID-19 pneumonia, and ARDS.
Ground-glass opacity on CT indicates partial filling of alveolar spaces, thickening of the interstitium, or both; it is a nonspecific finding.
Consolidation on CT represents complete filling of alveolar spaces; air bronchograms are frequently visible within areas of consolidation.
Tree-in-bud pattern on CT indicates filling of centrilobular bronchioles and alveolar ducts with secretions, pus, or fluid; it is characteristic of endobronchial spread of infection.
Honeycombing on high-resolution CT is characterized by clustered cystic air spaces with thick walls in the subpleural lung and represents end-stage fibrosis.
Usual interstitial pneumonia pattern on HRCT shows peripheral, basal, and subpleural honeycombing with traction bronchiectasis, correlating pathologically with IPF.
Ground-glass opacity predominating over fibrosis on HRCT suggests an active inflammatory process that may be reversible, unlike a predominant honeycombing pattern.
Non-specific interstitial pneumonia (NSIP) on HRCT shows bilateral, basal, symmetric ground-glass opacity and reticular changes with subpleural sparing.
Organizing pneumonia on CT shows peripheral, peribronchial, or band-like consolidation with the reverse halo (atoll) sign.
The atoll sign or reverse halo sign is a central ground-glass opacity surrounded by a ring of denser consolidation; it is characteristic of organizing pneumonia.
Lymphangitic carcinomatosis on HRCT shows irregular thickening of the interlobular septa and peribronchial thickening, often with pleural effusion and hilar lymphadenopathy.
Sarcoidosis on chest X-ray is staged from 0 to IV: 0 normal, I bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, II bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy with pulmonary infiltrates, III pulmonary infiltrates alone, IV pulmonary fibrosis.
On CT, sarcoidosis shows perilymphatic nodules distributed along the bronchovascular bundles, subpleural regions, and interlobular septa.
Silicosis on chest X-ray shows upper lobe predominant small rounded nodules; progressive massive fibrosis creates large upper lobe masses with eggshell calcification of hilar nodes.
Eggshell calcification of hilar lymph nodes is most commonly associated with silicosis and sarcoidosis.
Asbestosis on chest X-ray and CT shows lower lobe reticulation, pleural plaques, and pleural thickening; pleural plaques are pathognomonic of asbestos exposure.
Pleural plaques are calcified or non-calcified fibrous lesions on the parietal pleura visible on chest X-ray and CT, indicating prior asbestos exposure.
Mesothelioma on CT presents as circumferential pleural thickening, pleural effusion, and encasement of the lung; it is associated with asbestos exposure.
Lung cancer on chest X-ray may appear as a spiculated mass, hilar enlargement, lobar collapse, or pleural effusion.
A solitary pulmonary nodule is defined as a focal opacity up to 3 cm in diameter completely surrounded by lung parenchyma.
Nodules larger than 3 cm are termed masses and are more likely to be malignant.
The Fleischner Society guidelines recommend management of incidental pulmonary nodules based on size, morphology, and patient risk factors.
Calcification patterns in pulmonary nodules that favor benignity include popcorn (hamartoma), central, laminated, and diffuse calcification.
Eccentric or stippled calcification in a pulmonary nodule does not exclude malignancy.
A corona radiata sign—fine spiculations radiating from a nodule—is highly associated with malignancy.
PET-CT using FDG is used to evaluate pulmonary nodules and stage lung cancer; SUV above 2.5 suggests malignancy but false positives occur in infection and inflammation.
Cavitation within a pulmonary lesion may indicate necrotizing infection, squamous cell carcinoma, vasculitis, or septic emboli.
Thick irregular walls of a cavitary lesion are more suggestive of malignancy, whereas thin smooth walls favor benign etiology.
Septic emboli on CT appear as multiple peripheral nodules, often with cavitation, and may have a feeding vessel sign.
CT-guided percutaneous lung biopsy is used to sample peripheral pulmonary lesions inaccessible by bronchoscopy; complications include pneumothorax and hemorrhage.
Endobronchial ultrasound (EBUS) is used to sample central lesions, mediastinal lymph nodes, and perihilar masses under real-time ultrasound guidance.
V/Q scintigraphy is used to diagnose pulmonary embolism when CTPA is contraindicated due to contrast allergy or renal impairment.
A normal V/Q scan effectively excludes pulmonary embolism; a high-probability scan with appropriate clinical probability is diagnostic.
On plain abdominal X-ray, gas in the small bowel appears centrally as featureless loops with complete circumferential mucosal folds (valvulae conniventes).
Colonic gas on abdominal X-ray is identified peripherally with haustrations that do not extend across the full width of the bowel.
Small bowel obstruction on plain X-ray shows dilated small bowel loops (above 3 cm) with air-fluid levels on the erect film and a paucity of colonic gas.
The string-of-beads sign on abdominal X-ray is seen in small bowel obstruction when small pockets of gas are trapped between the valvulae conniventes.
Large bowel obstruction on plain X-ray shows dilated colon (above 6 cm for the colon, above 9 cm for the cecum) proximal to the point of obstruction.
Sigmoid volvulus on abdominal X-ray produces the coffee-bean sign: a markedly distended loop of sigmoid colon folded on itself pointing toward the right upper quadrant.
Cecal volvulus on abdominal X-ray shows a large oval gas-filled structure in the left upper quadrant or mid-abdomen, with small bowel obstruction.
Pneumoperitoneum (free air under the diaphragm) on erect chest X-ray is best seen as a crescent of air under the right hemidiaphragm.
On supine X-ray, pneumoperitoneum produces the Rigler sign (both sides of the bowel wall visible due to intraluminal and extraluminal gas) and the football sign (large collection of free air).
Free intraperitoneal gas is confirmed on CT as extraluminal air adjacent to bowel or beneath the anterior abdominal wall.
Portal venous gas on plain X-ray or CT appears as gas branching peripherally within the liver and indicates bowel ischemia or infarction.
Pneumatosis intestinalis (gas within the bowel wall) may be benign (asthma, COPD, steroid use) or a sign of bowel ischemia and necrosis.
CT of the abdomen and pelvis with IV contrast is the first-line imaging for acute abdominal pain in adults when plain X-ray is inconclusive.
The transition point in CT of small bowel obstruction identifies the level of obstruction and may reveal the cause such as adhesion, hernia, or mass.
Closed-loop obstruction on CT shows a C- or U-shaped loop of dilated bowel with a beak sign at the point of torsion; it is a surgical emergency.
Bowel wall thickening above 3 mm in small bowel and above 5 mm in large bowel on CT indicates pathology such as ischemia, inflammation, or neoplasm.
Target sign or halo sign on CT bowel represents alternating layers of high and low attenuation within a thickened bowel wall, typical of submucosal edema or hemorrhage.
Mesenteric stranding on CT indicates inflammation or ischemia of the adjacent bowel and is seen in Crohn's disease, appendicitis, and mesenteric ischemia.
CT angiography is used to evaluate mesenteric ischemia; superior mesenteric artery occlusion is identified as an abrupt cutoff of contrast within the vessel.
Pneumobilia (gas in the biliary tree) on CT may be benign (prior ERCP, biliary-enteric anastomosis) or indicate a biliary-enteric fistula from gallstone erosion (Rigler's triad).
Rigler's triad for gallstone ileus on plain X-ray includes pneumobilia, small bowel obstruction, and an ectopic gallstone.
Acute appendicitis on CT is identified by a distended appendix with wall thickening (above 6 mm outer diameter), periappendiceal fat stranding, and lack of luminal contrast.
An appendicolith (calcified fecalith) within the appendix on CT increases the probability of complicated appendicitis.
Ultrasound diagnosis of appendicitis relies on visualization of a non-compressible, aperistaltic appendix with a diameter above 6 mm.
Perforated appendicitis on CT shows periappendiceal abscess, free fluid, free air, and loss of the normal appendiceal wall.
The Alvarado score incorporates migration of pain to right iliac fossa, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, right iliac fossa tenderness, rebound tenderness, elevated temperature, leukocytosis, and left shift.
Acute cholecystitis on ultrasound shows gallbladder wall thickening above 3 mm, pericholecystic fluid, a positive sonographic Murphy's sign, and gallstones.
Gangrenous cholecystitis on CT is identified by gallbladder wall irregularity, intraluminal membranes, pericholecystic abscess, and pneumobilia.
Choledocholithiasis on ultrasound is identified by a dilated common bile duct (above 6 mm) with acoustic shadowing from stones; sensitivity is only 50-75%.
MRCP is the gold standard non-invasive imaging for biliary tract pathology and provides detailed images of the biliary and pancreatic ducts without radiation.
CT severity index (Balthazar score) in acute pancreatitis grades pancreatic necrosis and peri-pancreatic fluid collections; higher scores correlate with increased morbidity and mortality.
Walled-off pancreatic necrosis on CT appears as a well-defined fluid collection with non-enhancing solid debris, developing 4 or more weeks after acute necrotizing pancreatitis.
Pancreatic pseudocyst on CT is a well-defined homogeneous fluid collection without solid content, arising 4 or more weeks after acute edematous pancreatitis.
Double duct sign on CT or MRCP shows simultaneous dilatation of the common bile duct and pancreatic duct; it is strongly associated with pancreatic head carcinoma.
Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma on CT typically appears as a hypoattenuating mass in the pancreatic head with vascular encasement and ductal dilatation.
Vascular encasement by pancreatic cancer on CT is assessed by the degree of contact of tumor with the superior mesenteric artery, celiac axis, and portal vein.
Hepatocellular carcinoma on CT shows arterial phase enhancement with washout on portal venous phase (arterial enhancement and portal venous washout pattern); this is diagnostic in cirrhotic patients without biopsy.
The LI-RADS system categorizes liver observations in patients at risk for HCC from LR-1 (definitely benign) to LR-5 (definitely HCC).
Hepatic hemangioma on CT shows peripheral nodular enhancement in the arterial phase with progressive centripetal fill-in on delayed phase.
Hepatic hemangioma on MRI appears markedly hyperintense on T2-weighted sequences and shows the same fill-in enhancement pattern as on CT.
Focal nodular hyperplasia on CT shows a well-defined hypervascular mass with a central scar that enhances on delayed phase; no washout occurs.
Hepatic adenoma on CT is a hypervascular lesion associated with oral contraceptive use; it may contain fat, hemorrhage, or show washout; larger adenomas are at risk of rupture.
Liver metastases from hypervascular tumors (renal cell carcinoma, neuroendocrine tumors) enhance avidly in the arterial phase; hypovascular metastases (colorectal) appear as hypoattenuating lesions on portal venous phase.
MRI with liver-specific contrast agents (gadoxetic acid) improves detection of small liver metastases by providing hepatobiliary phase imaging.
Simple renal cyst on CT or ultrasound is a well-defined, thin-walled, non-enhancing fluid attenuation structure; it is classified as Bosniak I.
Bosniak classification stratifies renal cysts from I (simple cyst, benign) to IV (solid enhancing components, high risk of malignancy requiring surgery).
Renal cell carcinoma on CT typically shows a heterogeneous enhancing renal mass; clear cell RCC is the most common subtype and enhances avidly.
Von Hippel-Lindau syndrome is associated with bilateral and multifocal clear cell renal cell carcinomas, hemangioblastomas, and pancreatic cysts.
Adrenal adenoma on CT typically has an attenuation below 10 HU on unenhanced CT due to intracytoplasmic lipid content.
The absolute washout value (above 60%) and relative washout value (above 40%) at 15 minutes on adrenal CT protocol confirm adenoma.
Pheochromocytoma on CT appears as a large heterogeneous adrenal mass, often with central necrosis; it shows intense enhancement.
On MRI, pheochromocytoma characteristically appears very bright on T2-weighted imaging (light bulb sign).
Adrenal metastases are usually bilateral and seen in patients with known primary malignancy; they do not show lipid on chemical shift MRI.
Renal calculi are best visualized on non-contrast CT; the majority (90%) are radiopaque on plain X-ray.
Uric acid stones are radiolucent on plain X-ray but are visible on CT as soft tissue attenuation calculi within the collecting system.
Hydronephrosis on ultrasound appears as anechoic separation of the central renal sinus echoes; grading from I to IV reflects severity.
CT urogram with non-contrast, nephrographic, and excretory phases is used to evaluate the entire urinary tract for calculi, tumors, and structural abnormalities.
Urothelial carcinoma (transitional cell carcinoma) appears on CT urogram as an intraluminal filling defect or wall thickening of the ureter or collecting system.
Bladder cancer on CT cystography or MRI appears as a sessile or papillary intraluminal mass; MRI is used for local staging (T staging).
Prostate cancer on MRI is detected on T2-weighted imaging as a hypointense area in the peripheral zone; DWI and DCE sequences improve detection.
PI-RADS scoring on multiparametric prostate MRI categorizes findings from 1 (very low) to 5 (very high) likelihood of clinically significant cancer.
Renal artery stenosis on CT or MR angiography is identified as a narrowing of the renal artery; it is most commonly due to atherosclerosis in older patients and fibromuscular dysplasia in younger women.
Trauma to the kidney on CT is graded I-V by the American Association for the Surgery of Trauma; grade IV-V injuries may require intervention.
Splenic laceration on CT is graded I-V; grade IV-V injuries involve vascular disruption and are managed with angioembolization or splenectomy.
Active arterial hemorrhage on CT is identified as extravasation of contrast (blush) adjacent to an injured organ in the arterial phase.
CT angiography of the abdomen and pelvis is the first-line investigation for suspected abdominal aortic aneurysm rupture in stable patients.
A normal aortic diameter at the infrarenal level is up to 3 cm; aneurysm is defined as dilatation above 3 cm.
Endovascular aortic repair (EVAR) follow-up imaging with CT angiography detects endoleaks, which are classified type I-V based on source of blood flow outside the stent graft.
CT aortography is used to classify aortic dissection (Stanford A and B), identify the intimal flap, and assess the extent of the dissection.
The intimal flap on CT aortography divides the aorta into a true lumen (usually smaller) and a false lumen (usually larger with slower flow).
Skull X-ray has largely been replaced by CT; indications are limited to detecting depressed skull fractures and foreign bodies.
Non-contrast CT of the brain is the first-line investigation for acute stroke, head trauma, and altered consciousness.
On non-contrast CT, acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage appears as a hyperdense (white) area due to the protein content of fresh blood.
Subarachnoid hemorrhage on CT appears as hyperdensity filling the basal cisterns, sylvian fissures, and sulci.
CT angiography of the brain is used to identify the source of subarachnoid hemorrhage and is highly sensitive for aneurysms above 3 mm.
MRI with TOF (time-of-flight) MR angiography is used to screen for intracranial aneurysms without radiation or contrast.
Cerebral infarction is initially isodense on CT; the first detectable sign may be the dense MCA sign (hyperdensity of the occluded middle cerebral artery) or loss of grey-white matter differentiation.
The ASPECTS score grades CT changes in MCA territory ischemic stroke from 0 (diffuse changes) to 10 (normal); scores below 6 are associated with poor outcome after thrombolysis.
DWI MRI is the most sensitive imaging for acute ischemic stroke, detecting cytotoxic edema within minutes of onset as restricted diffusion.
Restricted diffusion on DWI appears as hyperintensity, confirmed by corresponding hypointensity on the ADC (apparent diffusion coefficient) map.
Hemorrhagic transformation of ischemic stroke on CT appears as hyperdensity within a previously hypodense ischemic area.
MRI is more sensitive than CT for posterior fossa and brainstem pathology due to the absence of beam-hardening artifact from the petrous bones.
Epidural hematoma on CT appears as a biconvex (lenticular) hyperdense collection between the inner table of the skull and the dura; it does not cross suture lines.
The lucid interval in epidural hematoma represents a period of normal consciousness between initial loss of consciousness and deterioration due to hematoma expansion.
Subdural hematoma on CT is a crescent-shaped collection that follows the contour of the brain and crosses suture lines; acute SDH is hyperdense, chronic SDH is hypodense.
Isodense subdural hematoma on CT (subacute phase, 1-3 weeks old) may be difficult to detect; look for effacement of sulci or midline shift.
Midline shift on CT is measured at the level of the septum pellucidum; shift above 5 mm is clinically significant.
Diffuse axonal injury (DAI) is best detected on MRI with gradient echo or SWI sequences as multiple small hypointense foci at the grey-white matter junction.
Cerebral contusions on CT appear as hyperdense hemorrhagic foci surrounded by hypodense edema, commonly in the frontal and temporal poles.
Herniation syndromes on CT include uncal herniation (effacement of ipsilateral suprasellar cistern, contralateral P1 compression), subfalcine herniation (cingulate gyrus displacement), and downward transtentorial herniation.
CT perfusion imaging in acute stroke defines the ischemic core (irreversibly damaged tissue) and the penumbra (salvageable tissue) to guide thrombolysis and thrombectomy decisions.
Brain metastases on contrast-enhanced CT appear as enhancing nodules at the grey-white matter junction, often with surrounding vasogenic edema.
Vasogenic edema in brain metastases appears as perilesional hypodensity on CT and T2/FLAIR hyperintensity on MRI, extending along white matter tracts.
Glioblastoma (WHO grade IV glioma) on MRI shows a central necrotic core with ring enhancement and surrounding vasogenic edema crossing the corpus callosum (butterfly glioma pattern).
Meningioma on CT appears as an extra-axial isodense or hyperdense mass that homogeneously enhances and may show adjacent bony hyperostosis.
The dural tail sign of meningioma is enhancement of adjacent dura; it is characteristic but not pathognomonic.
Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) on MRI appears as a well-defined mass in the internal auditory canal and cerebellopontine angle, enhancing with gadolinium.
Pituitary macroadenoma on MRI shows a sellar and suprasellar mass that may compress the optic chiasm; it does not enhance as brightly as normal pituitary tissue.
Empty sella on MRI shows cerebrospinal fluid filling a partially or completely empty pituitary fossa with a flattened pituitary gland.
Spine MRI is the investigation of choice for cord compression, disc herniation, infection, and intrinsic cord pathology.
Cervical spine CT is performed in all unconscious trauma patients or those with high-risk mechanisms to exclude fracture and instability.
The NEXUS criteria and Canadian C-Spine Rule stratify patients after blunt trauma to identify who requires cervical spine imaging.
Jefferson fracture is a burst fracture of C1 (atlas) from axial loading; the odontoid peg view or CT shows lateral masses displaced laterally.
Hangman's fracture is a bilateral fracture through the pedicles of C2 from hyperextension and axial loading.
Odontoid fracture classification: type I (above the base), type II (through the base, highest nonunion rate), type III (extending into the body of C2).
SCIWORA (spinal cord injury without radiological abnormality) occurs mainly in children and is diagnosed on MRI showing cord signal abnormality with normal CT.
Wedge compression fracture of the thoracic spine on X-ray shows anterior vertebral body height reduction with intact posterior height; MRI identifies acute versus chronic fractures.
Chance fracture is a flexion-distraction injury through the vertebral body and posterior elements, associated with seat-belt injury and intra-abdominal trauma.
Burst fracture of the lumbar spine on CT shows comminution of the vertebral body with retropulsion of bone fragments into the spinal canal.
Spondylolisthesis is graded by the percentage of forward slip of one vertebra on the one below: grade I (0-25%), grade II (25-50%), grade III (50-75%), grade IV (75-100%).
Spondylolysis is a stress fracture through the pars interarticularis, best seen on oblique X-ray as the Scottie dog collar sign.
On MRI, disc herniation is classified as protrusion (disc material contained within the annulus), extrusion (disc material extends beyond the disc space), or sequestration (free fragment).
Cauda equina syndrome on MRI is identified by compression of the nerve roots in the thecal sac below L1-L2; urgent decompression is required.
Spinal cord compression on MRI requires urgent assessment; intrinsic T2 signal hyperintensity within the cord indicates myelopathic change.
Vertebral osteomyelitis on MRI shows T2 hyperintensity within the disc and adjacent vertebral endplates; contrast enhancement confirms active infection.
Epidural abscess on MRI appears as a hyperintense extradural collection compressing the thecal sac; gadolinium enhances the periphery of the abscess.
Bone metastases on plain X-ray may be lytic (most common: myeloma, renal cell carcinoma, thyroid), sclerotic (prostate, breast), or mixed.
Bone scan (99mTc-MDP scintigraphy) is sensitive for osteoblastic (sclerotic) metastases but less sensitive for purely lytic lesions such as myeloma.
PET-CT with FDG is used for staging and restaging of malignancies; it detects metabolically active bone metastases that may not yet be visible on CT.
MRI is the most sensitive modality for bone marrow infiltration by metastases; low T1 signal and high T2 (STIR) signal replace normal marrow fat.
Osteoporosis is diagnosed on DXA scan by a T-score at or below -2.5 at the lumbar spine, femoral neck, or total hip.
A T-score between -1 and -2.5 on DXA indicates osteopenia; fragility fracture risk is used alongside DXA in management decisions.
Insufficiency fractures occur in osteoporotic bone under normal physiological stress; sacral insufficiency fractures on MRI show bilateral parasagittal fracture lines (Honda sign on bone scan).
Paget's disease of bone shows enlargement and cortical thickening on X-ray with a coarse trabecular pattern; the blade of grass sign is seen in active long bone involvement.
Osteoid osteoma on CT shows a small nidus (usually less than 1.5 cm) with surrounding sclerosis; nocturnal pain relieved by aspirin is characteristic.
Giant cell tumor of bone on X-ray appears as an eccentric lytic lesion at the epiphysis of a long bone, reaching the articular surface without sclerotic margin.
Aneurysmal bone cyst on X-ray is a well-defined expansile lytic lesion with fluid-fluid levels visible on MRI.
Osteosarcoma on X-ray shows aggressive bone destruction with periosteal reaction, including the sunburst pattern and Codman's triangle.
Codman's triangle is periosteal new bone formation lifted by an aggressive lesion, appearing as a triangular shadow at the margin of the tumor.
Ewing's sarcoma on X-ray shows a permeative lytic lesion with an onion-skin periosteal reaction, most commonly in the diaphysis of long bones.
Chondrosarcoma on X-ray appears as a lesion with chondroid matrix calcification (rings and arcs pattern) within an aggressive bone lesion.
Enchondroma on X-ray is a benign cartilaginous lesion within the medullary cavity with chondroid calcification, most commonly in the short tubular bones of the hand.
Fibrous dysplasia on X-ray shows a ground-glass appearance in the medullary cavity with cortical thinning and bowing deformity.
Periosteal reaction types on X-ray: solid (benign), interrupted (aggressive), sunburst (aggressive malignancy), laminated (onion-skin, Ewing's sarcoma), and Codman's triangle (aggressive).
Looser zones (pseudofractures) on X-ray are narrow transverse lucencies through the cortex perpendicular to the long axis of bone, seen in osteomalacia.
Rickets on X-ray shows widening, splaying, and cupping of the metaphyses of long bones due to unmineralized osteoid.
Fracture description on X-ray requires: bone involved, location (proximal/mid/distal, diaphysis/metaphysis/epiphysis), pattern (transverse/oblique/spiral/comminuted), displacement, angulation, and shortening.
Growth plate fractures in children are classified by the Salter-Harris classification: type I (through growth plate only), type II (through growth plate and metaphysis), type III (through growth plate and epiphysis), type IV (through all three), type V (crush of growth plate).
Salter-Harris type II fractures are the most common (75%) and have a good prognosis; type V are the most serious as they damage the germinal layer.
Toddler's fracture is an oblique fracture of the distal tibia in children aged 9 months to 3 years from minor twisting injury; it may be invisible initially on X-ray.
Greenstick fracture is an incomplete fracture where one cortex breaks and the other bends; it is seen in children due to the greater bone plasticity.
Buckle (torus) fracture of the distal radius is the most common pediatric fracture, caused by axial compression leading to cortical buckling.
Nursemaid's elbow (radial head subluxation) is diagnosed clinically in children; X-ray is performed only to exclude fracture.
Posterior fat pad sign on lateral elbow X-ray indicates intra-articular effusion and in the context of trauma strongly suggests an occult fracture, typically radial head fracture.
The anterior fat pad (sail sign) is visible normally; elevation or displacement of the anterior fat pad indicates joint effusion.
Colles' fracture is an extra-articular distal radius fracture with dorsal displacement and angulation; it is the most common fracture in adults over 50.
Smith's fracture (reverse Colles') involves volar displacement and angulation of the distal radius fragment.
Scaphoid fractures are at risk of avascular necrosis and nonunion due to the retrograde blood supply; the proximal pole is most vulnerable.
Bennett's fracture is an intra-articular fracture of the base of the first metacarpal with subluxation of the carpometacarpal joint.
Rolando's fracture is a comminuted intra-articular fracture of the base of the first metacarpal.
Gamekeeper's (skier's) thumb is a tear of the ulnar collateral ligament of the thumb MCP joint; stress views or MRI confirm the diagnosis.
Hip fractures are classified as intracapsular (femoral neck) or extracapsular (intertrochanteric, subtrochanteric); intracapsular fractures risk avascular necrosis.
The Garden classification grades femoral neck fractures: I (incomplete valgus impacted), II (complete undisplaced), III (complete partially displaced), IV (complete fully displaced).
Stress fractures on plain X-ray may be invisible initially; bone scan or MRI detects them early as periosteal reaction or marrow edema.
March fracture is a stress fracture of the second or third metatarsal neck from repetitive loading; it is common in military recruits and athletes.
Lisfranc injury is a fracture-dislocation of the tarsometatarsal joints; a fleck fracture at the base of the second metatarsal on plain X-ray is pathognomonic.
Calcaneal fracture on lateral X-ray shows reduction in Böhler's angle below 20 degrees; CT is used for surgical planning.
Böhler's angle is measured between the posterior facet and the anterior process of the calcaneus; normal is 20-40 degrees.
Ottawa Ankle Rules: X-ray is required if there is tenderness at the posterior aspect of the fibula or medial malleolus or inability to weight-bear.
Ottawa Foot Rules: X-ray required if there is tenderness at the base of the fifth metatarsal or navicular or inability to weight-bear.
Maisonneuve fracture is a proximal fibular fracture with disruption of the ankle syndesmosis and deltoid ligament; X-ray of the full fibula is essential.
Tillaux fracture is an avulsion fracture of the anterolateral distal tibial epiphysis in adolescents from a Salter-Harris III mechanism.
Triplane fracture is a complex distal tibial physeal fracture in adolescents with components in three planes; CT is required to characterize.
Knee effusion on lateral X-ray is identified by a suprapatellar soft tissue shadow above the normal prefemoral fat pad; a lipohemarthrosis (fat-fluid level) indicates intra-articular fracture.
Segond fracture is an avulsion fracture of the lateral tibial plateau (capsular sign) associated with ACL and meniscal tears.
Tibial plateau fracture on plain X-ray may be subtle; CT is required to assess depression and comminution for surgical planning.
MRI of the knee is the gold standard for soft tissue injury assessment: ACL, PCL, menisci, and collateral ligaments.
ACL tear on MRI appears as discontinuity of the ligament, edema, bone bruising of the lateral femoral condyle and posterolateral tibial plateau (pivot shift contusions).
Medial meniscus tears on MRI appear as linear signal reaching the articular surface on fluid-sensitive sequences.
Bucket handle meniscal tear on MRI shows the displaced inner fragment within the intercondylar notch (double PCL sign) or displaced medially (double delta sign).
Patellar dislocation on X-ray may show lateral displacement; bone bruising of the medial patellar facet and lateral femoral condyle on MRI confirms the mechanism.
Shoulder X-ray in two planes (AP and axillary or Y-view) is required to assess for fracture and to confirm reduction post-dislocation.
Hill-Sachs lesion is a posterolateral humeral head compression fracture from anterior dislocation of the shoulder; it is best seen on AP X-ray in internal rotation.
Bankart lesion is an avulsion of the anterior glenoid labrum and capsule from anterior shoulder dislocation; MRI arthrography is the imaging of choice.
HAGL lesion (humeral avulsion of the glenohumeral ligament) is an avulsion at the humeral insertion of the inferior glenohumeral ligament, detected on MRI arthrography.
Rotator cuff tears on MRI appear as discontinuity or full-thickness signal abnormality within the tendon; supraspinatus is the most commonly torn.
Calcific tendinitis of the rotator cuff on plain X-ray shows amorphous calcium deposits in the supraspinatus tendon.
Acromioclavicular joint dislocation is graded I-VI by the Rockwood classification based on degree of ligamentous disruption on stress views.
Sternoclavicular joint dislocation is rare; posterior dislocation is life-threatening due to proximity of mediastinal structures and requires CT for assessment.
Mammography is the gold standard screening modality for breast cancer, recommended annually or biennially from age 40-50 depending on guidelines.
BI-RADS (Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System) classifies breast findings from 0 (incomplete) to 6 (known malignancy); category 4 and 5 require biopsy.
Microcalcifications on mammography are suspicious when they are grouped, fine pleomorphic, or linear/branching; these patterns suggest DCIS or malignancy.
Breast MRI is more sensitive than mammography for lobular carcinoma, multifocal disease, contralateral breast assessment, and screening in high-risk women.
Breast ultrasound characterizes lesions as cystic or solid; parallel orientation, circumscribed margins, and homogeneous echotexture favor benignity.
Spiculated margins, non-parallel orientation, posterior acoustic shadowing, and irregular shape on breast ultrasound are features of malignancy.
Stereotactic biopsy is used for microcalcifications visible on mammography but not on ultrasound; MRI-guided biopsy is used for lesions visible only on MRI.
Sentinel lymph node biopsy for axillary staging uses radiotracer and blue dye injection; lymphoscintigraphy confirms sentinel node location.
Ovarian cysts on ultrasound are characterized by the IOTA classification: simple cysts (anechoic, thin wall) are almost always benign; complex cysts require further assessment.
Complex ovarian cysts with solid components, thick septations, internal vascularity, or ascites on ultrasound raise concern for malignancy.
CA-125 combined with transvaginal ultrasound is used for ovarian cancer risk stratification using the Risk of Malignancy Index.
Dermoid cyst (mature teratoma) on ultrasound shows echogenic fat and calcification (Rokitansky nodule) with posterior acoustic shadowing.
Endometrioma (chocolate cyst) on ultrasound appears as a cyst with uniform low-level internal echoes ('ground glass') and no solid components.
Ectopic pregnancy on transvaginal ultrasound is confirmed by visualizing an adnexal mass or gestational sac outside the uterine cavity; an empty uterus with beta-hCG above 1500-2000 IU/L suggests ectopic pregnancy.
Nuchal translucency measurement on ultrasound at 11-14 weeks gestation screens for chromosomal abnormalities; thickness above 3 mm increases risk.
Placenta previa is diagnosed on transvaginal ultrasound when the placenta covers the internal cervical os; it is confirmed after 28 weeks.
Placental abruption on ultrasound may show a retroplacental hematoma; a negative ultrasound does not exclude abruption.
Doppler velocimetry of the umbilical artery assesses fetal circulation; absent or reversed end-diastolic flow indicates placental insufficiency and fetal compromise.
The biophysical profile combines fetal breathing, movement, tone, amniotic fluid volume, and NST to assess fetal well-being; a score of 8-10 is reassuring.
Fetal growth restriction is defined as estimated fetal weight below the 10th percentile; serial ultrasound measurements assess interval growth.
Vasa previa on Doppler ultrasound shows fetal vessels crossing the internal os; it is a critical diagnosis as membrane rupture can cause fatal hemorrhage.
Fluoroscopy is used for real-time imaging of swallowing, barium studies, joint arthrography, and catheter guidance.
Barium swallow identifies esophageal motility disorders, hiatus hernia, strictures, and esophageal carcinoma; water-soluble contrast is used if perforation is suspected.
Achalasia on barium swallow shows smooth tapering of the distal esophagus (bird-beak sign) with proximal esophageal dilatation.
Zenker's diverticulum on barium swallow appears as a posterior pharyngoesophageal outpouching at Killian's dehiscence.
Small bowel follow-through or CT enterography assesses Crohn's disease, showing mural thickening, skip lesions, cobblestoning, and fistulae.
The string sign on barium follow-through in Crohn's disease represents a severely narrowed terminal ileum.
Barium enema has largely been replaced by CT colonography and colonoscopy; water-soluble contrast enema is used to assess anastomotic integrity.
CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy) detects polyps above 6 mm with sensitivity comparable to optical colonoscopy.
Intravenous urogram (IVU) has been largely replaced by CT urogram; CT is more sensitive for calculi and renal masses.
VCUG (voiding cystourethrogram) is performed in children with UTI to detect vesicoureteral reflux; reflux is graded I-V by the International Reflux Classification.
Hysterosalpingography uses fluoroscopy with uterine contrast instillation to assess tubal patency in infertility evaluation.
Fistulogram uses water-soluble contrast injected through a sinus tract to delineate the extent and communication of fistulae.
Digital subtraction angiography remains the gold standard for cerebral vascular imaging and allows simultaneous treatment of lesions.
CT angiography of the coronary arteries (CTCA) assesses coronary artery disease non-invasively; calcium scoring identifies subclinical atherosclerosis.
Coronary artery calcium scoring (Agatston score) above 400 indicates severe coronary artery disease and guides statin therapy and further investigation.
MR angiography uses the inherent signal of flowing blood to image vessels without ionizing radiation; it is used for peripheral arterial disease and renal artery assessment.
Carotid Doppler ultrasound measures internal carotid artery stenosis; peak systolic velocity above 125 cm/s corresponds to stenosis above 50%.
NASCET criteria measure carotid stenosis as percentage reduction in diameter at the maximum stenosis compared to the normal distal ICA.
Deep vein thrombosis on venous duplex ultrasound is confirmed by non-compressibility of the vein, echogenic thrombus, and absent flow on color Doppler.
Ankle-brachial pressure index below 0.9 indicates peripheral arterial disease; below 0.5 indicates severe ischemia.
CT angiography of the lower limb arteries shows the level and extent of arterial occlusion and guides revascularization planning.
Interventional radiology procedures include percutaneous drainage of abscesses, biliary intervention, nephrostomy insertion, embolization, and TIPSS.
Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPSS) reduces portal hypertension by creating a shunt between the portal and hepatic veins under fluoroscopic guidance.
Uterine artery embolization treats symptomatic fibroids by occluding blood supply with embolic particles under fluoroscopic guidance.
Radiofrequency ablation, microwave ablation, and cryoablation are image-guided thermal ablation techniques used for liver, kidney, and lung tumors.
Vertebroplasty and kyphoplasty are fluoroscopy-guided procedures that inject bone cement into osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures to relieve pain.
PET-MRI combines the metabolic information of PET with the superior soft tissue resolution of MRI; it is emerging for oncological staging.
Dual-energy CT can separate different materials based on their attenuation at two energies; it is used to characterize renal calculi composition and detect gout.
Spectral CT (photon-counting CT) provides energy-resolved imaging, enabling virtual monoenergetic images, material decomposition, and improved lesion characterization.
Cone beam CT (CBCT) is used in dentistry, orthopedics, and ENT to provide high-resolution 3D imaging with lower radiation than conventional CT.
Flat-panel detector CT in angiography suites provides high-resolution roadmap images during interventional procedures.
Radiation dose optimization in CT uses automatic tube current modulation, iterative reconstruction, and low-dose protocols to reduce patient exposure.
CT radiation dose is measured in CTDIvol (volume CT dose index) and DLP (dose-length product); effective dose in mSv is estimated from DLP.
The ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) guides radiation protection: justification, optimization, and dose limits.
Pediatric CT protocols use weight-based and age-based dose reduction to minimize radiation exposure in children who are more radiosensitive.
MRI is contraindicated in patients with non-MRI-compatible pacemakers, cochlear implants, and certain metallic implants; compatibility must be verified before scanning.
Gadolinium-based MRI contrast agents are associated with nephrogenic systemic fibrosis in patients with severe renal impairment (eGFR below 30).
Macrocyclic gadolinium agents (gadobutrol, gadoteridol) have a lower risk of gadolinium deposition than linear agents.
Iodinated CT contrast agents can cause acute hypersensitivity reactions (mild, moderate, severe) and contrast-induced nephropathy.
Pre-medication with corticosteroids (methylprednisolone) and antihistamines is used to reduce risk of contrast reaction in patients with prior moderate reactions.
Contrast-induced nephropathy is minimized by IV hydration with normal saline before and after contrast administration in at-risk patients.
Metformin is held before and 48 hours after iodinated contrast administration in patients with renal impairment to prevent lactic acidosis.
Ultrasound uses sound waves at frequencies above 2 MHz; image resolution increases with frequency but depth of penetration decreases.
B-mode (brightness mode) ultrasound provides real-time 2D grayscale imaging; M-mode (motion mode) tracks movement over time.
Color Doppler maps the direction and velocity of blood flow; power Doppler is more sensitive to slow flow but does not show direction.
Spectral Doppler waveforms characterize arterial (pulsatile, systolic peak) and venous (continuous, phasic) flow patterns.
Acoustic shadowing on ultrasound occurs posterior to highly echogenic structures (calculi, calcification, gas); it confirms calcification.
Posterior acoustic enhancement (through transmission) occurs behind fluid-filled structures such as cysts and confirms fluid content.
Reverberation artifact on ultrasound produces repeated parallel echoes posterior to a highly reflective interface such as bowel gas or a metallic foreign body.
Ring-down artifact on ultrasound consists of a continuous streak of echoes posterior to gas bubbles and is used to identify pneumobilia.
Elastography measures tissue stiffness using ultrasound or MRI; it is used to assess liver fibrosis and characterize breast and thyroid nodules.
Contrast-enhanced ultrasound (CEUS) uses microbubble contrast agents to characterize liver lesions and assess perfusion without radiation.
TIRADS (Thyroid Imaging Reporting and Data System) classifies thyroid nodules on ultrasound from TR1 (benign) to TR5 (highly suspicious); FNA is guided by TIRADS category and nodule size.
Features of malignancy in thyroid nodules on ultrasound: taller-than-wide shape, hypoechogenicity, irregular margins, microcalcifications, and extrathyroidal extension.
Parathyroid adenoma on ultrasound appears as an oval hypoechoic structure posterior to the thyroid gland; sestamibi scintigraphy confirms the diagnosis.
Salivary gland calculi on ultrasound appear as echogenic foci with posterior shadowing within the ductal system; the submandibular gland is most commonly affected.
Lymph node malignancy on ultrasound is suggested by rounded shape (L:T ratio below 2), loss of fatty hilum, peripheral vascularity, and internal necrosis.
Reactive lymph nodes are oval with a preserved echogenic hilum and hilar vascularity on Doppler ultrasound.
Musculoskeletal ultrasound can dynamically assess tendons, muscles, joints, and peripheral nerves; it guides injection procedures.
Carpal tunnel syndrome on ultrasound shows increased cross-sectional area of the median nerve above 10 mm2 at the level of the pisiform.
Deep vein thrombosis in the upper limb (subclavian, axillary, brachial vein) is detected by venous duplex ultrasound and may require anticoagulation.
Ultrasound-guided procedures include central venous access, thoracentesis, paracentesis, abscess drainage, and joint aspiration, reducing complication rates.
Interventional ultrasound with real-time needle guidance reduces complication rates and improves first-pass success for vascular access.
Pediatric abdominal ultrasound evaluates appendicitis, pyloric stenosis, intussusception, and renal pathology as first-line imaging due to absence of radiation.
Intussusception on ultrasound shows a target sign (concentric rings of bowel within bowel) in transverse section and a pseudokidney sign in longitudinal section.
Pyloric stenosis on ultrasound is confirmed by a pyloric muscle thickness above 3-4 mm, channel length above 17 mm, and non-passage of fluid.
Neonatal brain ultrasound through the anterior fontanelle is used for screening for intraventricular hemorrhage in premature infants.
Grade IV intraventricular hemorrhage (periventricular hemorrhagic infarction) in neonates on ultrasound shows asymmetric periventricular hyperechogenicity with ventricular dilatation.
Congenital hip dysplasia is assessed by the Graf classification on hip ultrasound: alpha angle above 60 degrees is normal; below 43 degrees indicates dysplasia.
Fetal anomaly scan at 18-20 weeks evaluates fetal anatomy including brain, face, spine, heart, abdominal wall, kidneys, and limbs.
Skeletal dysplasias in utero are suspected on ultrasound by short long bones, abnormal bone echogenicity, and abnormal head shape or skull ossification.
Nuclear medicine bone scan uses 99mTc-MDP to detect osteoblastic activity; whole-body scan screens for bone metastases.
Gallium-67 and 18F-FDG PET are used to detect active infection and inflammation; gallium accumulates in areas of increased metabolic activity.
SPECT (single photon emission computed tomography) provides 3D functional imaging; SPECT-CT fuses functional and anatomical data.
Myocardial perfusion scintigraphy (MIBI scan) assesses myocardial viability and perfusion under stress and rest conditions; fixed defects indicate infarction, reversible defects indicate ischemia.
Thyroid scintigraphy with 99mTc or 123I assesses thyroid function; hot nodules (autonomous) are rarely malignant; cold nodules carry a 10-15% risk of malignancy.
Whole-body 131I scan after thyroidectomy detects residual thyroid tissue and distant metastases in differentiated thyroid carcinoma.
Hepatobiliary scintigraphy (HIDA scan) assesses bile duct patency and gallbladder function; failure of isotope to enter the gallbladder suggests acute cholecystitis.
Meckel's diverticulum scintigraphy uses 99mTc-pertechnetate, which is taken up by ectopic gastric mucosa; it is most sensitive in children with rectal bleeding.
Renography with MAG3 or DTPA assesses differential renal function and drainage; an obstructed pattern on furosemide renography suggests ureteropelvic junction obstruction.
Lymphoscintigraphy maps the lymphatic drainage from a tumor site and identifies sentinel lymph nodes for biopsy in breast cancer and melanoma.
Octreotide scintigraphy and 68Ga-DOTATATE PET-CT detect somatostatin receptor-positive neuroendocrine tumors.
68Ga-PSMA PET-CT is highly sensitive for prostate cancer staging and detection of biochemical recurrence.
18F-DOPA PET is used for neurological applications including Parkinson's disease assessment and evaluation of movement disorders.
Interventional radiology in trauma includes hepatic, splenic, pelvic, and renal angioembolization for solid organ injury and pelvic fracture hemorrhage.
TIPSS reduces portal pressure and treats variceal hemorrhage and refractory ascites; it is guided by fluoroscopy with real-time venous pressure measurement.
IVC filter placement prevents pulmonary embolism in patients with DVT who cannot be anticoagulated; retrievable filters are preferred.
Chest drain insertion under CT or fluoroscopic guidance is used for complex or loculated pleural collections requiring accurate drain positioning.
Percutaneous nephrostomy is performed under ultrasound or fluoroscopic guidance to relieve ureteral obstruction and allow access for subsequent intervention.
Biliary drainage is performed percutaneously under fluoroscopic guidance in patients with obstructive jaundice who cannot undergo ERCP.
CT-guided biopsy of deep lesions provides tissue diagnosis with a complication rate below 5% for pneumothorax and bleeding.
Radiofrequency ablation of liver tumors achieves local tumor control rates of 85-90% for tumors below 3 cm; MRI or CT at 1 month confirms ablation completeness.
Prostatic artery embolization reduces prostatic volume and lower urinary tract symptoms in benign prostatic hyperplasia and is an alternative to surgery.
Fallopian tube recanalization is a fluoroscopy-guided procedure to treat proximal tubal occlusion in infertility; patency is confirmed by contrast passage.
Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC) delineates biliary anatomy before surgery or ERCP, particularly in complex biliary strictures or stones.
Spinal angiography identifies the artery of Adamkiewicz and the spinal cord vasculature before aortic surgery to prevent cord ischemia.
ERCP under fluoroscopy allows sphincterotomy, stone extraction, and stent placement in the bile duct and pancreatic duct.
Gastrostomy insertion under fluoroscopic or ultrasound guidance provides enteral access for patients who cannot swallow.
Intracranial pressure monitoring and CSF drainage can be performed under CT guidance with stereotactic precision.
Image-guided stereotactic biopsy of brain lesions is performed using CT or MRI coordinates to sample tissue from inaccessible deep lesions.
Gamma knife radiosurgery delivers precisely focused radiation to intracranial targets using MRI-based planning; it is used for brain metastases, meningioma, and AVM.
Stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) delivers high-dose radiation to extracranial targets such as lung, liver, and spine tumors using image guidance.
4D-CT incorporates respiratory motion information and is used in radiotherapy planning for thoracic and abdominal tumors to account for breathing movement.
MRI-guided radiotherapy uses real-time MRI to track tumor motion and adapt treatment delivery, improving precision and reducing toxicity.
Spectral Doppler of the hepatic artery after liver transplantation assesses patency and hepatic artery stenosis or thrombosis.
Renal transplant ultrasound with Doppler is performed on day 1 post-transplant and at follow-up to detect vascular complications, rejection, and hydronephrosis.
Resistive index (RI) on renal Doppler above 0.8 in a renal transplant suggests elevated intrarenal resistance from rejection, venous thrombosis, or obstruction.
Ovarian torsion on ultrasound shows an enlarged ovary, absent or reduced Doppler flow, and peripheral follicles; normal Doppler does not exclude torsion.
Testicular torsion on scrotal Doppler ultrasound shows absent intratesticular blood flow; surgical exploration must not be delayed for equivocal findings.
Epididymitis on scrotal Doppler shows increased blood flow in the epididymis with or without orchitis.
Hydrocele on ultrasound shows anechoic fluid surrounding the testis in the tunica vaginalis.
Varicocele on Doppler ultrasound is identified by dilated pampiniform plexus veins above 3 mm with reflux on Valsalva maneuver.
Penile Doppler ultrasound assesses erectile dysfunction by measuring peak systolic velocity and end-diastolic velocity in the cavernosal arteries during pharmacological erection.
Ultrasound contrast is used for CEUS of the liver, kidneys, and bladder, providing real-time enhancement information similar to CT without radiation.
Fusion imaging combines ultrasound in real time with pre-acquired CT or MRI data, allowing ultrasound-guided biopsy of lesions seen only on cross-sectional imaging.
Positron emission tomography relies on annihilation radiation from positron-emitting radioisotopes; coincidence detection produces tomographic images of metabolic activity.
FDG-PET reflects glucose metabolism; malignant cells with increased glycolysis show high FDG uptake; areas of active inflammation also show FDG uptake.
Physiological FDG uptake is seen in the brain, heart, kidneys, ureters, bladder, and bowel, which may obscure adjacent lesions on PET-CT.
FDG-PET is used for initial staging, restaging after treatment, and assessment of treatment response in lymphoma, non-small cell lung cancer, and colorectal cancer.
Deauville criteria use a 5-point scale comparing FDG uptake in lymphoma to mediastinal blood pool and liver; score 4-5 at interim assessment predicts treatment failure.
FDG-PET can detect unknown primary tumors in patients presenting with metastatic cancer when conventional imaging is negative.
Bone marrow involvement in lymphoma is detected by PET-CT without the need for bone marrow biopsy in PET-avid lymphomas.
PET-CT distinguishes viable tumor from radiation necrosis or scar tissue in treated brain tumors, complementing MRI.
Cardiac PET with 82Rb or 13N-ammonia assesses myocardial perfusion; 18F-FDG PET identifies viable myocardium in ischemic cardiomyopathy.
Amyloid PET (18F-florbetapir, florbetaben) detects brain amyloid plaques and supports diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease.
Tau PET tracers image neurofibrillary tau pathology and help differentiate Alzheimer's from other tauopathies.
Dopamine transporter (DaTscan) SPECT distinguishes Parkinson's disease from essential tremor; reduced striatal uptake confirms dopaminergic deficit.
MIBG (131I or 123I metaiodobenzylguanidine) scintigraphy detects pheochromocytoma, paraganglioma, and neuroblastoma with high specificity.
Sentinel node lymphoscintigraphy with 99mTc-nanocolloidal albumin maps lymphatic drainage in breast cancer and melanoma for sentinel node biopsy.
Radio-guided surgery uses an intraoperative gamma probe to localize sentinel lymph nodes or previously marked lesions for surgical excision.
Theranostics pairs a diagnostic radiotracer with a therapeutic radioligand targeting the same receptor; 177Lu-DOTATATE treats neuroendocrine tumors after 68Ga-DOTATATE PET staging.
177Lu-PSMA therapy targets PSMA-overexpressing prostate cancer cells; response is assessed by 68Ga-PSMA PET and PSA kinetics.
Selective internal radiation therapy (SIRT) with 90Y-microspheres treats hepatocellular carcinoma and colorectal liver metastases via hepatic artery administration.
Imaging of the pediatric airway by lateral neck X-ray shows the steeple sign (subglottic narrowing) in croup and the thumb sign (epiglottic swelling) in epiglottitis.
Hypertrophic pyloric stenosis on upper GI contrast study shows a string sign (thin channel of barium) and shoulder sign; ultrasound is preferred and avoids radiation.
Abdominal X-ray in a neonate with bilious vomiting and a double bubble sign suggests duodenal atresia or stenosis; the two bubbles represent the stomach and proximal duodenum.
Triple bubble sign on neonatal X-ray suggests jejunal atresia with three dilated bowel loops.
Hirschsprung's disease on contrast enema shows a transition zone between dilated proximal colon and narrowed aganglionic segment with a rectosigmoid ratio below 1.
Necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC) on abdominal X-ray shows distended bowel loops, pneumatosis intestinalis, portal venous gas, and pneumoperitoneum in severe cases.
Malrotation with midgut volvulus on upper GI contrast study shows the duodenojejunal junction (ligament of Treitz) displaced to the right of the spine.
Ultrasound of the neonatal spine screens for spinal dysraphism when skin stigmata are present; MRI is the definitive investigation for cord tethering.
DMSA renal scan is the gold standard for assessing renal cortical scarring in children following pyelonephritis.
MAG3 diuretic renography in children evaluates obstruction at the ureteropelvic or ureterovesical junction in hydronephrosis detected on antenatal ultrasound.
MR enterography is preferred over CT enterography in children and young patients with inflammatory bowel disease due to absence of ionizing radiation.
Abdominal and pelvic MRI in rectal cancer provides accurate T and N staging, assesses circumferential resection margin, and identifies extramural vascular invasion.
MR rectal cancer staging: T1 (confined to mucosa/submucosa), T2 (into muscularis propria), T3 (beyond muscularis propria), T4 (adjacent organ invasion).
Circumferential resection margin involvement on rectal MRI predicts local recurrence; a clear margin of 1 mm or more is the surgical goal.
MRCP is the primary investigation for primary sclerosing cholangitis, showing multifocal biliary strictures with beading and dilatation of intrahepatic and extrahepatic ducts.
Choledochal cysts are classified by Todani classification types I-V; type I (fusiform dilatation of CBD) is most common and is at risk of cholangiocarcinoma.
Cholangiocarcinoma on MRCP appears as a biliary stricture; MRI shows periductal enhancement; CA19-9 is elevated; biopsy confirms the diagnosis.
Autoimmune pancreatitis on CT shows a sausage-shaped pancreas with a halo of fibroinflammatory tissue; IgG4 levels are elevated in type 1.
Groove pancreatitis on MRI shows a mass-forming lesion in the groove between the head of pancreas, duodenum, and common bile duct; it mimics pancreatic cancer.
Intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm (IPMN) on MRCP shows dilatation of the main pancreatic duct or branch ducts with communication; high-risk features include main duct involvement and solid components.
Mucinous cystic neoplasm of the pancreas on CT shows a multilocular cystic mass with thick septations in the body or tail in middle-aged women; it has malignant potential.
Serous cystadenoma of the pancreas on CT shows a microcystic lesion (honeycomb pattern) with or without a central scar; it is almost always benign.
CT colonography preparation requires bowel cleansing and fecal tagging; supine and prone positions are acquired to differentiate feces from polyps.
Polyps on CT colonography are characterized by size (above 6 mm requires follow-up, above 10 mm requires colonoscopy), morphology, and density.
Sessile serrated adenoma on CT colonography may appear as a flat lesion that is difficult to detect; prone position and window leveling improve detection.
CT enteroclysis involves direct intubation of the jejunum with catheter and contrast infusion; it provides superior distension of the small bowel compared to oral CT enterography.
Wireless capsule endoscopy is used for small bowel evaluation of obscure GI bleeding, Crohn's disease, and small bowel tumors; it provides luminal images without radiation.
