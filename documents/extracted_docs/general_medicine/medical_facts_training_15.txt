First Aid: Procedures and Treatments (150 Facts)
Always wear disposable gloves before touching a victim's open wound to prevent cross-contamination.
If gloves are unavailable, use a clean plastic bag or multiple layers of cloth as a barrier.
Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water immediately after providing first aid care.
Clean minor wounds with cool running water and mild soap for at least 5 minutes.
Pat dry a minor wound with a sterile gauze pad rather than rubbing it to avoid further tissue damage.
Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment to minor cuts only if the victim is not allergic.
Change dressings daily or more frequently if they become wet or dirty.
Treat a nosebleed by having the person sit up and tilt their head slightly forward to prevent swallowing blood.
Pinch the nostrils together firmly for 10 full minutes without releasing to check the progress.
Apply a cold compress to the bridge of the nose to help constrict blood vessels and slow bleeding.
Do not blow the nose for several hours after a nosebleed has stopped.
For an object stuck in the ear, do not use a pin or wire to poke at it; tilt the head to let it fall out.
If an insect is in the ear, use a small amount of baby oil or olive oil to float it out if the eardrum is not perforated.
For an object stuck in the eye, encourage the victim to blink several times to see if tears wash it away.
If an eye object is visible on the white part, gently move it toward the corner with a damp cotton swab.
Treat a chemical splash in the eye by flushing from the inner corner to the outer corner with clean water.
Keep the unaffected eye closed during flushing to prevent cross-contamination.
Apply a cold pack to a closed fracture or sprain for 20 minutes every hour to reduce swelling.
Place a thin cloth between a cold pack and the skin to prevent frostbite-like skin damage.
Elevate an injured limb above the level of the heart to decrease pressure and swelling.
Immobilize a suspected dislocation in the position it was found using pillows or folded blankets.
Do not attempt to pop a dislocated joint back into place, as this can damage nerves and blood vessels.
Use a figure-eight bandage technique to support a sprained ankle or wrist.
Check for a distal pulse (like at the wrist or foot) before and after applying any bandage.
Treat a suspected rib fracture by having the person hold a pillow against the painful area to support breathing.
Do not wrap the chest tightly for a rib fracture, as this can prevent the lungs from expanding fully.
For a severe allergic reaction, help the victim use their prescribed auto-injector immediately.
Massage the injection site for 10 seconds after using an epinephrine auto-injector to speed absorption.
Keep the victim lying down with legs elevated after an epinephrine injection to manage potential shock.
For an asthma attack, help the person sit upright and lean forward slightly to ease breathing.
Encourage slow, deep breaths and assist with the administration of their rescue inhaler.
If an inhaler is used, have the person hold their breath for 10 seconds after inhalation.
Treat hyperventilation by coaching the person to breathe slowly through pursed lips.
Provide sips of water to a conscious victim of heat exhaustion once they are in a cool environment.
Fan the victim and spray them with lukewarm water to encourage evaporative cooling.
For severe heatstroke, apply ice packs to the armpits, groin, and neck areas.
Remove a person from a cold environment and replace wet clothing with dry, warm blankets for hypothermia.
Offer warm, sweet, non-alcoholic drinks to a conscious person suffering from mild hypothermia.
Do not rub or massage the skin of a person with severe hypothermia, as it can cause cardiac arrest.
Treat frostbite by soaking the affected area in warm water (100-104 degrees Fahrenheit) for 20-30 minutes.
Discontinue rewarming frostbite once the skin becomes soft and sensation returns.
Loosely wrap rewarmned frostbitten fingers or toes with sterile gauze, keeping them separated.
For a diabetic person who is confused, give 15-20 grams of fast-acting sugar like honey or fruit juice.
Wait 15 minutes after giving sugar to see if the person's condition improves (the 15-15 rule).
If a seizure occurs, clear the area and place something soft under the person's head.
Note the start and end time of a seizure to provide accurate information to paramedics.
Do not put anything in the mouth during a seizure, including your fingers or medicine.
For a person who faints, loosen tight clothing around the neck and waist.
If a person feels faint, have them sit and put their head between their knees or lie down.
Treat an animal bite by washing it with soap and water for at least 10 to 15 minutes.
Apply a sterile dressing to an animal bite and advise the victim to seek medical care for rabies risk.
Remove a bee stinger by scraping it away with a credit card to avoid squeezing more venom into the skin.
Apply a paste of baking soda and water to a bee sting to help neutralize the pain.
Treat a jellyfish sting by rinsing the area with vinegar for at least 30 seconds to stop venom release.
If vinegar is unavailable, use salt water to rinse a jellyfish sting; never use fresh water.
Carefully remove tentacles from a jellyfish sting using tweezers or a gloved hand.
For a snake bite, keep the victim still and calm to slow the spread of venom.
Pressure immobilization bandages are used specifically for elapid snake bites (like coral snakes).
Mark the edge of the swelling on a snake bite with a pen and note the time.
Treat a blister by leaving it intact; if it bursts, clean it with soap and water and cover it.
Apply a 'doughnut' shaped moleskin pad around a blister to prevent further friction.
For a knocked-out permanent tooth, touch only the crown and rinse it gently with water if dirty.
Try to place the tooth back into the socket and have the person bite down on gauze.
If the tooth cannot be reinserted, store it in a container of milk or the victim's saliva.
Treat a minor burn by holding it under cool running water until the pain subsides.
Cover a burn with a loose sterile dressing to keep air out and reduce pain.
For a chemical burn to the skin, remove all contaminated jewelry and clothing while flushing.
Treat a brush burn (road rash) as a combination of a scrape and a heat burn.
For a puncture wound, do not pull out large impaled objects; stabilize them with bulky dressings.
Clean the area around a puncture wound but do not flush the wound itself deeply.
Treat a tick bite by removing the tick with tweezers, pulling steadily and directly upward.
Disinfect the tick bite area and your hands with alcohol or soap and water after removal.
For an infant with a high fever, remove excess clothing and provide a lukewarm sponge bath.
Do not use alcohol rubs to cool down a child or infant with a fever.
Treat a suspected poisoning by calling the poison control center before taking any action.
Do not give ipecac syrup or induce vomiting for poison ingestion unless specifically instructed.
If a poison was inhaled, move the victim to fresh air immediately and start CPR if needed.
Treat a conscious choking adult with 5 back blows followed by 5 abdominal thrusts.
For a choking pregnant or obese person, perform chest thrusts in the center of the breastbone.
Treat an unconscious choking victim by starting CPR, beginning with chest compressions.
For a drowning victim, prioritize rescue breaths and ensure the airway is clear of water or vomit.
Use an AED as soon as it is available for any victim of sudden cardiac arrest.
Ensure the chest is dry before applying AED pads to a victim pulled from water.
Apply direct pressure to a severe wound using the cleanest material available.
If blood soaks through a bandage, apply more pressure and more layers without removing the original.
Place a tourniquet 2 to 3 inches above a life-threatening limb wound, but not on a joint.
Tighten the tourniquet until the bleeding stops and the distal pulse is absent.
Secure an impaled object by using log-cabin style padding around the object before bandaging.
Treat a sucking chest wound by applying a sterile dressing and taping it on three sides.
Monitor a person with a chest seal for signs of worsening breathing, which may indicate a tension pneumothorax.
For an abdominal evisceration, cover the organs with a sterile dressing moistened with sterile water or saline.
Do not cover an evisceration with a dry dressing, as it will stick to the organs.
Treat an eye injury from a blunt object by applying a cold pack gently for 15 minutes.
Cover both eyes if one is severely injured to prevent movement of the affected eye.
Stabilize a suspected neck injury by placing your hands on both sides of the victim's head.
Do not move a victim with a spinal injury unless there is an immediate threat to life (like fire).
Treat a sprained joint with the RICE method: Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation.
Use a sling and swathe to completely immobilize a shoulder or upper arm injury.
For a fractured leg, use the uninjured leg as a splint by padding between them and tying them together.
Treat a scalp wound by applying direct pressure, but be gentle if a skull fracture is suspected.
If a skull fracture is suspected, apply pressure around the edges of the wound rather than directly over it.
Treat a victim of a lightning strike by checking for cardiac arrest and starting CPR immediately.
Victims of lightning strikes do not carry an electrical charge and are safe to touch.
For a jellyfish sting to the eye, flush with large amounts of saline or tap water only.
Treat a venomous spider bite by cleaning the wound and applying a cold pack to slow venom spread.
Keep the bite site of a venomous spider or snake at or below the level of the heart.
Treat an opioid overdose by administering naloxone and providing rescue breaths if they aren't breathing.
Continue to monitor an overdose victim after naloxone, as it may wear off before the opioid does.
For a person in shock, keep them lying flat on their back unless they are vomiting.
Cover a person in shock with a blanket to prevent heat loss and maintain body temperature.
Treat a minor toothache by rinsing with warm salt water and applying a cold compress to the cheek.
Do not place aspirin directly on the gums for a toothache, as it can cause a chemical burn.
For a broken finger, tape it to the adjacent healthy finger (buddy taping) with padding in between.
Treat a sunburn with cool compresses and moisturizing aloe vera gel.
Avoid using petroleum-based ointments on fresh burns, as they trap heat.
For a person with a high-voltage electric shock, do not approach until the power is confirmed off.
Treat a victim of an electric shock for burns at both the entry and exit points.
For a splinter, clean the area and remove it with sterilized tweezers by pulling at the same angle it entered.
If a splinter is deep or under a nail, seek professional medical help for removal.
Treat a bruised fingernail (subungual hematoma) by applying ice and elevating the hand.
For a person experiencing a panic attack, encourage them to focus on a single object or their breath.
Do not tell a person in a panic attack to 'calm down'; instead, acknowledge their fear and stay with them.
Treat a black eye by checking for vision changes and applying a cold pack gently.
For a person with low body temperature, remove any jewelry that may restrict circulation as they rewarm.
Treat a person with a suspected stroke by noting the time the symptoms first appeared.
Keep a stroke victim lying on their side (recovery position) if they have difficulty swallowing or are vomiting.
For an unconscious victim of unknown cause, check for a medical alert necklace or bracelet.
Treat a wound with an embedded glass shard by stabilizing the shard and not removing it.
For a victim of a fall, assess for head, neck, and back pain before allowing them to move.
Treat a friction burn from a rope or carpet by cleaning it gently and applying a dry sterile dressing.
For a minor allergy (like hay fever), help the person move away from the trigger and use an antihistamine.
Treat a person with suspected meningitis by keeping them in a dark, quiet room while waiting for help.
For an earache, have the person sit upright and apply a warm (not hot) cloth to the outside of the ear.
Treat a hand injury by removing rings and watches immediately before swelling occurs.
For a bruised muscle, avoid heavy activity for at least 48 hours to prevent further tearing.
Treat a minor scrape by washing from the center of the wound outward to move dirt away.
For a person who has inhaled smoke, move them to an area with fresh air and sit them upright.
Treat a victim of a suspected internal injury by keeping them still and monitoring for signs of shock.
For a person with a severe headache and stiff neck, treat as an emergency and do not give medication.
Treat a small cut on the lip by applying firm pressure with a clean cloth to stop the bleeding.
For a person who is hyperventilating, do not use a paper bag; use the pursed-lip breathing technique instead.
Treat a persistent hiccup by having the person sip ice-cold water or hold their breath briefly.
For a victim of a gas leak, move them to fresh air and do not turn on any lights or electronics.
Treat a person with a suspected broken jaw by gently supporting the jaw with a bandage wrapped over the head.
For a child who has swallowed a non-toxic object, monitor their breathing and seek medical advice.
Treat a blister caused by a chemical by flushing the area for 20 minutes and covering loosely.
For a person who is shivering uncontrollably, treat for hypothermia by adding layers of dry clothing.
Treat a victim of a fall from a significant height as a multi-trauma patient with potential spinal injury.
For a person with chest pain, have them rest in a comfortable position, usually sitting up with knees bent.
Treat a minor scratch from a pet by washing thoroughly and monitoring for signs of infection over the next week.
Always document the time and type of first aid provided to hand over to professional medical staff.
