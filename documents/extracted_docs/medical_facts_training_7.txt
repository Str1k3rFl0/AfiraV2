The first step in any emergency is to ensure the scene is safe for both the rescuer and the victim.
The 'DRSABCD' protocol stands for Danger, Response, Send for help, Airway, Breathing, CPR, and
Defibrillation.
To check for a response, talk to the person and gently squeeze their shoulders.
If a person is unconscious but breathing normally, place them in the Recovery Position.
The Recovery Position keeps the airway open and prevents choking on vomit or secretions.
For an adult, CPR involves chest compressions at a rate of 100-120 per minute.
The correct depth for adult chest compressions is at least 2 inches (5 cm) but no more than 2.4 inches (6 cm).
The ratio of compressions to rescue breaths in CPR for adults, children, and infants (single rescuer) is 30:2.
Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions to allow blood to flow back into the heart.
An Automated External Defibrillator (AED) should be used as soon as it is available.
Follow the voice prompts of the AED exactly; it will only deliver a shock if a shockable rhythm is detected.
Do not touch the victim while the AED is analyzing the heart rhythm or delivering a shock.
For a choking adult who is conscious, perform the Heimlich Maneuver (abdominal thrusts).
To perform abdominal thrusts, stand behind the person and pull inward and upward just above the navel.
If a choking person becomes unconscious, lower them to the ground and start CPR immediately.
For a choking infant, use a combination of 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts.
Never perform a blind finger sweep in the mouth of a choking person; only remove visible objects.
To control severe external bleeding, apply direct pressure to the wound with a clean cloth or bandage.
If bleeding does not stop with direct pressure, apply a tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound (not on a joint).
Once a tourniquet is applied, note the exact time of application and do not remove it.
For a minor burn, run cool (not cold) water over the area for at least 10-20 minutes.
Do not apply ice, butter, or ointments to a severe burn, as this can damage the tissue further.
Cover a burn loosely with a sterile, non-stick dressing or plastic wrap.
In the case of a chemical burn, flush the area with water for at least 20 minutes while removing contaminated
clothing.
Signs of a stroke can be remembered by the acronym FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty,
Time to call emergency services.
If you suspect a heart attack, have the person sit down, rest, and keep calm while waiting for help.
For a person having a seizure, clear the area of hard or sharp objects and do not restrain them.
Do not put anything in the mouth of someone having a seizure.
After a seizure, place the person in the recovery position to keep their airway clear.
Signs of anaphylaxis include swelling of the face/throat, hives, and difficulty breathing.
The primary treatment for anaphylaxis is the immediate administration of an Epinephrine Auto-Injector
(EpiPen).
Inject the EpiPen into the middle of the outer thigh and hold it for the recommended time (usually 3-10
seconds).
For a suspected neck or spinal injury, do not move the person unless they are in immediate danger.
Manually stabilize the head and neck in the position found to prevent further injury.
Heat exhaustion signs include heavy sweating, rapid pulse, and dizziness.
Treat heat exhaustion by moving the person to a cool place, loosening clothing, and providing sips of water.
Heatstroke is a medical emergency characterized by a body temperature above 104°F (40°C) and altered
mental state.
Cool a heatstroke victim rapidly using ice baths or cold wet towels until help arrives.
Hypothermia occurs when the body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C).
To treat hypothermia, move the person out of the cold, remove wet clothing, and wrap them in warm blankets.
Frostbite usually affects extremities like fingers, toes, and the nose; do not rub the affected area.
Rewarm frostbitten areas slowly using warm water (not hot) only if there is no risk of refreezing.
For a nosebleed, lean the person forward and pinch the soft part of the nose for 10-15 minutes.
If a person faints, lay them flat on their back and elevate their legs about 12 inches (30 cm).
Signs of shock include pale, cold, clammy skin, rapid breathing, and a weak pulse.
To treat shock, lay the person down, elevate their legs, and cover them with a blanket to maintain warmth.
For a suspected fracture, immobilize the limb using a splint in the position it was found.
Do not try to realign or push a bone back into place if it is protruding (open fracture).
A sprain is an injury to a ligament, while a strain is an injury to a muscle or tendon.
Treat minor sprains and strains with RICE: Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation.
For a snake bite, keep the person calm and the affected limb immobilized below the level of the heart.
Do not use a tourniquet, cut the wound, or attempt to suck out the venom from a snake bite.
If a tooth is knocked out, handle it by the crown (top), not the root, and keep it moist in milk or saliva.
For a person with low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) who is conscious, provide a sugary drink or food.
In the case of poisoning, identify the substance and call poison control immediately; do not induce vomiting
unless told to do so.
Electric shock victims should not be touched until the power source is turned off.
Assess for an exit wound in cases of high-voltage electrical injury.
Capillary refill time (pressing on a fingernail) should be less than 2 seconds to indicate good circulation.
Use a sterile dressing for wounds to prevent infection; if a bandage becomes soaked with blood, add more on
top.
Hyperventilation can often be managed by having the person breathe slowly or use a paper bag (if coached).
Asthma attacks require the use of a rescue inhaler (bronchodilator) immediately.
If someone is stung by a bee, scrape the stinger away with a flat surface (like a credit card) rather than using
tweezers.
For a diabetic emergency where you are unsure if sugar is high or low, always give sugar if the person is
conscious.
Signs of internal bleeding include bruising, pain, and symptoms of shock without visible blood loss.
A concussion is a mild traumatic brain injury; look for symptoms like confusion, headache, or vomiting.
Anyone who loses consciousness after a head injury should be evaluated by a professional immediately.
A tourniquet should be tightened until the bleeding stops completely and the distal pulse disappears.
Chest thrusts for a choking pregnant woman or very obese person are performed higher up on the
breastbone.
In drowning cases, start rescue breaths as soon as possible, even before reaching the shore if safe.
Frostnip is the precursor to frostbite and can be treated by simple rewarming with skin-to-skin contact.
Chemicals in the eyes should be flushed for at least 20 minutes from the inner corner outward.
A sucking chest wound should be covered with an occlusive dressing taped on three sides only.
Standard precautions (gloves, masks) should be used whenever possible to prevent the spread of
bloodborne pathogens.
Check for medical ID jewelry (bracelets/necklaces) on unconscious patients for vital health information.
Signs of a broken hip include one leg appearing shorter and rotated outward.
Pneumothorax symptoms include sudden sharp chest pain and one-sided shortness of breath.
An open pneumothorax is often called a 'sucking chest wound' because of the sound air makes entering the
chest.
The primary goal of first aid for a fracture is to prevent further movement of the bone fragments.
A sling can be used to support and immobilize injuries to the arm or shoulder.
Hypovolemic shock is caused by a significant loss of blood or fluids.
Cardiogenic shock occurs when the heart is damaged and cannot pump enough blood.
Neurogenic shock can result from a spinal cord injury, causing blood vessels to lose their tone.
The rule of nines is used to estimate the percentage of body surface area affected by burns.
Third-degree burns are full-thickness burns that may appear charred or white and are often painless due to
nerve damage.
A secondary survey involves a head-to-toe check for other injuries after life-threatening conditions are
managed.
If an object is impaled in the body, do not remove it; stabilize it in place with bulky dressings.
The only exception to removing an impaled object is if it is in the cheek and obstructing the airway.
A compound fracture involves a break in the skin over the fracture site.
An AED will not shock a 'flatline' (asystole); it requires a rhythm like ventricular fibrillation to work.
Continue CPR until the person shows signs of life, an AED is ready, or professional help takes over.
If you are alone and have no phone, perform 2 minutes of CPR before leaving a child/infant to call for help.
Always assume a head injury also involves a neck injury until proven otherwise.
In a multi-victim scenario, prioritize those who are not breathing or have life-threatening bleeding.
A 'Pulse Check' during CPR should take no longer than 10 seconds.
Chest compressions on an infant are performed using two fingers in the center of the chest.
For a small child, chest compressions may be performed using one or two hands depending on the child's
size.
A sprained ankle should be elevated above the level of the heart to reduce swelling.
Normal adult respiration rate is 12 to 16 breaths per minute.
Normal adult resting heart rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute.
First aid is intended to provide immediate, temporary care until professional medical assistance arrives.
