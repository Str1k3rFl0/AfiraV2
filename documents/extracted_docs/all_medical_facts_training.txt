The first step in any emergency is to ensure the scene is safe for both the rescuer and the victim.
The 'DRSABCD' protocol stands for Danger, Response, Send for help, Airway, Breathing, CPR, and
Defibrillation.
To check for a response, talk to the person and gently squeeze their shoulders.
If a person is unconscious but breathing normally, place them in the Recovery Position.
The Recovery Position keeps the airway open and prevents choking on vomit or secretions.
For an adult, CPR involves chest compressions at a rate of 100-120 per minute.
The correct depth for adult chest compressions is at least 2 inches (5 cm) but no more than 2.4 inches (6 cm).
The ratio of compressions to rescue breaths in CPR for adults, children, and infants (single rescuer) is 30:2.
Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions to allow blood to flow back into the heart.
An Automated External Defibrillator (AED) should be used as soon as it is available.
Follow the voice prompts of the AED exactly; it will only deliver a shock if a shockable rhythm is detected.
Do not touch the victim while the AED is analyzing the heart rhythm or delivering a shock.
For a choking adult who is conscious, perform the Heimlich Maneuver (abdominal thrusts).
To perform abdominal thrusts, stand behind the person and pull inward and upward just above the navel.
If a choking person becomes unconscious, lower them to the ground and start CPR immediately.
For a choking infant, use a combination of 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts.
Never perform a blind finger sweep in the mouth of a choking person; only remove visible objects.
To control severe external bleeding, apply direct pressure to the wound with a clean cloth or bandage.
If bleeding does not stop with direct pressure, apply a tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound (not on a joint).
Once a tourniquet is applied, note the exact time of application and do not remove it.
For a minor burn, run cool (not cold) water over the area for at least 10-20 minutes.
Do not apply ice, butter, or ointments to a severe burn, as this can damage the tissue further.
Cover a burn loosely with a sterile, non-stick dressing or plastic wrap.
In the case of a chemical burn, flush the area with water for at least 20 minutes while removing contaminated
clothing.
Signs of a stroke can be remembered by the acronym FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty,
Time to call emergency services.
If you suspect a heart attack, have the person sit down, rest, and keep calm while waiting for help.
For a person having a seizure, clear the area of hard or sharp objects and do not restrain them.
Do not put anything in the mouth of someone having a seizure.
After a seizure, place the person in the recovery position to keep their airway clear.
Signs of anaphylaxis include swelling of the face/throat, hives, and difficulty breathing.
The primary treatment for anaphylaxis is the immediate administration of an Epinephrine Auto-Injector
(EpiPen).
Inject the EpiPen into the middle of the outer thigh and hold it for the recommended time (usually 3-10
seconds).
For a suspected neck or spinal injury, do not move the person unless they are in immediate danger.
Manually stabilize the head and neck in the position found to prevent further injury.
Heat exhaustion signs include heavy sweating, rapid pulse, and dizziness.
Treat heat exhaustion by moving the person to a cool place, loosening clothing, and providing sips of water.
Heatstroke is a medical emergency characterized by a body temperature above 104°F (40°C) and altered
mental state.
Cool a heatstroke victim rapidly using ice baths or cold wet towels until help arrives.
Hypothermia occurs when the body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C).
To treat hypothermia, move the person out of the cold, remove wet clothing, and wrap them in warm blankets.
Frostbite usually affects extremities like fingers, toes, and the nose; do not rub the affected area.
Rewarm frostbitten areas slowly using warm water (not hot) only if there is no risk of refreezing.
For a nosebleed, lean the person forward and pinch the soft part of the nose for 10-15 minutes.
If a person faints, lay them flat on their back and elevate their legs about 12 inches (30 cm).
Signs of shock include pale, cold, clammy skin, rapid breathing, and a weak pulse.
To treat shock, lay the person down, elevate their legs, and cover them with a blanket to maintain warmth.
For a suspected fracture, immobilize the limb using a splint in the position it was found.
Do not try to realign or push a bone back into place if it is protruding (open fracture).
A sprain is an injury to a ligament, while a strain is an injury to a muscle or tendon.
Treat minor sprains and strains with RICE: Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation.
For a snake bite, keep the person calm and the affected limb immobilized below the level of the heart.
Do not use a tourniquet, cut the wound, or attempt to suck out the venom from a snake bite.
If a tooth is knocked out, handle it by the crown (top), not the root, and keep it moist in milk or saliva.
For a person with low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) who is conscious, provide a sugary drink or food.
In the case of poisoning, identify the substance and call poison control immediately; do not induce vomiting
unless told to do so.
Electric shock victims should not be touched until the power source is turned off.
Assess for an exit wound in cases of high-voltage electrical injury.
Capillary refill time (pressing on a fingernail) should be less than 2 seconds to indicate good circulation.
Use a sterile dressing for wounds to prevent infection; if a bandage becomes soaked with blood, add more on
top.
Hyperventilation can often be managed by having the person breathe slowly or use a paper bag (if coached).
Asthma attacks require the use of a rescue inhaler (bronchodilator) immediately.
If someone is stung by a bee, scrape the stinger away with a flat surface (like a credit card) rather than using
tweezers.
For a diabetic emergency where you are unsure if sugar is high or low, always give sugar if the person is
conscious.
Signs of internal bleeding include bruising, pain, and symptoms of shock without visible blood loss.
A concussion is a mild traumatic brain injury; look for symptoms like confusion, headache, or vomiting.
Anyone who loses consciousness after a head injury should be evaluated by a professional immediately.
A tourniquet should be tightened until the bleeding stops completely and the distal pulse disappears.
Chest thrusts for a choking pregnant woman or very obese person are performed higher up on the
breastbone.
In drowning cases, start rescue breaths as soon as possible, even before reaching the shore if safe.
Frostnip is the precursor to frostbite and can be treated by simple rewarming with skin-to-skin contact.
Chemicals in the eyes should be flushed for at least 20 minutes from the inner corner outward.
A sucking chest wound should be covered with an occlusive dressing taped on three sides only.
Standard precautions (gloves, masks) should be used whenever possible to prevent the spread of
bloodborne pathogens.
Check for medical ID jewelry (bracelets/necklaces) on unconscious patients for vital health information.
Signs of a broken hip include one leg appearing shorter and rotated outward.
Pneumothorax symptoms include sudden sharp chest pain and one-sided shortness of breath.
An open pneumothorax is often called a 'sucking chest wound' because of the sound air makes entering the
chest.
The primary goal of first aid for a fracture is to prevent further movement of the bone fragments.
A sling can be used to support and immobilize injuries to the arm or shoulder.
Hypovolemic shock is caused by a significant loss of blood or fluids.
Cardiogenic shock occurs when the heart is damaged and cannot pump enough blood.
Neurogenic shock can result from a spinal cord injury, causing blood vessels to lose their tone.
The rule of nines is used to estimate the percentage of body surface area affected by burns.
Third-degree burns are full-thickness burns that may appear charred or white and are often painless due to
nerve damage.
A secondary survey involves a head-to-toe check for other injuries after life-threatening conditions are
managed.
If an object is impaled in the body, do not remove it; stabilize it in place with bulky dressings.
The only exception to removing an impaled object is if it is in the cheek and obstructing the airway.
A compound fracture involves a break in the skin over the fracture site.
An AED will not shock a 'flatline' (asystole); it requires a rhythm like ventricular fibrillation to work.
Continue CPR until the person shows signs of life, an AED is ready, or professional help takes over.
If you are alone and have no phone, perform 2 minutes of CPR before leaving a child/infant to call for help.
Always assume a head injury also involves a neck injury until proven otherwise.
In a multi-victim scenario, prioritize those who are not breathing or have life-threatening bleeding.
A 'Pulse Check' during CPR should take no longer than 10 seconds.
Chest compressions on an infant are performed using two fingers in the center of the chest.
For a small child, chest compressions may be performed using one or two hands depending on the child's
size.
A sprained ankle should be elevated above the level of the heart to reduce swelling.
Normal adult respiration rate is 12 to 16 breaths per minute.
Normal adult resting heart rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute.
First aid is intended to provide immediate, temporary care until professional medical assistance arrives.
A Complete Blood Count (CBC) measures red blood cells, white blood cells, and
platelets.
Normal adult hemoglobin range is typically 13.5-17.5 g/dL for men and 12.0-15.5
g/dL for women.
Low hemoglobin levels indicate Anemia.
High hemoglobin levels can indicate Polycythemia or dehydration.
The normal White Blood Cell (WBC) count is between 4,500 and 11,000 cells per
microliter.
Leukocytosis (high WBC count) often indicates an ongoing infection or
inflammation.
Leukopenia (low WBC count) can indicate bone marrow failure or autoimmune
conditions.
Neutrophils are the most common type of WBC and increase during bacterial
infections.
Lymphocytes increase during viral infections.
Eosinophils often increase during allergic reactions or parasitic infections.
Platelets (thrombocytes) help in blood clotting; normal range is 150,000 to
450,000 per microliter.
Thrombocytopenia (low platelets) increases the risk of spontaneous bleeding and
bruising.
Thrombocytosis (high platelets) can increase the risk of unwanted blood clots.
Hematocrit measures the proportion of red blood cells in the blood.
Blood glucose levels are normally between 70 and 99 mg/dL when fasting.
Hyperglycemia (fasting glucose >126 mg/dL) is a primary indicator of Diabetes
Mellitus.
HbA1c measures average blood sugar over the past 2-3 months; a level of 6.5% or
higher indicates diabetes.
Creatinine is a waste product filtered by kidneys; high levels suggest impaired
kidney function.
BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) levels also increase when kidney function is reduced.
The Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) is the best overall measure of kidney
function.
ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) is an enzyme found mostly in the liver; high
levels indicate liver damage.
AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase) is found in the liver and heart; high levels can
suggest liver or muscle injury.
Bilirubin is a yellow pigment; high levels cause jaundice and suggest liver or
gallbladder issues.
Albumin is a protein made by the liver; low levels can indicate liver disease or
malnutrition.
Sodium (Na+) normal range is 135-145 mEq/L; it is crucial for fluid balance and
nerve function.
Hyponatremia (low sodium) can cause confusion, seizures, and brain swelling.
Hypernatremia (high sodium) is usually caused by dehydration.
Potassium (K+) normal range is 3.5-5.0 mEq/L; it is vital for heart rhythm.
Hypokalemia (low potassium) can cause muscle weakness and dangerous heart
arrhythmias.
Hyperkalemia (high potassium) is a medical emergency that can lead to sudden
cardiac arrest.
Calcium (Ca2+) is essential for bones, nerves, and muscles; normal range is
8.5-10.5 mg/dL.
CRP (C-Reactive Protein) is a marker of systemic inflammation in the body.
High CRP levels can indicate infection, autoimmune flare-ups, or heart disease
risk.
Troponin is a protein released into the blood during a heart attack; high levels
are diagnostic for myocardial infarction.
BNP (B-type Natriuretic Peptide) is a marker used to diagnose and monitor heart
failure.
Total cholesterol should generally be below 200 mg/dL.
LDL cholesterol is often called 'bad' cholesterol; high levels increase heart
disease risk.
HDL cholesterol is 'good' cholesterol; higher levels help protect the heart.
Triglycerides are a type of fat in the blood; high levels increase the risk of
pancreatitis.
TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) is the primary screen for thyroid function.
High TSH typically indicates Hypothyroidism (the brain is trying to stimulate a
slow thyroid).
Low TSH typically indicates Hyperthyroidism (the brain stops stimulating an
overactive thyroid).
INR (International Normalized Ratio) measures how long it takes blood to clot,
especially for patients on Warfarin.
A normal INR for someone not on anticoagulants is around 1.0.
D-dimer is a test used to rule out blood clots (like deep vein thrombosis or
pulmonary embolism).
Amylase and Lipase are enzymes measured to diagnose Pancreatitis.
Urinalysis checks for the presence of protein, glucose, and ketones in the urine.
Protein in the urine (proteinuria) can be an early sign of kidney disease.
Ketones in the urine can indicate that the body is burning fat for fuel, often
seen in diabetic ketoacidosis.
Specific gravity of urine measures how concentrated the urine is; high values
indicate dehydration.
Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) tests measure oxygen, carbon dioxide, and pH levels in
the blood.
Normal blood pH is tightly regulated between 7.35 and 7.45.
Acidosis occurs when blood pH falls below 7.35; Alkalosis occurs when it rises
above 7.45.
PCO2 (partial pressure of carbon dioxide) indicates how well the lungs are
removing CO2.
Lactate levels increase when tissues do not receive enough oxygen, often seen in
sepsis.
Serum iron and Ferritin tests are used to evaluate iron stores in the body.
B12 and Folate levels are checked to investigate specific types of anemia.
Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) is used as a screening tool for prostate issues.
Magnesium is essential for many biochemical reactions; low levels can cause
tremors and arrhythmias.
Chloride (Cl-) levels usually follow sodium levels and help maintain fluid
balance.
Bicarbonate (HCO3-) acts as a buffer to maintain normal blood pH levels.
Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) is an enzyme related to the liver and bones.
High ALP can indicate bone diseases or bile duct obstruction.
Uric acid high levels are associated with Gout and kidney stones.
ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate) is another non-specific marker for
inflammation.
Procalcitonin is a marker specifically used to distinguish bacterial from non-
bacterial infections.
PTT (Partial Thromboplastin Time) is used to monitor Heparin therapy.
A high anion gap in an electrolyte panel can indicate metabolic acidosis.
T4 (Thyroxine) is the main hormone secreted by the thyroid gland.
Microalbuminuria is a very small amount of albumin in the urine, indicating early
diabetic kidney damage.
A shift to the left in a CBC means there is an increase in immature neutrophils,
indicating acute infection.
Hemolysis is the destruction of red blood cells, which can artificially raise
potassium levels in a lab sample.
The occult blood test checks for hidden blood in the stool, a screen for colon
cancer.
Cultures (blood, urine, sputum) are used to identify the specific bacteria
causing an infection.
Sensitivity testing tells doctors which antibiotic will effectively kill the
identified bacteria.
Peak and Trough levels are measured for certain antibiotics (like Vancomycin) to
ensure safety.
Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH) is a general marker of tissue damage.
Ammonia levels are checked in patients with advanced liver disease to assess for
brain involvement (encephalopathy).
Creatine Kinase (CK) is an enzyme that rises when there is damage to muscles
(including the heart).
Reference ranges for lab tests can vary slightly depending on the laboratory and
the equipment used.
Differential Medical Facts for AI Training
Tachycardia is a fast heart rate (>100 bpm), whereas Bradycardia is a
slow heart rate (<60 bpm).
Hypertension refers to high blood pressure, while Hypotension refers to
low blood pressure.
Hyperglycemia is high blood sugar, whereas Hypoglycemia is dangerously
low blood sugar.
The Afferent neurons carry signals toward the brain, while Efferent
neurons carry signals away from the brain.
The Arteries carry oxygenated blood away from the heart, except for the
pulmonary artery.
The Veins carry deoxygenated blood toward the heart, except for the
pulmonary vein.
Ligaments connect bone to bone, while Tendons connect muscle to bone.
Dysphagia is difficulty swallowing, whereas Dysphasia is a language
disorder (speech).
Apnea is the temporary cessation of breathing, while Dyspnea is
difficult or labored breathing.
Hematuria is blood in the urine, while Hemoptysis is coughing up blood
from the lungs.
Anemia is a lack of red blood cells, while Leukemia is a cancer of the
white blood cells.
The Axial skeleton is the central axis (skull, spine), while the
Appendicular skeleton is the limbs and girdles.
Abduction is moving a limb away from the midline, while Adduction is
moving it toward the midline.
The Sympathetic nervous system triggers 'fight or flight', while the
Parasympathetic triggers 'rest and digest'.
Systolic pressure is the pressure during heart contraction, while
Diastolic is the pressure during heart relaxation.
Endocrine glands secrete hormones into the blood, while Exocrine glands
secrete through ducts (like sweat).
A Thrombus is a blood clot that stays in place, while an Embolus is a
clot that travels through the bloodstream.
Prone position is lying face down, while Supine position is lying face
up.
Mastication is the act of chewing, while Deglutition is the act of
swallowing.
Osteoblasts are cells that build bone, while Osteoclasts are cells that
break down bone tissue.
Acute conditions start suddenly and are short-term, while Chronic
conditions develop slowly and persist long-term.
Antigens are foreign substances that trigger immune responses, while
Antibodies are proteins produced to fight them.
Hypoxia is low oxygen in tissues, while Hypoxemia is low oxygen
specifically in the blood.
Infection is the invasion of pathogens, while Inflammation is the
body's response to injury or infection.
Benign tumors do not spread, while Malignant tumors are cancerous and
can invade other tissues.
Proximal means closer to the trunk of the body, while Distal means
further away from the trunk.
Superior means toward the head, while Inferior means toward the feet.
The Epidermis is the top layer of skin, while the Dermis is the thick
layer underneath it.
Type 1 Diabetes is an insulin deficiency, while Type 2 Diabetes is
insulin resistance.
Ureters carry urine from kidneys to the bladder, while the Urethra
carries urine out of the body.
The Cerebrum controls high-level functions, while the Cerebellum
controls balance and coordination.
White matter consists of myelinated axons, while Gray matter consists
mainly of neuronal cell bodies.
Ischemia is a restriction in blood supply, while Infarction is tissue
death due to lack of blood supply.
A Sign is an objective finding seen by a doctor, while a Symptom is a
subjective feeling reported by a patient.
Vasoconstriction is the narrowing of blood vessels, while Vasodilation
is the widening of blood vessels.
Atrophy is the wasting away of tissue, while Hypertrophy is the
increase in volume of an organ or tissue.
Stenosis is the abnormal narrowing of a body channel, while Dilation is
the widening.
The Right Lung has 3 lobes, while the Left Lung has 2 lobes to
accommodate the heart.
Visceral pleura covers the lungs, while Parietal pleura lines the chest
cavity.
Aerobic exercise requires oxygen, while Anaerobic exercise occurs
without sufficient oxygen.
Auscultation is listening to body sounds, while Palpation is feeling
the body with hands.
The Radius is on the thumb side of the forearm, while the Ulna is on
the pinky side.
The Tibia is the shin bone (inner), while the Fibula is the smaller
outer leg bone.
Etiology is the cause of a disease, while Pathogenesis is the manner of
its development.
Prognosis is the predicted outcome of a disease, while Diagnosis is the
identification of the disease.
Somatic nervous system is voluntary, while Autonomic nervous system is
involuntary.
Bile is produced by the Liver and stored in the Gallbladder.
The Small Intestine absorbs nutrients, while the Large Intestine
absorbs water and forms stool.
Myopia is nearsightedness, while Hyperopia is farsightedness.
Enzyme is a biological catalyst, while a Hormone is a chemical
messenger.
