A Complete Blood Count (CBC) measures red blood cells, white blood cells, and
platelets.
Normal adult hemoglobin range is typically 13.5-17.5 g/dL for men and 12.0-15.5
g/dL for women.
Low hemoglobin levels indicate Anemia.
High hemoglobin levels can indicate Polycythemia or dehydration.
The normal White Blood Cell (WBC) count is between 4,500 and 11,000 cells per
microliter.
Leukocytosis (high WBC count) often indicates an ongoing infection or
inflammation.
Leukopenia (low WBC count) can indicate bone marrow failure or autoimmune
conditions.
Neutrophils are the most common type of WBC and increase during bacterial
infections.
Lymphocytes increase during viral infections.
Eosinophils often increase during allergic reactions or parasitic infections.
Platelets (thrombocytes) help in blood clotting; normal range is 150,000 to
450,000 per microliter.
Thrombocytopenia (low platelets) increases the risk of spontaneous bleeding and
bruising.
Thrombocytosis (high platelets) can increase the risk of unwanted blood clots.
Hematocrit measures the proportion of red blood cells in the blood.
Blood glucose levels are normally between 70 and 99 mg/dL when fasting.
Hyperglycemia (fasting glucose >126 mg/dL) is a primary indicator of Diabetes
Mellitus.
HbA1c measures average blood sugar over the past 2-3 months; a level of 6.5% or
higher indicates diabetes.
Creatinine is a waste product filtered by kidneys; high levels suggest impaired
kidney function.
BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) levels also increase when kidney function is reduced.
The Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) is the best overall measure of kidney
function.
ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) is an enzyme found mostly in the liver; high
levels indicate liver damage.
AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase) is found in the liver and heart; high levels can
suggest liver or muscle injury.
Bilirubin is a yellow pigment; high levels cause jaundice and suggest liver or
gallbladder issues.
Albumin is a protein made by the liver; low levels can indicate liver disease or
malnutrition.
Sodium (Na+) normal range is 135-145 mEq/L; it is crucial for fluid balance and
nerve function.
Hyponatremia (low sodium) can cause confusion, seizures, and brain swelling.
Hypernatremia (high sodium) is usually caused by dehydration.
Potassium (K+) normal range is 3.5-5.0 mEq/L; it is vital for heart rhythm.
Hypokalemia (low potassium) can cause muscle weakness and dangerous heart
arrhythmias.
Hyperkalemia (high potassium) is a medical emergency that can lead to sudden
cardiac arrest.
Calcium (Ca2+) is essential for bones, nerves, and muscles; normal range is
8.5-10.5 mg/dL.
CRP (C-Reactive Protein) is a marker of systemic inflammation in the body.
High CRP levels can indicate infection, autoimmune flare-ups, or heart disease
risk.
Troponin is a protein released into the blood during a heart attack; high levels
are diagnostic for myocardial infarction.
BNP (B-type Natriuretic Peptide) is a marker used to diagnose and monitor heart
failure.
Total cholesterol should generally be below 200 mg/dL.
LDL cholesterol is often called 'bad' cholesterol; high levels increase heart
disease risk.
HDL cholesterol is 'good' cholesterol; higher levels help protect the heart.
Triglycerides are a type of fat in the blood; high levels increase the risk of
pancreatitis.
TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) is the primary screen for thyroid function.
High TSH typically indicates Hypothyroidism (the brain is trying to stimulate a
slow thyroid).
Low TSH typically indicates Hyperthyroidism (the brain stops stimulating an
overactive thyroid).
INR (International Normalized Ratio) measures how long it takes blood to clot,
especially for patients on Warfarin.
A normal INR for someone not on anticoagulants is around 1.0.
D-dimer is a test used to rule out blood clots (like deep vein thrombosis or
pulmonary embolism).
Amylase and Lipase are enzymes measured to diagnose Pancreatitis.
Urinalysis checks for the presence of protein, glucose, and ketones in the urine.
Protein in the urine (proteinuria) can be an early sign of kidney disease.
Ketones in the urine can indicate that the body is burning fat for fuel, often
seen in diabetic ketoacidosis.
Specific gravity of urine measures how concentrated the urine is; high values
indicate dehydration.
Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) tests measure oxygen, carbon dioxide, and pH levels in
the blood.
Normal blood pH is tightly regulated between 7.35 and 7.45.
Acidosis occurs when blood pH falls below 7.35; Alkalosis occurs when it rises
above 7.45.
PCO2 (partial pressure of carbon dioxide) indicates how well the lungs are
removing CO2.
Lactate levels increase when tissues do not receive enough oxygen, often seen in
sepsis.
Serum iron and Ferritin tests are used to evaluate iron stores in the body.
B12 and Folate levels are checked to investigate specific types of anemia.
Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) is used as a screening tool for prostate issues.
Magnesium is essential for many biochemical reactions; low levels can cause
tremors and arrhythmias.
Chloride (Cl-) levels usually follow sodium levels and help maintain fluid
balance.
Bicarbonate (HCO3-) acts as a buffer to maintain normal blood pH levels.
Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) is an enzyme related to the liver and bones.
High ALP can indicate bone diseases or bile duct obstruction.
Uric acid high levels are associated with Gout and kidney stones.
ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate) is another non-specific marker for
inflammation.
Procalcitonin is a marker specifically used to distinguish bacterial from non-
bacterial infections.
PTT (Partial Thromboplastin Time) is used to monitor Heparin therapy.
A high anion gap in an electrolyte panel can indicate metabolic acidosis.
T4 (Thyroxine) is the main hormone secreted by the thyroid gland.
Microalbuminuria is a very small amount of albumin in the urine, indicating early
diabetic kidney damage.
A shift to the left in a CBC means there is an increase in immature neutrophils,
indicating acute infection.
Hemolysis is the destruction of red blood cells, which can artificially raise
potassium levels in a lab sample.
The occult blood test checks for hidden blood in the stool, a screen for colon
cancer.
Cultures (blood, urine, sputum) are used to identify the specific bacteria
causing an infection.
Sensitivity testing tells doctors which antibiotic will effectively kill the
identified bacteria.
Peak and Trough levels are measured for certain antibiotics (like Vancomycin) to
ensure safety.
Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH) is a general marker of tissue damage.
Ammonia levels are checked in patients with advanced liver disease to assess for
brain involvement (encephalopathy).
Creatine Kinase (CK) is an enzyme that rises when there is damage to muscles
(including the heart).
Reference ranges for lab tests can vary slightly depending on the laboratory and
the equipment used.
