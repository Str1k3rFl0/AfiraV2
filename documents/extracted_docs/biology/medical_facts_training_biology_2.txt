There are 20 standard amino acids used by cells to synthesize proteins.
Essential amino acids cannot be synthesized by the body and must be obtained from the diet.
Peptide bonds link amino acids together through a dehydration synthesis reaction.
The primary structure of a protein is its unique sequence of amino acids.
Secondary structure refers to local folding patterns like alpha helices and beta-pleated sheets.
Tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional shape of a single polypeptide chain.
Quaternary structure results from the aggregation of two or more polypeptide subunits (e.g., hemoglobin).
Denaturation is the loss of a protein's native structure due to heat, pH changes, or chemicals.
Chaperonin proteins assist in the proper folding of other proteins.
Enzymes lower the activation energy required for a chemical reaction to proceed.
Competitive inhibitors bind to the active site of an enzyme, blocking the substrate.
Non-competitive inhibitors bind to an allosteric site, changing the enzyme's shape.
Coenzymes are organic molecules, often derived from vitamins, that assist enzyme function.
Cofactors are inorganic ions like magnesium or iron that are required for enzyme activity.
The induced fit model describes how an enzyme changes shape slightly to bind its substrate more firmly.
Saturated fatty acids have no double bonds between carbon atoms and are typically solid at room temperature.
Unsaturated fatty acids have one or more double bonds, creating kinks in the chain.
Phospholipids are amphipathic, meaning they have a hydrophilic head and hydrophobic tails.
Steroids are lipids characterized by a carbon skeleton with four fused rings (e.g., cholesterol).
Glycogen is the primary storage polysaccharide in animals, found in liver and muscle cells.
Starch is the primary storage polysaccharide in plants.
Chitin is a structural polysaccharide found in fungal cell walls and arthropod exoskeletons.
Nucleosides consist of a nitrogenous base and a five-carbon sugar, lacking a phosphate group.
DNA replication is semi-conservative, meaning each new helix contains one old and one new strand.
Helicase is the enzyme that unwinds the DNA double helix at the replication fork.
DNA polymerase adds nucleotides to the growing DNA strand in the 5' to 3' direction.
The leading strand is synthesized continuously toward the replication fork.
The lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously in short segments called Okazaki fragments.
DNA ligase joins Okazaki fragments together on the lagging strand.
Primase synthesizes a short RNA primer to start DNA replication.
Topoisomerase relieves the over-winding strain ahead of the replication fork.
Telomerase adds repetitive sequences to the ends of chromosomes to prevent shortening.
RNA polymerase binds to a promoter sequence to initiate transcription.
Introns are non-coding regions of a gene that are spliced out of the primary mRNA transcript.
Exons are the coding regions of a gene that are joined together to form mature mRNA.
The 5' cap and poly-A tail protect mRNA from degradation and aid in ribosome binding.
Alternative splicing allows one gene to code for multiple different proteins.
The A, P, and E sites on a ribosome facilitate the binding of tRNA during translation.
Polysomes are groups of ribosomes translating the same mRNA molecule simultaneously.
Operons are clusters of genes with related functions controlled by a single promoter in prokaryotes.
The lac operon is an inducible system regulated by the presence of lactose.
The trp operon is a repressible system regulated by the levels of tryptophan.
Transcription factors are proteins that help switch genes 'on' or 'off'.
Epigenetics involves changes in gene expression that do not alter the DNA sequence itself.
DNA methylation generally suppresses gene transcription.
Histone acetylation usually promotes a more open chromatin structure and active transcription.
MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small RNA molecules that can silence gene expression by binding to mRNA.
The proteome is the entire set of proteins expressed by a cell or organism at a given time.
Signal transduction pathways convert extracellular signals into specific cellular responses.
G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) are the largest family of cell-surface receptors.
Second messengers like cAMP and calcium ions relay signals inside the cell.
Protein kinases add phosphate groups to proteins (phosphorylation) to activate or inhibit them.
Protein phosphatases remove phosphate groups from proteins (dephosphorylation).
Apoptosis can be triggered by internal signals (DNA damage) or external death ligands.
Cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) regulate the progression of the cell cycle.
The G1 checkpoint ensures the cell is ready for DNA synthesis.
The G2 checkpoint ensures DNA replication is complete and accurate before mitosis.
The M checkpoint ensures all chromosomes are properly attached to the spindle.
Cancer cells often ignore cell cycle checkpoints and lack density-dependent inhibition.
Mendel's 'factors' are now known as genes located on chromosomes.
A testcross involves crossing an individual with a dominant phenotype with a homozygous recessive individual.
The recombination frequency between two genes depends on their distance from each other on a chromosome.
Genes located very close together on the same chromosome are called linked genes.
A linkage map shows the relative positions of genes based on recombination frequencies.
Nondisjunction is the failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis.
Aneuploidy is the condition of having an abnormal number of a particular chromosome.
Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) is the most common autosomal aneuploidy in humans.
Klinefelter syndrome (XXY) results in a male with an extra X chromosome.
Turner syndrome (X0) results in a sterile female with only one X chromosome.
Polyploidy is common in plants and often results in larger, more robust organisms.
Cytoplasmic inheritance involves genes found in mitochondria or chloroplasts, usually inherited from the mother.
The endosymbiotic theory explains the origin of eukaryotes from prokaryotic ancestors.
Molecular clocks use the rate of mutations in DNA to estimate the time of evolutionary divergence.
Horizontal gene transfer is the movement of genetic material between different species (common in bacteria).
Transformation is the uptake of foreign DNA from the environment by a bacterial cell.
Transduction is the transfer of bacterial DNA from one cell to another via a virus.
Conjugation is the direct transfer of DNA between two bacterial cells through a pilus.
Reverse transcriptase is an enzyme used by retroviruses to turn RNA into DNA.
Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific recognition sequences (palindromes).
Sticky ends are single-stranded overhangs produced by some restriction enzymes.
Plasmids used in biotechnology often contain an antibiotic resistance gene as a selectable marker.
The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) involves cycles of denaturation, annealing, and extension.
Taq polymerase is a heat-stable DNA polymerase used in PCR.
DNA sequencing determines the exact order of nucleotides in a DNA molecule.
Genomics focuses on the structure, function, and evolution of whole genomes.
Proteomics analyzes the patterns of protein expression and interaction.
Bioinformatics applies computational tools to biological data analysis.
Metagenomics is the study of genetic material recovered directly from environmental samples.
The microbiome is the collection of all microorganisms living in a particular environment (like the human gut).
Photosynthesis consists of the light reactions and the Calvin cycle.
Chlorophyll a is the main photosynthetic pigment, absorbing blue-violet and red light.
Accessory pigments like chlorophyll b and carotenoids expand the spectrum of light used.
Photosystem II splits water molecules to release electrons, protons, and oxygen.
The proton gradient generated during the light reactions drives ATP synthesis (chemiosmosis).
NADPH is the electron carrier produced during the light reactions.
The Calvin cycle uses ATP and NADPH to convert CO2 into G3P (a sugar precursor).
Rubisco is the enzyme that catalyzes the first step of carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle.
C3 plants fix CO2 directly into a three-carbon compound.
C4 plants minimize photorespiration by fixing CO2 into a four-carbon compound in separate cells.
CAM plants open their stomata at night to take up CO2, storing it as an organic acid for daytime use.
Xylem consists of tracheids and vessel elements which are dead at maturity.
Phloem consists of sieve-tube elements and companion cells which are living at maturity.
The cohesion-tension hypothesis explains how water is pulled up through the xylem.
Translocation is the transport of organic nutrients through the phloem from source to sink.
The pressure-flow hypothesis explains the movement of sap in the phloem.
Nitrogen-fixing bacteria in root nodules convert atmospheric N2 into ammonia for plants.
Mycorrhizae are symbiotic associations between fungi and plant roots that enhance nutrient uptake.
Plant hormones (phytohormones) include auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, abscisic acid, and ethylene.
Auxins promote cell elongation and are responsible for phototropism.
Ethylene is a gaseous hormone that triggers fruit ripening and leaf abscission.
Abscisic acid (ABA) promotes seed dormancy and stomatal closure during drought.
Gibberellins stimulate stem elongation and seed germination.
Cytokinins promote cell division and delay leaf senescence.
Photoperiodism is a plant's response to the relative lengths of day and night.
Short-day plants flower when the night length exceeds a critical threshold.
Long-day plants flower when the night length is shorter than a critical threshold.
Phytochromes are light-sensitive pigments that regulate a plant's circadian rhythms.
Invertebrates are animals that lack a backbone, making up 95% of animal species.
Sponges (Porifera) are basal animals that lack true tissues.
Cnidarians (jellyfish, corals) exhibit radial symmetry and have stinging cells called cnidocytes.
Flatworms (Platyhelminthes) are acoelomate and have bilateral symmetry.
Nematodes (roundworms) are pseudocoelomate and have a complete digestive tract.
Mollusks have a muscular foot, a visceral mass, and a mantle.
Annelids are segmented worms, including earthworms and leeches.
Arthropods have segmented bodies, hard exoskeletons, and jointed appendages.
Echinoderms (starfish, sea urchins) have a water vascular system and radial symmetry as adults.
Chordates are defined by a notochord, a dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, and a post-anal tail.
Craniates are chordates that have a head.
Vertebrates are craniates that have a backbone.
Gnathostomes are vertebrates that have jaws.
Tetrapods are gnathostomes that have four limbs.
Amniotes are tetrapods that have a terrestrially adapted egg (reptiles, birds, mammals).
Ectotherms regulate body temperature through behavioral means.
Endotherms use metabolic heat to maintain a stable internal temperature.
Monotremes are egg-laying mammals (e.g., platypus).
Marsupials are mammals whose young complete development in a pouch.
Eutherians are placental mammals.
Tissues are organized into organs, which are organized into organ systems.
Interstitial fluid surrounds cells and facilitates exchange with the blood.
Homeostasis often relies on negative feedback to return a system to its set point.
Thermoregulation is the process by which animals maintain an internal temperature within a tolerable range.
Basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the metabolic rate of a resting, fasting, non-stressed endotherm.
Standard metabolic rate (SMR) is the metabolic rate of a resting, fasting, non-stressed ectotherm at a specific temperature.
Torpor is a state of low activity and decreased metabolism that allows animals to save energy.
Hibernation is long-term torpor that is an adaptation to winter cold and food scarcity.
Estivation is summer torpor, an adaptation to high temperatures and scarce water.
Essential nutrients include essential amino acids, essential fatty acids, vitamins, and minerals.
Vitamins are organic molecules required in small amounts (water-soluble vs.
fat-soluble).
Minerals are simple inorganic nutrients, such as calcium and phosphorus.
Intracellular digestion occurs inside food vacuoles after endocytosis.
Extracellular digestion occurs in compartments continuous with the outside of the body.
The alimentary canal is a complete digestive tract with a mouth and an anus.
Salivary amylase begins the chemical digestion of carbohydrates in the mouth.
The stomach converts food into a liquid mixture called chyme.
Pepsin is an enzyme in the stomach that begins protein digestion.
Bile, produced by the liver, emulsifies fats in the small intestine.
The small intestine is the major site of both chemical digestion and nutrient absorption.
The large intestine (colon) primarily functions in water reabsorption and waste elimination.
Ruminants have specialized stomachs with multiple chambers to digest cellulose using symbiotic bacteria.
Open circulatory systems allow hemolymph to bathe organs directly.
Closed circulatory systems confine blood to vessels, distinct from interstitial fluid.
Arteries carry blood away from the heart; veins carry blood toward the heart.
Capillaries are the sites of exchange between blood and tissues.
Double circulation in mammals involves pulmonary and systemic circuits.
The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the body.
The left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood into the systemic circuit via the aorta.
Cardiac output is the volume of blood pumped per minute by each ventricle.
Heart valves prevent the backflow of blood.
The lymphatic system returns leaked fluid and proteins to the circulatory system.
Erythrocytes (red blood cells) lack nuclei in mammals to maximize space for hemoglobin.
Leukocytes (white blood cells) function in defense and immunity.
Platelets are fragments of cells involved in blood clotting.
Gas exchange occurs by diffusion across moist respiratory surfaces.
Gills use countercurrent exchange to maximize oxygen uptake from water.
The tracheal system in insects delivers oxygen directly to body cells.
Lungs are localized respiratory organs in most terrestrial vertebrates.
Negative pressure breathing in mammals pulls air into the lungs.
Hemoglobin binds oxygen cooperatively; the binding of one O2 increases the affinity for others.
The Bohr shift is a decrease in hemoglobin's affinity for O2 in response to low pH (high CO2).
Carbon dioxide is mostly transported in the blood as bicarbonate ions.
Innate immunity provides broad, non-specific protection against pathogens.
Acquired (adaptive) immunity is highly specific and involves memory cells.
Phagocytic cells like macrophages and neutrophils ingest and destroy pathogens.
Natural killer (NK) cells identify and kill infected or cancerous body cells.
The inflammatory response involves the release of histamine and increased blood flow to an injury site.
B cells mature in the bone marrow and are central to the humoral immune response.
T cells mature in the thymus and are central to the cell-mediated immune response.
An antigen is any substance that elicits a response from a B or T cell.
MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex) molecules present antigen fragments on the cell surface.
Clonal selection produces effector cells (plasma cells) and memory cells.
Primary immune response occurs on the first exposure to an antigen.
Secondary immune response is faster and more robust due to memory cells.
Passive immunity is the transfer of antibodies (e.g., from mother to fetus).
Allergies are exaggerated (hypersensitive) responses to antigens called allergens.
Autoimmune diseases occur when the immune system loses self-tolerance.
Osmoregulation is the control of solute concentrations and water balance.
Excretion is the removal of nitrogenous metabolites and other waste products.
Ammonia is a highly toxic nitrogenous waste excreted mostly by aquatic animals.
Urea is a less toxic nitrogenous waste produced by mammals and adult amphibians.
Uric acid is an insoluble, non-toxic waste excreted by birds, insects, and reptiles.
The nephron is the functional unit of the vertebrate kidney.
Filtration occurs in the glomerulus and Bowman's capsule.
Reabsorption and secretion occur in the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, and distal tubule.
The loop of Henle is essential for creating a concentration gradient in the renal medulla.
Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) increases water reabsorption in the collecting ducts.
Hormones are chemical signals that travel through the bloodstream to target cells.
Water-soluble hormones bind to cell-surface receptors.
Lipid-soluble hormones (like steroids) pass through the cell membrane and bind to intracellular receptors.
The hypothalamus regulates the anterior pituitary through releasing and inhibiting hormones.
The posterior pituitary stores and releases oxytocin and ADH produced by the hypothalamus.
Thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) regulate metabolism and development.
Parathyroid hormone (PTH) increases blood calcium levels.
Calcitonin (from the thyroid) decreases blood calcium levels.
Insulin (from the pancreas) lowers blood glucose levels.
Glucagon (from the pancreas) raises blood glucose levels.
The adrenal medulla secretes epinephrine and norepinephrine in response to short-term stress.
The adrenal cortex secretes corticosteroids in response to long-term stress.
Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring (fission, budding, fragmentation).
Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of haploid gametes to form a diploid zygote.
Hermaphroditism is a condition where an individual has both male and female reproductive systems.
External fertilization occurs when eggs are shed by the female and fertilized by the male in the environment.
Internal fertilization occurs when sperm are deposited in or near the female reproductive tract.
Spermatogenesis is the continuous production of sperm in the testes.
Oogenesis is the development of a mature egg, a prolonged and interrupted process.
The ovarian cycle includes the follicular phase, ovulation, and the luteal phase.
The uterine (menstrual) cycle involves the thickening and shedding of the endometrium.
GnRH from the hypothalamus triggers the release of FSH and LH from the anterior pituitary.
FSH and LH stimulate the gonads to produce sex hormones and gametes.
The placenta facilitates the exchange of nutrients and wastes between mother and fetus.
Neurons are the structural and functional units of the nervous system.
Dendrites receive signals; axons transmit signals to other cells.
The resting potential of a neuron is typically around -70 mV.
Action potentials are all-or-none electrical signals that travel down the axon.
Depolarization is an increase in membrane potential (less negative).
Hyperpolarization is a decrease in membrane potential (more negative).
The myelin sheath, made by Schwann cells or oligodendrocytes, speeds up nerve impulses.
Saltatory conduction is the 'jumping' of an action potential between nodes of Ranvier.
Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers that cross the synaptic cleft.
Excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs) depolarize the postsynaptic membrane.
Inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (IPSPs) hyperpolarize the postsynaptic membrane.
The central nervous system (CNS) consists of the brain and spinal cord.
The peripheral nervous system (PNS) consists of cranial and spinal nerves.
The autonomic nervous system regulates the internal environment involuntarily.
The sympathetic division prepares the body for 'fight or flight'.
The parasympathetic division promotes 'rest and digest' functions.
The cerebral cortex is the outer layer of the cerebrum, responsible for higher processing.
The limbic system is involved in emotion, motivation, and memory.
The hippocampus is critical for the formation of new memories.
Neural plasticity is the ability of the nervous system to change in response to experience.
Sensory receptors convert stimuli into changes in membrane potential (transduction).
Mechanoreceptors respond to physical deformation (touch, pressure, sound).
Chemoreceptors respond to specific chemical concentrations (smell, taste).
Electromagnetic receptors detect light, electricity, or magnetism.
Thermoreceptors respond to heat or cold.
Nociceptors are pain receptors that detect harmful stimuli.
The human eye focuses by changing the shape of the lens (accommodation).
Rods are highly sensitive to light; cones provide color vision.
The gustatory system (taste) detects sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami.
Skeletal muscle consists of bundles of muscle fibers containing myofibrils.
Sarcomeres are the basic contractile units of skeletal muscle.
The sliding-filament model describes muscle contraction involving actin and myosin.
Calcium ions and the troponin-tropomyosin complex regulate muscle contraction.
Motor units consist of a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it controls.
Oxidative muscle fibers rely on aerobic respiration; glycolytic fibers rely on glycolysis.
Fixed action patterns are innate, unchangeable behaviors triggered by a sign stimulus.
Imprinting is a type of learning with a sensitive period, often involving a parent-offspring bond.
Spatial learning is the establishment of a memory that reflects the environment's spatial structure.
Associative learning is the ability to associate one environmental feature with another.
Social learning is learning through the observation of others.
Altruism can be explained by kin selection, which enhances the reproductive success of relatives.
Inclusive fitness is the total effect an individual has on proliferating its genes.
Ecology study levels: organismal, population, community, ecosystem, landscape, and global.
Climate is the long-term prevailing weather conditions in a given area.
Biomes are major life zones characterized by vegetation type or physical environment.
Density-dependent factors (like disease) limit population growth more as density increases.
Density-independent factors (like weather) limit population regardless of density.
Exponential growth (J-shaped curve) occurs under ideal conditions with unlimited resources.
Logistic growth (S-shaped curve) occurs as population growth levels off at the carrying capacity (K).
R-selection (opportunistic) favors high reproductive rates in unstable environments.
K-selection (equilibrial) favors competitive ability in stable environments near carrying capacity.
Interspecific interactions include competition, predation, herbivory, and symbiosis.
The competitive exclusion principle states that two species with the same niche cannot coexist.
Resource partitioning allows similar species to coexist by using different parts of the niche.
Cryptic coloration (camouflage) makes prey difficult to see.
Aposematic coloration (warning) warns predators that prey is toxic or dangerous.
Batesian mimicry is when a harmless species mimics a harmful one.
Mullerian mimicry is when two harmful species mimic each other.
Species richness is the number of different species in a community.
Relative abundance is the proportion each species represents of all individuals.
Keystone species have a disproportionately large effect on their community relative to their abundance.
Bottom-up control suggests nutrient supply limits higher trophic levels.
Top-down control (trophic cascade) suggests predators limit lower trophic levels.
Primary production is the amount of light energy converted to chemical energy by autotrophs.
Net primary production (NPP) is GPP minus energy used by primary producers for respiration.
Secondary production is the amount of chemical energy in consumers' food converted to their own new biomass.
Production efficiency is the percentage of energy stored in assimilated food not used for respiration.
Trophic efficiency is the percentage of production transferred from one trophic level to the next (usually 10%).
Biogeochemical cycles involve both biotic and abiotic components.
Decomposition is faster in warm, moist environments.
The greenhouse effect is necessary for life but intensified by human activity (global warming).
The ozone layer protects Earth from harmful UV radiation; it was damaged by CFCs.
Conservation biology aims to conserve biodiversity at the genetic, species, and ecosystem levels.
The extinction vortex describes the downward spiral of small populations toward extinction.
Minimum viable population (MVP) is the smallest size at which a population can sustain itself.
Biological magnification concentrates toxins at higher trophic levels.
Restoration ecology uses ecological principles to return degraded ecosystems to a natural state.
Bioremediation uses organisms (bacteria, plants) to detoxify polluted ecosystems.
Biological augmentation uses organisms to add essential materials to a degraded ecosystem.
Sustainable development meets human needs while preserving the environment for the future.
The tree of life is constantly revised as new molecular data becomes available.
