The first law of thermodynamics in biology: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.
The second law of thermodynamics: every energy transfer increases the entropy (disorder) of the universe.
Free energy (Gibbs free energy) is the portion of a system's energy that can perform work.
Exergonic reactions release free energy and occur spontaneously.
Endergonic reactions absorb free energy and require an input of energy to proceed.
Energy coupling is the use of an exergonic process to drive an endergonic one.
ATP powers cellular work by coupling exergonic hydrolysis to endergonic reactions.
Phosphorylation is the transfer of a phosphate group from ATP to another molecule.
Regeneration of ATP comes from the phosphorylation of ADP using energy from catabolism.
Activation energy is the initial investment of energy for starting a reaction.
Enzymes do not change the free energy change (delta G) of a reaction.
The Vmax of an enzyme is the maximum rate of reaction when the enzyme is saturated with substrate.
The Km (Michaelis constant) is the substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of Vmax.
A low Km indicates high affinity of the enzyme for its substrate.
Allosteric regulation involves a protein's function at one site being affected by the binding of a regulatory molecule at a separate site.
Feedback inhibition is a metabolic control in which the end product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an enzyme within that pathway.
Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons between reactants.
Oxidation is the loss of electrons; reduction is the gain of electrons (OIL RIG).
NAD+ is a coenzyme that functions as an electron carrier during cellular respiration.
The electron transport chain is located in the inner mitochondrial membrane (cristae).
Cytochromes are proteins in the electron transport chain that contain iron atoms (heme groups).
The proton-motive force is the gradient of H+ ions across a membrane that drives ATP synthesis.
Chemiosmosis is the use of energy in a H+ gradient to drive cellular work.
Substrate-level phosphorylation produces a small amount of ATP during glycolysis and the citric acid cycle.
Oxidative phosphorylation produces the bulk of ATP in aerobic organisms.
Beta-oxidation breaks down fatty acids into acetyl-CoA for entry into the citric acid cycle.
Deamination is the removal of an amino group from an amino acid before it is used for energy.
Gluconeogenesis is the synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors like pyruvate or lactic acid.
The glyoxylate cycle in plants and bacteria allows for the conversion of fats to carbohydrates.
Cyclic electron flow in photosynthesis uses only photosystem I and produces ATP but not NADPH.
Photorespiration occurs when rubisco binds O2 instead of CO2, wasting energy and carbon.
In C4 plants, the bundle-sheath cells are where the Calvin cycle takes place.
Mesophyll cells in C4 plants incorporate CO2 into organic acids.
Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM) is an adaptation to arid conditions.
A signal peptide is a sequence of amino acids that targets a protein to the endoplasmic reticulum.
Proteasomes are protein complexes that degrade unneeded or damaged proteins tagged with ubiquitin.
The endomembrane system includes the nuclear envelope, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, and vacuoles.
Autophagy is the process by which a cell recycles its own organic material.
Tight junctions prevent leakage of extracellular fluid across a layer of epithelial cells.
Desmosomes (anchoring junctions) fasten cells together into strong sheets.
Gap junctions (communicating junctions) provide cytoplasmic channels between adjacent cells.
Plasmodesmata are channels that perforate plant cell walls, connecting the cytoplasm of adjacent cells.
The fluid mosaic model describes the membrane as a mosaic of protein molecules in a fluid bilayer.
Cholesterol acts as a 'temperature buffer' for the plasma membrane, maintaining fluidity.
Integral proteins penetrate the hydrophobic interior of the lipid bilayer.
Peripheral proteins are loosely bound to the surface of the membrane.
Membrane carbohydrates function primarily in cell-cell recognition.
Electrogenic pumps, like the sodium-potassium pump, generate voltage across a membrane.
Cotransport occurs when active transport of a solute indirectly drives transport of other substances.
Phagocytosis is 'cell eating'; pinocytosis is 'cell drinking'.
Receptor-mediated endocytosis allows a cell to acquire bulk quantities of specific substances.
A genome is the total genetic information in a cell or organism.
Chromatin is the complex of DNA and proteins that makes up eukaryotic chromosomes.
Somatic cells are non-reproductive cells with two sets of chromosomes (diploid).
Gametes are reproductive cells (sperm and eggs) with one set of chromosomes (haploid).
Sister chromatids are joined copies of the original chromosome.
The centromere is the region where sister chromatids are most closely attached.
Kinetochores are protein structures on the centromere where spindle fibers attach.
Binary fission is the method of cell division in prokaryotes.
Growth factors are proteins released by certain cells that stimulate other cells to divide.
Density-dependent inhibition is a phenomenon where crowded cells stop dividing.
Anchorage dependence requires cells to be attached to a substratum to divide.
Transformation is the process that converts a normal cell to a cancer cell.
A benign tumor remains at its original site; a malignant tumor invades surrounding tissues.
Metastasis is the spread of cancer cells to locations distant from their original site.
Genes are the units of heredity and are made up of segments of DNA.
A locus is a gene's specific location along the length of a chromosome.
Asexual reproduction produces clones.
The life cycle is the generation-to-generation sequence of stages in the reproductive history of an organism.
Karyotype is an ordered display of the pairs of chromosomes from a cell.
Homologous chromosomes (homologs) carry genes controlling the same inherited characters.
Autosomes are the chromosomes that do not determine sex.
The X and Y chromosomes are the sex chromosomes.
Fertilization is the union of gametes, resulting in a diploid zygote.
Meiosis I is the reductional division; meiosis II is the equational division.
Synapsis is the pairing of homologous chromosomes during prophase I.
Crossing over involves the exchange of DNA between non-sister chromatids.
Chiasmata are the X-shaped regions where crossing over has occurred.
Cohesins hold sister chromatids together along their entire length.
Three mechanisms contribute to genetic variation: independent assortment, crossing over, and random fertilization.
Characters are heritable features (e.g., flower color); traits are variations of a character (e.g., purple).
True-breeding organisms produce offspring with the same trait over many generations.
The P generation is the parent generation; F1 is the first filial generation.
The Law of Segregation: the two alleles for a heritable character separate during gamete formation.
A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict the allele composition of offspring.
Phenotype can include physiological traits as well as physical appearance.
Pleiotropy is the property of a gene having multiple phenotypic effects (e.g., cystic fibrosis).
Epistasis is when one gene alters the phenotypic expression of a second gene.
Quantitative characters vary along a continuum in a population.
Norm of reaction is the phenotypic range for a genotype depending on the environment.
Pedigree analysis is used to track the inheritance of traits through human generations.
Carriers are heterozygous individuals who carry a recessive allele but are phenotypically normal.
The chromosomal theory of inheritance states that genes have specific loci on chromosomes.
Wild type is the phenotype most commonly observed in natural populations.
Sex-linked genes are located on either sex chromosome.
X-linked genes follow specific patterns of inheritance; fathers pass them to daughters only.
Duchenne muscular dystrophy and hemophilia are well-known X-linked recessive disorders.
Barr bodies are inactive X chromosomes in female mammalian cells.
Genetic recombination is the production of offspring with combinations of traits differing from either parent.
Parental types are offspring that match the parental phenotypes.
Recombinant types (recombinants) are offspring with new combinations of phenotypes.
A linkage map is based on the assumption that the further apart genes are, the higher the recombination frequency.
Cytogenetic maps locate genes with respect to chromosomal features like stained bands.
Deletion removes a chromosomal segment.
Duplication repeats a segment.
Inversion reverses the orientation of a segment within a chromosome.
Translocation moves a segment from one chromosome to a non-homologous one.
Genomic imprinting involves the silencing of certain genes depending on which parent passes them on.
Extranuclear genes are found in organelles like mitochondria and chloroplasts.
Mitochondrial myopathy is a disease caused by defects in mitochondrial DNA.
Griffith's experiment showed that heat-killed pathogenic bacteria could transform live non-pathogenic ones.
Avery, McCarty, and MacLeod identified DNA as the transforming substance.
Hershey and Chase used radioactive phosphorus and sulfur to prove that DNA is the genetic material in viruses.
Chargaff's rules: A=T and G=C, and the composition of DNA varies between species.
Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography images were crucial in determining DNA's structure.
Watson and Crick built the first accurate model of the DNA double helix.
Antiparallel means the two sugar-phosphate backbones of DNA run in opposite directions.
Origins of replication are specific sites where the DNA strands start to separate.
Replication bubbles expand in both directions from the origin.
DNA polymerases require a primer and a DNA template strand.
Mismatch repair uses enzymes to fix errors in DNA base pairing.
Nucleotide excision repair replaces damaged DNA segments (e.g., thymine dimers from UV).
Histones contain a high proportion of positively charged amino acids to bind to negatively charged DNA.
Nucleosomes are 'beads on a string', the basic unit of DNA packing.
Heterochromatin is highly condensed interphase DNA that is generally not transcribed.
Euchromatin is less compacted DNA that is available for transcription.
Gene expression is the process by which DNA directs protein synthesis.
In prokaryotes, translation can begin while transcription is still in progress.
In eukaryotes, the nuclear envelope separates transcription from translation.
The promoter includes the start point and the TATA box (in eukaryotes).
The terminator is the sequence that signals the end of transcription in bacteria.
RNA splicing is carried out by spliceosomes (complexes of proteins and small RNAs).
Ribozymes are RNA molecules that function as enzymes.
tRNA molecules have an anticodon that base-pairs with a specific mRNA codon.
Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase is the enzyme that matches a tRNA with its correct amino acid.
Wobble is the flexible pairing at the third base of a codon.
Signal-recognition particles (SRP) escort ribosomes to the ER membrane.
Silent mutations have no effect on the amino acid sequence.
Missense mutations change one amino acid to another.
Nonsense mutations change an amino acid codon into a stop codon.
Mutagens are physical or chemical agents that can cause mutations.
Gene regulation allows cells to differentiate and respond to their environment.
Corepressors cooperate with repressor proteins to switch operons off.
Inducers inactivate repressors to switch operons on.
CAP (catabolite activator protein) is a positive regulator of the lac operon.
Control elements are segments of non-coding DNA that serve as binding sites for transcription factors.
Enhancers are distal control elements that can be thousands of nucleotides away from a gene.
Ubiquitin-proteasome pathway is a major mechanism for regulated protein degradation.
RNA interference (RNAi) uses small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) to block gene expression.
Piwi-interacting RNAs (piRNAs) induce formation of heterochromatin, blocking expression of transposons.
LncRNAs (long non-coding RNAs) play various roles in gene regulation and chromatin remodeling.
Cytoplasmic determinants are maternal substances in the egg that influence early development.
Induction is the process by which signals from neighboring cells cause changes in target cells.
Determination commits a cell to its final fate.
Differentiation is the marked change in structure and function as a cell becomes specialized.
Pattern formation is the development of a spatial organization of tissues and organs.
Positional information consists of molecular cues that control pattern formation.
Maternal effect genes (egg-polarity genes) control the orientation of the egg.
Bicoid gene is essential for setting up the anterior end of the Drosophila embryo.
Proto-oncogenes are normal cellular genes that code for proteins that stimulate normal cell growth.
Oncogenes can arise from amplification, point mutations, or translocation of proto-oncogenes.
Ras protein is a G protein that, when mutated, can lead to uncontrolled cell division.
p53 is often called the 'guardian angel of the genome'.
The multi-step model of cancer suggests that multiple mutations are needed for full malignancy.
Viruses are not cells; they are 'a kind of borrowed life'.
Capsid is the protein shell enclosing the viral genome.
Viral envelopes are derived from the host cell's membrane and contain viral proteins.
Host range is the limited number of species a virus can infect.
Retroviruses use reverse transcriptase to copy their RNA genome into DNA.
HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) is the retrovirus that causes AIDS.
A provirus is a viral genome that is permanently integrated into the host genome.
Prions are slow-acting, virtually indestructible infectious proteins (e.g., Mad Cow disease).
Genetic engineering is the direct manipulation of genes for practical purposes.
DNA cloning produces multiple copies of a specific DNA segment.
Restriction sites are the specific DNA sequences recognized by restriction enzymes.
Gel electrophoresis separates DNA molecules based on their length and charge.
Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) uses a three-step cycle: heating, cooling, and replication.
DNA sequencing uses fluorescently labeled nucleotides to 'read' a DNA sequence.
Stem cells can reproduce themselves indefinitely and differentiate into specialized cells.
Totipotent cells can give rise to all specialized cell types in an organism.
Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPS) are adult cells 'reprogrammed' to act like ES cells.
Gene therapy is the introduction of genes into an afflicted individual for therapeutic purposes.
Transgenic animals are made by introducing genes from one species into the genome of another.
Short tandem repeats (STRs) are used in DNA profiling for forensic identification.
Genetically modified (GM) organisms are used in agriculture to improve crop yield and resistance.
Bioethics considers the moral and social implications of biological research and technology.
Systems biology studies the interactions among the parts of a biological system.
Metabolic pathways are organized into interconnected networks.
Homeostasis requires the coordination of various organ systems.
Negative feedback is the primary mechanism of homeostatic control.
Regulators use internal mechanisms to control internal change in the face of external fluctuation.
Conformers allow their internal condition to change with external variables.
Endothermy is more energetically expensive than ectothermy.
Metabolic rate can be measured by monitoring heat loss or oxygen consumption.
Energy budgets account for an animal's use of chemical energy for various functions.
Essential vitamins: B vitamins (coenzymes), Vitamin C (collagen), A (vision), D (calcium), E (antioxidant), K (clotting).
The pancreas is both an endocrine and exocrine gland.
The hepatic portal vein carries nutrient-rich blood from the digestive tract to the liver.
Gastrin, secretin, and CCK are hormones that regulate digestion.
Leptin is a hormone produced by fat tissue that suppresses appetite.
Countercurrent exchange in fish gills maintains a diffusion gradient for oxygen.
Partial pressure is the pressure exerted by a particular gas in a mixture.
Air is inhaled into the lungs through the branching system of bronchi and bronchioles.
Tidal volume is about 500 mL in an average resting human adult.
Residual volume is the air that remains in the lungs after a maximal exhalation.
Myoglobin is an oxygen-storing protein found in muscle cells.
The inflammatory response involves vasodilation and increased permeability of capillaries.
MHC class I molecules are found on almost all nucleated cells.
MHC class II molecules are found only on specialized antigen-presenting cells.
Cytotoxic T cells use perforins and granzymes to induce apoptosis in target cells.
Helper T cells secrete cytokines that activate both B and T cell responses.
Autoimmune disorders: Rheumatoid arthritis, Type 1 diabetes, Multiple Sclerosis.
Excretion in planaria is performed by protonephridia (flame cells).
Excretion in insects is performed by Malpighian tubules.
The nephron includes the glomerulus, proximal tubule, loop of Henle, and distal tubule.
The juxtaglomerular apparatus (JGA) helps regulate blood pressure and filtrate volume.
Aldosterone increases sodium and water reabsorption in the distal tubules.
Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) opposes the effects of aldosterone to lower blood pressure.
The pineal gland secretes melatonin, which regulates circadian rhythms.
The parathyroid glands regulate blood calcium levels via PTH.
The adrenal cortex produces glucocorticoids (cortisol) and mineralocorticoids (aldosterone).
Islets of Langerhans in the pancreas contain alpha cells (glucagon) and beta cells (insulin).
Eugenics is the discredited movement for improving the genetic quality of the human population.
Oogenesis involves long resting periods, whereas spermatogenesis is continuous.
The corpus luteum secretes progesterone to maintain the uterine lining.
HCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) prevents the degeneration of the corpus luteum during early pregnancy.
Menopause is the cessation of the menstrual cycle in females, typically in their late 40s or early 50s.
The resting potential is maintained by the sodium-potassium pump and leak channels.
Threshold potential is the minimum level of depolarization needed to trigger an action potential.
Voltage-gated sodium channels open rapidly during the depolarization phase.
The absolute refractory period prevents the backward travel of an action potential.
Acetylcholine is a common neurotransmitter at the neuromuscular junction.
GABA is the major inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain.
Glutamate is the major excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain.
The amygdala is involved in processing fear and emotional memories.
Lateralization refers to the specialization of the two hemispheres of the cerebral cortex.
Wernicke's area is involved in understanding language; Broca's area is for speech production.
Sensory adaptation is a decrease in responsiveness to continued stimulation.
The fovea is the center of the visual field, with a high density of cones.
The vestibular system in the inner ear is responsible for balance and spatial orientation.
The sarcoplasmic reticulum stores calcium ions in muscle fibers.
Fast-twitch fibers are adapted for rapid, powerful contractions but fatigue quickly.
Slow-twitch fibers are adapted for sustained contractions and have high myoglobin content.
Hydrostatic skeletons use fluid under pressure (found in cnidarians and annelids).
Exoskeletons are hard encasements on the surface of an animal (arthropods).
Endoskeletons are hard supporting elements buried within soft tissues (chordates).
Foraging behavior includes not only eating but also searching for and recognizing food.
Optimal foraging model suggests that animals maximize energy gain relative to cost.
Promiscuous mating involves no strong pair-bonds.
Monogamous mating involves one male and one female for a duration.
Polygamous mating involves an individual of one sex mating with several of the other.
Inclusive fitness accounts for both an individual's offspring and the offspring of relatives.
Ethology is the scientific study of animal behavior.
Fixed action patterns are highly stereotyped behaviors.
Learning is the modification of behavior based on specific experiences.
Cognition is the process of knowing that involves awareness, reasoning, and judgment.
Microevolution is a change in allele frequencies in a population over generations.
Genetic variation is the prerequisite for evolution by natural selection.
The main sources of genetic variation are mutation, chromosomal changes, and sexual reproduction.
A population is a group of individuals that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.
A gene pool consists of all the alleles for all loci in a population.
The Hardy-Weinberg equation: p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1.
Natural selection, genetic drift, and gene flow are the three main causes of evolutionary change.
Adaptive evolution results in a better match between organisms and their environment.
Directional selection favors individuals at one end of the phenotypic range.
Disruptive selection favors individuals at both extremes of the range.
Stabilizing selection favors intermediate variants and acts against extremes.
Intraspecific competition occurs between members of the same species.
Biological species concept: a species is a group of populations that interbreed and produce fertile offspring.
Reproductive isolation prevents different species from producing fertile hybrids.
Prezygotic barriers block fertilization from occurring.
Postzygotic barriers prevent a hybrid zygote from developing into a fertile adult.
Allopatric speciation occurs when a population is divided by a geographic barrier.
Sympatric speciation occurs in populations that live in the same geographic area.
Hybrid zones are regions where members of different species meet and mate.
Polyploidy, sexual selection, and habitat differentiation can lead to sympatric speciation.
Earth formed about 4.6 billion years ago.
The first life forms (prokaryotes) appeared about 3.5 billion years ago.
The oxygen revolution occurred about 2.7 to 2.4 billion years ago.
The first eukaryotes appeared about 1.8 billion years ago.
The Cambrian explosion (535-525 million years ago) saw a sudden appearance of many animal phyla.
Mass extinctions can alter the course of evolution and lead to adaptive radiations.
Systematics is the discipline focused on classifying organisms and determining their relationships.
Taxonomy levels: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species.
Clades are groups of species that include an ancestral species and all its descendants.
Monophyletic groups consist of an ancestor and all its descendants.
Paraphyletic groups consist of an ancestor and some, but not all, descendants.
Polyphyletic groups include distantly related species but not their most recent common ancestor.
Shared ancestral characters originated in an ancestor of the taxon.
Shared derived characters are unique to a particular clade.
Maximum parsimony assumes the tree that requires the fewest evolutionary events is most likely.
Orthologous genes are homologous genes that are found in different species due to speciation.
Paralogous genes result from gene duplication within a species.
Horizontal gene transfer complicates the reconstruction of the 'tree of life'.
Biology is a dynamic science that continuously incorporates new discoveries and technologies.
