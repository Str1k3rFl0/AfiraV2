The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living things.
The cell theory states that all living organisms are composed of cells.
Cells arise only from pre-existing cells through cell division.
Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles.
Eukaryotic cells have a defined nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.
The plasma membrane is a phospholipid bilayer that regulates what enters and exits the cell.
Cytoplasm is the jelly-like substance that fills the cell and surrounds the organelles.
The nucleus contains the cell's genetic material (DNA).
Nuclear pores allow the transport of molecules between the nucleus and cytoplasm.
The nucleolus is the site of ribosome synthesis inside the nucleus.
Mitochondria are the 'powerhouses' of the cell, generating ATP through cellular respiration.
Mitochondria have their own DNA, suggesting an endosymbiotic origin.
Ribosomes are the sites of protein synthesis.
The Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER) is studded with ribosomes and modifies proteins.
The Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER) is involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification.
The Golgi apparatus sorts, modifies, and packages proteins for secretion.
Lysosomes contain digestive enzymes to break down waste materials.
Peroxisomes break down fatty acids and detoxify harmful substances like hydrogen peroxide.
The cytoskeleton provides structural support and aids in cell movement.
Microtubules are composed of tubulin and form the spindle fibers during cell division.
Microfilaments are composed of actin and are involved in muscle contraction.
Intermediate filaments help maintain cell shape and anchor organelles.
Chloroplasts are found in plant cells and are the site of photosynthesis.
The central vacuole in plants stores water and maintains turgor pressure.
Cell walls in plants are made of cellulose, providing rigid support.
Passive transport moves substances across the membrane without energy expenditure.
Diffusion is the movement of particles from high to low concentration.
Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a semi-permeable membrane.
Facilitated diffusion uses carrier proteins to move large or polar molecules.
Active transport requires energy (ATP) to move substances against a concentration gradient.
The sodium-potassium pump is a vital active transport mechanism in nerve cells.
Endocytosis is the process of taking material into the cell by folding the membrane.
Exocytosis is the process of releasing materials from the cell via vesicles.
Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions.
Substrates bind to the active site of an enzyme to form an enzyme-substrate complex.
Enzymes follow the 'lock and key' or 'induced fit' models.
Metabolism is the sum of all chemical reactions in a living organism.
Anabolism is the set of metabolic pathways that construct molecules from smaller units.
Catabolism is the set of metabolic pathways that break down molecules into smaller units.
Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP) is the primary energy currency of the cell.
Cellular respiration converts biochemical energy from nutrients into ATP.
Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm and breaks glucose into pyruvate.
The Krebs cycle (Citric Acid Cycle) occurs in the mitochondrial matrix.
The electron transport chain produces the majority of ATP in aerobic respiration.
Anaerobic respiration occurs in the absence of oxygen.
Fermentation produces lactic acid or ethanol as a byproduct of anaerobic respiration.
Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.
The light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis occur in the thylakoid membranes.
The Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions) occurs in the stroma of chloroplasts.
DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) stores the genetic instructions for life.
DNA is composed of two strands forming a double helix.
Nucleotides are the building blocks of DNA: adenine, thymine, cytosine, and guanine.
Adenine pairs with thymine, and cytosine pairs with guanine in DNA.
RNA (Ribonucleic acid) is typically single-stranded and contains uracil instead of thymine.
The central dogma of molecular biology is: DNA -> RNA -> Protein.
Transcription is the process of copying DNA into messenger RNA (mRNA).
Translation is the process where ribosomes synthesize proteins using mRNA templates.
Transfer RNA (tRNA) brings amino acids to the ribosome during translation.
Codons are triplets of nucleotides on mRNA that code for specific amino acids.
The genetic code is universal and redundant.
Mutations are permanent changes in the DNA sequence.
Point mutations involve the change of a single nucleotide.
Frameshift mutations result from insertions or deletions of nucleotides.
Mitosis is the division of the nucleus resulting in two genetically identical daughter cells.
Prophase is the first stage of mitosis where chromosomes condense.
Metaphase is when chromosomes align at the cell's equator.
Anaphase is when sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles.
Telophase is when new nuclear envelopes form around the separated chromosomes.
Cytokinesis is the division of the cytoplasm following mitosis.
Meiosis is a specialized cell division that produces gametes (sperm and eggs).
Meiosis reduces the chromosome number by half (diploid to haploid).
Crossing over during prophase I of meiosis increases genetic diversity.
Genotype refers to the genetic makeup of an organism.
Phenotype refers to the observable physical characteristics of an organism.
Alleles are different versions of a gene.
Homozygous means having two identical alleles for a gene.
Heterozygous means having two different alleles for a gene.
Dominant alleles mask the expression of recessive alleles.
Mendel’s Law of Segregation states that alleles separate during gamete formation.
Mendel’s Law of Independent Assortment states that genes for different traits segregate independently.
Incomplete dominance results in a phenotype that is a blend of the two parents.
Codominance occurs when both alleles are expressed equally (e.g., AB blood type).
Polygenic traits are controlled by multiple genes (e.g., skin color, height).
Sex-linked traits are located on the X or Y chromosomes.
The Human Genome Project mapped all the genes in human DNA.
Evolution is the change in the heritable characteristics of biological populations over generations.
Natural selection is the process where organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive.
Charles Darwin is credited with the theory of evolution by natural selection.
Adaptation is a trait that enhances an organism's fitness in its environment.
Speciation is the formation of new and distinct species in the course of evolution.
Homologous structures share a common evolutionary origin (e.g., whale flipper and human arm).
Analogous structures have similar functions but different origins (e.g., bird wing and insect wing).
Vestigial structures are remnants of ancestral organs that no longer serve a function.
The fossil record provides evidence of past life forms and evolutionary transitions.
Binomial nomenclature is the two-part naming system for species (e.g., Homo sapiens).
Taxonomy is the science of naming and classifying organisms.
The three domains of life are Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya.
The six kingdoms of life are Archaebacteria, Eubacteria, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia.
Bacteria are unicellular prokaryotes with cell walls containing peptidoglycan.
Archaea are unicellular prokaryotes often found in extreme environments.
Protists are a diverse group of mostly unicellular eukaryotes.
Fungi are heterotrophic eukaryotes that absorb nutrients from decomposing organic matter.
Plants are autotrophic multicellular eukaryotes that perform photosynthesis.
Animals are heterotrophic multicellular eukaryotes that usually move and respond to stimuli.
Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment despite external changes.
Negative feedback loops counteract changes to maintain equilibrium (e.g., temperature regulation).
Positive feedback loops amplify a response (e.g., blood clotting or childbirth).
Tissues are groups of similar cells working together to perform a function.
The four types of animal tissues are epithelial, connective, muscle, and nervous.
Epithelial tissue covers body surfaces and lines cavities.
Connective tissue supports and binds other tissues (e.g., bone, blood, fat).
Muscle tissue is specialized for contraction and movement.
Nervous tissue transmits electrical signals throughout the body.
Organs are structures composed of multiple tissue types working together.
Organ systems are groups of organs that coordinate to perform major functions.
The circulatory system transports oxygen, nutrients, and waste products.
The respiratory system facilitates gas exchange (O2 in, CO2 out).
The digestive system breaks down food into absorbable nutrients.
The excretory system removes metabolic wastes from the body.
The nervous system controls body activities through electrical impulses.
The endocrine system regulates body functions through hormones.
The skeletal system provides support, protection, and a site for blood cell production.
The muscular system enables movement and maintains posture.
The immune system protects the body against pathogens.
The lymphatic system drains excess fluid and aids in immune response.
The reproductive system produces offspring.
Viruses are non-living infectious agents consisting of genetic material inside a protein coat.
Bacteriophages are viruses that infect bacteria.
The lytic cycle of a virus results in the destruction of the host cell.
The lysogenic cycle involves the viral genome integrating into the host DNA.
Vaccines stimulate the immune system to recognize and fight specific pathogens.
Antibiotics are drugs used to treat bacterial infections; they do not work on viruses.
Antigens are foreign substances that trigger an immune response.
Antibodies are proteins produced by B cells to neutralize antigens.
Pathogens are biological agents that cause disease.
Zoonotic diseases are those that jump from animals to humans.
Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their environment.
An ecosystem includes all living organisms and non-living factors in an area.
A population is a group of individuals of the same species in a specific area.
A community consists of all the different populations in an ecosystem.
The biosphere is the global sum of all ecosystems.
Abiotic factors are non-living components (e.g., sunlight, temperature, soil).
Biotic factors are living components (e.g., plants, animals, bacteria).
A niche is the role and position a species has in its environment.
Symbiosis is a close relationship between two different species.
Mutualism is a relationship where both species benefit.
Commensalism is where one species benefits and the other is unaffected.
Parasitism is where one species benefits at the expense of the other.
Producers (autotrophs) form the base of the food chain.
Consumers (heterotrophs) obtain energy by eating other organisms.
Decomposers break down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients.
Trophic levels represent the feeding positions in a food chain.
Only about 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next.
Biomagnification is the increasing concentration of toxic substances in higher trophic levels.
The nitrogen cycle involves nitrogen fixation, nitrification, and denitrification.
The carbon cycle moves carbon through the atmosphere, biosphere, and geosphere.
The water cycle includes evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and transpiration.
Biodiversity refers to the variety of life in an area.
Invasive species are non-native organisms that disrupt local ecosystems.
Carrying capacity is the maximum population size an environment can support.
Succession is the gradual process of change in an ecosystem over time.
Primary succession occurs in an area with no soil (e.g., after a volcanic eruption).
Secondary succession occurs after a disturbance that leaves soil intact (e.g., after a fire).
Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that can develop into specialized cell types.
Pluripotent stem cells can give rise to almost any cell type in the body.
Apoptosis is essential for normal development, such as the removal of webbing between fingers.
Pheromones are chemical signals used for communication within a species.
The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium describes a non-evolving population.
Gene flow is the transfer of alleles between populations.
Genetic drift is a change in allele frequency due to chance events.
The bottleneck effect occurs when a population size is drastically reduced.
The founder effect occurs when a small group starts a new population.
Polyploidy is the condition of having more than two complete sets of chromosomes.
Telomeres are protective caps at the ends of chromosomes.
Histones are proteins that help package DNA into structural units called nucleosomes.
Plasmids are small, circular DNA molecules found in bacteria.
Gel electrophoresis is a technique used to separate DNA fragments by size.
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) is used to amplify small segments of DNA.
Cloning produces genetically identical copies of a biological entity.
CRISPR-Cas9 is a revolutionary gene-editing technology.
Transgenic organisms contain DNA from a different species.
Metamorphosis is a biological process involving a conspicuous change in body structure (e.g., tadpole to frog).
Ectotherms rely on external heat sources to regulate body temperature.
Endotherms generate internal heat to maintain a constant body temperature.
Xylem transports water and minerals from roots to leaves in plants.
Phloem transports sugars from leaves to the rest of the plant.
Stomata are small openings on leaves that allow for gas exchange.
Transpiration is the loss of water vapor from plant leaves.
Phototropism is the growth of a plant toward a light source.
Geotropism (gravitropism) is a plant's growth response to gravity.
Germination is the process by which a seed develops into a new plant.
Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the male to female reproductive organs in plants.
Double fertilization is a complex fertilization process unique to flowering plants.
Hermaphrodites are organisms that possess both male and female reproductive organs.
Parthenogenesis is a form of asexual reproduction where embryos develop without fertilization.
Gastrulation is a phase in early embryonic development where the blastula reorganizes into three layers.
The ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm are the three primary germ layers.
Organogenesis is the process of organ formation during embryonic development.
The placenta allows for nutrient and waste exchange between mother and fetus.
Amphibians are vertebrates that typically spend part of their life in water and part on land.
Reptiles are vertebrates with scaly skin that usually lay leathery eggs.
Mammals are characterized by the presence of mammary glands and hair or fur.
Primate characteristics include opposable thumbs and large brains.
Homo sapiens evolved in Africa approximately 300,000 years ago.
Altruism in biology is behavior that increases the fitness of another at a cost to oneself.
Circadian rhythms are physical, mental, and behavioral changes that follow a 24-hour cycle.
Bioluminescence is the production of light by a living organism.
Hemoglobin is a protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen.
Chlorophyll is the primary pigment used in photosynthesis.
The myelin sheath insulates nerve fibers and increases the speed of electrical impulses.
Synapses are the junctions between nerve cells where communication occurs.
The blood-brain barrier protects the brain from harmful substances in the blood.
Prions are misfolded proteins that can cause infectious diseases.
Viroids are infectious agents composed solely of short strands of circular RNA.
Antigenic drift is a small genetic change in a virus over time.
Antigenic shift is a major, abrupt change in a virus, often leading to pandemics.
The lymphatic system includes the spleen, thymus, and lymph nodes.
B cells produce antibodies as part of the humoral immune response.
T cells are involved in the cell-mediated immune response.
Phagocytosis is the process by which certain cells engulf and digest large particles.
Interferons are proteins produced by cells in response to viral infections.
Complement system is a group of proteins that enhance the ability of antibodies to clear pathogens.
Active immunity results from exposure to a pathogen or vaccine.
Passive immunity is the short-term immunity resulting from the introduction of antibodies from another person.
Autoimmune diseases occur when the immune system mistakenly attacks body tissues.
Allergies are hypersensitive responses to harmless environmental substances.
Histamine is a chemical released during allergic reactions that causes inflammation.
Anaphylactic shock is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction.
Blood types are determined by the presence or absence of specific antigens on red blood cells.
Type O negative blood is known as the universal donor.
Type AB positive blood is known as the universal recipient.
The Rh factor is another antigen found on red blood cells.
Hematopoiesis is the process of blood cell formation in the bone marrow.
Plasma makes up about 55% of total blood volume.
Platelets (thrombocytes) are essential for blood clotting.
Fibrinogen is a plasma protein that is converted to fibrin during clotting.
Leukocytes (white blood cells) are the primary cells of the immune system.
Neutrophils are the most abundant type of white blood cell.
Monocytes can differentiate into macrophages in tissues.
Eosinophils are involved in fighting parasitic infections.
Basophils release histamine and heparin.
The aorta is the largest artery in the human body.
Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels where gas exchange occurs.
Veins contain valves to prevent the backflow of blood.
The sinoatrial (SA) node is the natural pacemaker of the heart.
Systole is the phase of the heartbeat when the heart muscle contracts.
Diastole is the phase when the heart muscle relaxes and fills with blood.
Blood pressure is measured as systolic over diastolic pressure.
The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs.
The pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood back to the heart.
Alveoli are tiny air sacs in the lungs where oxygen and carbon dioxide are exchanged.
The diaphragm contracts to expand the chest cavity, pulling air into the lungs.
Vital capacity is the maximum amount of air a person can expel from the lungs.
Tidal volume is the amount of air inhaled or exhaled during a normal breath.
The epiglottis prevents food from entering the trachea during swallowing.
Peristalsis is the wave-like muscle contractions that move food through the digestive tract.
The stomach secretes hydrochloric acid and pepsin to digest proteins.
Villi and microvilli in the small intestine increase surface area for absorption.
The liver produces bile, which emulsifies fats.
The gallbladder stores and concentrates bile.
The pancreas secretes digestive enzymes and insulin.
The large intestine absorbs water and forms feces.
The kidneys filter blood to remove waste and regulate water balance.
Nephrons are the functional units of the kidney.
Glomerular filtration is the first step in urine formation.
Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) regulates the amount of water reabsorbed by the kidneys.
The ureters carry urine from the kidneys to the bladder.
The urethra is the tube through which urine exits the body.
Pituitary gland is the 'master gland' that controls other endocrine glands.
The hypothalamus links the nervous system to the endocrine system.
Thyroid hormones regulate the body's metabolic rate.
Insulin lowers blood glucose, while glucagon raises it.
Adrenaline (epinephrine) triggers the fight-or-flight response.
Cortisol is a stress hormone that helps regulate metabolism and inflammation.
Estrogen and testosterone are primary sex hormones.
Melatonin regulates sleep-wake cycles.
Dopamine is a neurotransmitter involved in reward and motivation.
Serotonin helps regulate mood and social behavior.
The central nervous system consists of the brain and spinal cord.
The peripheral nervous system includes all nerves outside the CNS.
Sensory neurons carry signals from receptors to the CNS.
Motor neurons carry signals from the CNS to muscles or glands.
The cerebrum is the largest part of the brain, responsible for higher functions.
The cerebellum coordinates movement and balance.
The brainstem controls basic life functions like breathing and heart rate.
The corpus callosum connects the left and right hemispheres of the brain.
Action potentials are the electrical signals that travel along axons.
The refractory period is the time after a nerve impulse when another cannot be triggered.
Reflexes are rapid, involuntary responses to stimuli.
The somatic nervous system controls voluntary movements.
The autonomic nervous system regulates involuntary functions.
The sympathetic nervous system prepares the body for stress.
The parasympathetic nervous system promotes 'rest and digest' activities.
Photoreceptors in the eye include rods (light sensitivity) and cones (color vision).
The cochlea in the inner ear converts sound waves into nerve impulses.
Olfactory receptors detect smell, and gustatory receptors detect taste.
Proprioception is the sense of the relative position of one's own body parts.
Nociceptors are sensory receptors that respond to painful stimuli.
Cartilage is a flexible connective tissue found in joints and ears.
Ligaments connect bone to bone.
Tendons connect muscle to bone.
Red bone marrow produces blood cells, while yellow marrow stores fat.
Osteoblasts build new bone tissue.
Osteoclasts break down bone tissue for remodeling.
The axial skeleton includes the skull, vertebral column, and rib cage.
The appendicular skeleton includes the limbs and girdles.
Antagonistic muscle pairs work in opposition (e.g., biceps and triceps).
The sliding filament theory explains how muscles contract.
Creatine phosphate provides a quick source of energy for muscle contraction.
Lactic acid buildup can lead to muscle fatigue during intense exercise.
The dermis is the thick layer of skin below the epidermis.
Melanocytes produce melanin, which protects the skin from UV radiation.
Sebaceous glands produce oil (sebum) to lubricate the skin.
Sweat glands help regulate body temperature through evaporation.
Keratin is a tough protein found in skin, hair, and nails.
Meiosis occurs in the gonads (testes and ovaries).
Fertilization usually occurs in the fallopian tubes.
A zygote is a single cell formed by the fusion of sperm and egg.
The blastocyst is an early-stage embryo that implants in the uterus.
Differentiation is the process by which cells become specialized.
Hox genes control the body plan and organ development in embryos.
Aging is a complex process involving cellular damage and telomere shortening.
Telomerase is an enzyme that can lengthen telomeres, often active in cancer cells.
Cancer is characterized by uncontrolled cell growth and division.
Oncogenes are genes that have the potential to cause cancer when mutated.
Tumor suppressor genes normally inhibit cell division.
P53 is a crucial protein that monitors DNA damage and can trigger apoptosis.
Genomics is the study of the entire genome of an organism.
Proteomics is the study of the entire set of proteins produced by an organism.
Bioinformatics uses computer science to analyze biological data.
Synthetic biology involves redesigning organisms for useful purposes.
Ecosystem services are benefits humans receive from nature (e.g., pollination).
Sustainable development aims to meet current needs without compromising future generations.
Global warming is primarily caused by an increase in greenhouse gases like CO2.
Ocean acidification is caused by excess CO2 dissolving in seawater.
Eutrophication is an overgrowth of algae due to excess nutrients in water.
Conservation biology focuses on protecting endangered species and habitats.
Endemic species are found only in one specific geographic area.
The Gaia hypothesis suggests that Earth and its biological systems behave as a single entity.
Microevolution refers to small-scale changes in allele frequencies within a population.
Macroevolution refers to large-scale evolutionary patterns and processes.
Adaptive radiation is the rapid evolution of many species from a common ancestor.
Coevolution occurs when two species influence each other's evolution.
Sexual selection is a mode of natural selection where individuals choose mates.
Punctuated equilibrium suggests evolution occurs in rapid bursts followed by stability.
Phylogenetic trees represent the evolutionary relationships between organisms.
Cladistics is a method of classification based on shared derived characteristics.
The RNA world hypothesis suggests that RNA preceded DNA as the genetic material.
The Miller-Urey experiment demonstrated that organic molecules could form from inorganic precursors.
Biological molecules follow the laws of physics and chemistry.
Structure and function are fundamentally linked at every level of biological organization.
