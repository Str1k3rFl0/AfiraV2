Analgesics are medications used specifically to relieve pain.
NSAIDs (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs) reduce pain, fever, and inflammation.
Acetaminophen (Paracetamol) is an analgesic and antipyretic but has weak anti-
inflammatory effects.
Opioids act on the central nervous system to relieve severe pain but carry a high
risk of addiction.
Antibiotics are used to treat bacterial infections and are ineffective against
viruses.
Penicillin was the first true antibiotic discovered and inhibits bacterial cell wall
synthesis.
Broad-spectrum antibiotics are effective against a wide range of both Gram-positive
and Gram-negative bacteria.
Antivirals inhibit the development of viral pathogens inside the host cell.
Antifungals are used to treat fungal infections like candidiasis or athlete's foot.
Statins are used to lower cholesterol by inhibiting the enzyme HMG-CoA reductase.
Beta-blockers reduce blood pressure by blocking the effects of epinephrine
(adrenaline).
ACE inhibitors relax blood vessels by preventing the formation of Angiotensin II.
Diuretics, often called water pills, help the body rid itself of excess salt and
water.
Anticoagulants, such as Warfarin or Heparin, prevent the formation of blood clots.
Antiplatelet drugs, like Aspirin, prevent platelets from sticking together to form a
clot.
Insulin is used to manage blood glucose levels in patients with Diabetes Mellitus.
Metformin is the first-line medication for the treatment of Type 2 Diabetes.
Corticosteroids are powerful anti-inflammatory drugs used in conditions like asthma
and arthritis.
Bronchodilators open the airways to treat conditions such as asthma and COPD.
Antihistamines block histamine receptors to treat allergic reactions.
Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) reduce the production of stomach acid to treat GERD.
Antacids neutralize existing stomach acid to provide quick relief from heartburn.
Benzodiazepines are used to treat anxiety and insomnia by enhancing the effect of
GABA.
Antidepressants, such as SSRIs, increase levels of serotonin in the brain.
Antipsychotics are used primarily to manage psychosis, including schizophrenia.
Anticonvulsants are medications used to prevent or reduce the severity of seizures.
Local anesthetics block nerve conduction in a specific area to prevent pain during
procedures.
General anesthetics induce a reversible loss of consciousness for major surgery.
Vaccines stimulate the immune system to produce antibodies without causing the
disease.
Epinephrine is the first-line treatment for severe allergic reactions known as
anaphylaxis.
Nitroglycerin is a vasodilator used to treat angina (chest pain).
Digoxin is used to improve the strength and efficiency of the heart in heart failure.
Laxatives are used to treat constipation by promoting bowel movements.
Antiemetics are medications used to prevent or treat nausea and vomiting.
Thyroxine is a synthetic hormone used to treat hypothyroidism.
Chemotherapy drugs target and kill rapidly dividing cells, primarily to treat cancer.
Immunosuppressants are used to prevent the body from rejecting a transplanted organ.
Diuretics can often cause a loss of potassium, requiring monitoring of electrolyte
levels.
Overuse of antibiotics leads to antibiotic resistance, making infections harder to
treat.
Sublingual administration involves placing a drug under the tongue for rapid
absorption.
Intravenous (IV) administration delivers medication directly into the bloodstream for
immediate effect.
The half-life of a drug is the time it takes for its concentration in the body to
reduce by half.
Pharmacokinetics describes how the body affects a drug (absorption, distribution,
metabolism, excretion).
Pharmacodynamics describes how a drug affects the body (mechanism of action).
Tolerance occurs when the body becomes less responsive to a drug after repeated use.
Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction to a drug or
substance.
Toxicity refers to the degree to which a substance can damage an organism.
A contraindication is a specific situation in which a drug should not be used because
it may be harmful.
The placebo effect is a beneficial effect produced by a drug that cannot be
attributed to the drug itself.
Synergy occurs when two drugs taken together produce a greater effect than the sum of
their individual effects.
Calcium channel blockers are used to treat high blood pressure and chest pain.
Warfarin requires regular blood tests (INR) to ensure the dose is safe and effective.
Morphine is a potent opioid analgesic used for severe acute or chronic pain.
Naloxone is an emergency medication used to reverse opioid overdose.
Albuterol is a short-acting bronchodilator used for quick relief of asthma symptoms.
Amiodarone is an antiarrhythmic medication used for serious heart rhythm problems.
Lidocaine can be used both as a local anesthetic and an antiarrhythmic.
Atropine is used to treat a dangerously slow heart rate (bradycardia).
Furosemide is a potent loop diuretic used for edema and heart failure.
Spironolactone is a potassium-sparing diuretic.
Omeprazole is a common PPI used for stomach ulcers and acid reflux.
Ranitidine is an H2-receptor antagonist that reduces stomach acid.
Azithromycin is a macrolide antibiotic often used for respiratory infections.
Amoxicillin is a common penicillin-type antibiotic used for various bacterial
infections.
Ciprofloxacin is a fluoroquinolone antibiotic used for urinary tract infections.
Doxycycline is a tetracycline antibiotic used for acne and Lyme disease.
Acyclovir is an antiviral drug used specifically for herpes virus infections.
Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) is an antiviral used to treat influenza.
Fluoxetine (Prozac) is a common SSRI used for depression and OCD.
Diazepam (Valium) is a benzodiazepine used for anxiety, muscle spasms, and seizures.
Lithium is a mood stabilizer used primarily for bipolar disorder.
Haloperidol is a traditional (typical) antipsychotic medication.
Phenytoin is a long-standing anticonvulsant used for epilepsy.
Prednisone is an oral corticosteroid used for severe inflammation.
Hydrocortisone is a topical steroid used for skin rashes and inflammation.
Lisinopril is an ACE inhibitor commonly used for hypertension.
Losartan is an Angiotensin II Receptor Blocker (ARB) used for high blood pressure.
Amlodipine is a long-acting calcium channel blocker.
Simvastatin is a medication used to lower 'bad' cholesterol (LDL).
Levothyroxine must usually be taken on an empty stomach for proper absorption.
Glucagon is used to treat severe hypoglycemia in emergency situations.
Methotrexate is used both for cancer and for autoimmune diseases like rheumatoid
arthritis.
Cyclosporine is an immunosuppressant used in organ transplants.
Rifampin is a key antibiotic used in the treatment of tuberculosis.
Vancomycin is a powerful antibiotic used for serious infections like MRSA.
Heparin is an anticoagulant administered via injection.
The liver is the primary site for drug metabolism in the body.
The kidneys are the primary site for drug excretion.
First-pass metabolism reduces the concentration of a drug before it reaches systemic
circulation.
Bioavailability is the fraction of an administered dose of drug that reaches the
systemic circulation.
Teratogenic drugs are those that can cause birth defects if taken during pregnancy.
Drug-drug interactions occur when one drug changes the effect of another drug.
A side effect is an unintended but predictable effect of a medication.
An adverse drug reaction (ADR) is an unwanted and harmful response to a medication.
The therapeutic index is the ratio between the toxic dose and the effective dose.
Narrow therapeutic index drugs require precise dosing and blood monitoring (e.g.,
Digoxin, Lithium).
Vitamins are organic compounds required in small amounts for normal metabolism.
Folic acid is essential for DNA synthesis and is vital during early pregnancy.
Vitamin K is essential for blood clotting and is the antidote for Warfarin.
Iron supplements are used to treat iron-deficiency anemia.
